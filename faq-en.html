Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

This page is for first-time readers: what is Hierarchical Calculus, what does it add, how is it used, and what are its limitations?

The idea in two lines

Hierarchical Calculus organizes “languages of change”: addition (level 0) → division (level 1) → log (level 2) → tetration (level 3) → … so you can choose the level where the law becomes simplest.

\[ D_0 f=\frac{df}{dx},\qquad D_1 f=\frac{d\ln f}{d\ln x},\qquad D_2 f=\frac{d\ln(\ln f)}{d\ln(\ln x)} \]
Rule of thumb
Power-law behavior → level 1 (\(D_1\)) is often the natural coordinate system. Multi-scale behavior like \(\exp((\ln x)^k)\) → level 2 (\(D_2\)) often yields a much more stable representation.

Q & A

1) Is this a replacement for classical calculus?
No. It is a general framework that includes classical differentiation (level 0) as a special case, and adds higher levels that are sometimes more natural for ratio/log/log-log behaviors.
2) What is the difference between \(D_0\) and \(D_1\)?
\(D_0\) measures additive change. \(D_1\) measures relative (scale-invariant) change:
\[ D_1 f=\frac{d\ln f}{d\ln x}=\frac{x}{f}\frac{df}{dx} \]
For \(f(x)=x^k\), we get \(D_1 f=k\) constant, i.e., power laws become linear in log coordinates.
3) Why can lifting improve numerical approximations?
Because you approximate in a coordinate system where the function is closer to linear and often better conditioned. Instead of approximating \(f\) directly, approximate \(\ln f\) or \(\ln(\ln f)\), then transform back.
  • See: Numerics.
4) Are there real physics examples?
Yes. Many physical laws are naturally relative. For an ideal gas in an adiabatic process: \(P V^\gamma=\text{const}\), hence:
\[ \frac{d\ln P}{d\ln V}=-\gamma \]
which is exactly a level-1 equation.
5) What domain assumptions are needed for \(D_2\) and higher?
For \(\ln(\ln x)\) you need \(\ln x>0\) i.e., \(x>1\). For \(\ln(\ln f)\) you typically need \(f>1\) and \(\ln f>0\).
