Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

This page answers common questions about Hierarchical Calculus.
Important: in this website, every derivative is written with both its rank and degree: \(\;D_r^n\).

The core idea in two lines

Hierarchical Calculus organizes different “languages of change”: additive (differential) → relative → logarithmic → higher levels, by choosing the rank that best matches the phenomenon.

\[ D_0^{1} f=\frac{df}{dx}, \qquad D_1^{1} f=\frac{d\ln f}{d\ln x}, \qquad D_2^{1} f=\frac{d\ln(\ln f)}{d\ln(\ln x)}. \]

Questions & Answers

  1. Is Hierarchical Calculus a replacement for classical calculus?
    No. Classical calculus (rank \(D_0\)) is a special case. Hierarchical Calculus extends it by adding higher ranks that are better suited to relative or logarithmic scaling.
  2. What is the intuitive difference between \(D_0^{1}\) and \(D_1^{1}\)?
    \(D_0^{1}\) measures additive change, while \(D_1^{1}\) measures relative change:
    \[ D_1^{1} f =\frac{d\ln f}{d\ln x} =\frac{x}{f}\frac{df}{dx} =\frac{x}{f}\,D_0^{1}f. \]
  3. Are hierarchical derivatives truly new derivatives?
    Formally, many hierarchical derivatives can be interpreted as classical derivatives after a suitable change of variables (e.g. \(u=\ln x\), \(v=\ln(\ln x)\)). The novelty lies in organizing these transformations into a unified, rank–degree framework.
  4. What is the rigorous definition of higher ranks?
    The rank-\(n\), degree-1 derivative is defined as:
    \[ D_n^{1} f(x)=\frac{d\,\ln^{(n)}(f(x))}{d\,\ln^{(n)}(x)}. \]
  5. Why can higher ranks improve numerical approximation?
    Because approximation is performed in a space where the function is closer to linear (e.g. approximating \(\ln f\) or \(\ln(\ln f)\)), which often reduces error amplification for large or rapidly growing values.
  6. Are there real physical examples?
    Yes. Many physical laws are naturally relative. For instance, the adiabatic law \(PV^\gamma=\text{const}\) gives:
    \[ \frac{d\ln P}{d\ln V}=-\gamma \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad D_1^{1}P(V)=-\gamma. \]
  7. What are the domain constraints for higher ranks?
    Rank \(D_1\) requires \(x>0\) and \(f>0\). Rank \(D_2\) additionally requires \(x>1\) and \(f>1\) so that \(\ln(\ln x)\) and \(\ln(\ln f)\) are defined.
Notation reminder: If you encounter older texts using \(D_r\) without a degree, it should always be read here as \(D_r^{1}\).
