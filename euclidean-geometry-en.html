Hierarchical Calculus in Euclidean Geometry

Euclidean geometry is naturally scale-based: lengths, areas, volumes, similarity, curvature, and mesh quality often matter relatively, not absolutely. This makes hierarchical calculus a natural tool for geometric analysis.
This page provides: (i) conceptual application framework, (ii) 7 real numeric examples, and (iii) a line-by-line comparison between \(D_0^1\) and \(D_1^1\).

Notation rule: On this website every derivative is written as \(D_r^n\) (explicit rank \(r\) and degree \(n\)). The classical differential derivative is always \(D_0^1\). The basic relative derivative is \(D_1^1\).

Practical idea: when geometry is dominated by similarity / scaling, \(D_1^1\) is often the stable descriptor.

A) Why Euclidean geometry is “relative” by nature

Many geometric statements remain true when we scale an object by a factor \(s\): if a triangle is similar to another, the ratio of corresponding sides is constant; areas scale as \(s^2\); volumes scale as \(s^3\). These are power laws.

\[ L \propto s^1,\qquad A \propto s^2,\qquad V \propto s^3. \]

The classical derivative \(D_0^1\) measures absolute change, so its value depends on units and scale. The relative derivative \(D_1^1\) measures structural change, so it is suited to similarity and scaling laws.

B) Minimal operational formulas used in this page

For a scaling parameter \(s>0\), we will compare:

Operator Meaning Typical behavior under scaling
\(D_0^1 y(s)=\dfrac{dy}{ds}\) Absolute change per unit of \(s\) Depends on units and current size
\(D_1^1 y(s)=\dfrac{d\ln y}{d\ln s}\) Relative structural change (dimension-like exponent) Stable for power laws \(y\sim s^a\Rightarrow D_1^1y=a\)
Key rule used repeatedly: If \(y(s)=C\,s^a\) with \(C>0\), then \(D_1^1 y(s)=a\) (constant).

Example 1) Similarity scaling of length (engineering drawing)

A CAD drawing is scaled by factor \(s\). A length is \(L(s)=10s\) (in cm). Compare what \(D_0^1\) and \(D_1^1\) report at \(s=2\).

\[ L(s)=10s,\quad L(2)=20. \]
Step \(D_0^1\) \(D_1^1\)
Compute \(D_0^1 L = \dfrac{d}{ds}(10s)=10\) \(D_1^1 L=\dfrac{d\ln(10s)}{d\ln s}=1\)
Interpretation “+10 cm per +1 scale unit” “Length scales like \(s^1\)” (dimension 1)
Geometric meaning: \(D_1^1\) returns the similarity exponent (1), independent of the unit “cm”.

Example 2) Area scaling (tiling & floor plan)

A square floor plan is scaled by \(s\). If side length is \(L(s)=3s\) meters, then area is \(A(s)=L(s)^2 = 9s^2\). Evaluate at \(s=2\).

\[ A(s)=9s^2,\quad A(2)=36. \]
Step \(D_0^1\) \(D_1^1\)
Compute \(D_0^1 A = \dfrac{d}{ds}(9s^2)=18s \Rightarrow D_0^1A(2)=36\) \(D_1^1 A=\dfrac{d\ln(9s^2)}{d\ln s}=2\)
Interpretation Absolute sensitivity grows with \(s\) Area scales like \(s^2\) (dimension 2)

Example 3) Volume scaling (3D model / printing)

A 3D object is scaled by \(s\). Suppose \(V(s)=2s^3\) (in cm\(^3\)). Evaluate the two derivatives at \(s=1.5\).

\[ V(s)=2s^3,\quad V(1.5)=2(1.5)^3=6.75. \]
Step \(D_0^1\) \(D_1^1\)
Compute \(D_0^1 V=\dfrac{d}{ds}(2s^3)=6s^2 \Rightarrow D_0^1V(1.5)=13.5\) \(D_1^1 V=\dfrac{d\ln(2s^3)}{d\ln s}=3\)
Interpretation Absolute change depends on current scale \(s\) Volume scales like \(s^3\) (dimension 3)
Important: The number “3” is a structural invariant: it is the Euclidean dimension of volume scaling.

Example 4) Similar triangles: invariance of side ratios

Two similar triangles have scale factor \(s\). Let a side be \(a(s)=5s\) cm. The ratio \(a(s)/s = 5\) is constant. Compare what derivatives “see”.

\[ a(s)=5s,\quad \frac{a(s)}{s}=5. \]
Line-by-line \(D_0^1\) \(D_1^1\)
Derivative of the ratio \(D_0^1\!\left(\frac{a}{s}\right)=D_0^1(5)=0\) (works but depends on building ratio first) \(D_1^1 a = 1\) immediately identifies similarity class
Geometric reading Need explicit normalization Normalization is built-in
Practical geometry: Similarity is naturally expressed by exponents. \(D_1^1\) detects them directly.

Example 5) Curvature scale: circle radius under scaling

A circle is scaled by \(s\). Its radius becomes \(R(s)=R_0 s\). Curvature is \(k(s)=1/R(s) = \frac{1}{R_0}s^{-1}\). Take \(R_0=2\) and compute at \(s=3\).

\[ R(s)=2s,\quad k(s)=\frac{1}{2}s^{-1}. \]
Step \(D_0^1\) \(D_1^1\)
Compute \(k(3)\) \(k(3)=\frac{1}{6}\approx0.1667\) —
Derivative \(D_0^1 k=\dfrac{d}{ds}\!\left(\frac{1}{2}s^{-1}\right)=-\frac{1}{2}s^{-2}\Rightarrow D_0^1k(3)=-\frac{1}{18}\) \(D_1^1 k=\dfrac{d\ln(\frac{1}{2}s^{-1})}{d\ln s}=-1\)
Meaning Absolute sensitivity changes with \(s\) Curvature scales like \(s^{-1}\) (structural exponent)

Example 6) Mesh refinement: step size and error scaling (FEM / numerical geometry)

In numerical geometry, if a mesh step is \(h\) and an error behaves like \(E(h)=C h^p\), the exponent \(p\) is the convergence order (a real engineering quantity). Let \(E(h)=4h^2\) and compare at \(h=0.2\).

\[ E(h)=4h^2,\quad E(0.2)=4(0.04)=0.16. \]
Line-by-line \(D_0^1\) \(D_1^1\)
Compute derivative \(D_0^1E=\frac{d}{dh}(4h^2)=8h \Rightarrow D_0^1E(0.2)=1.6\) \(D_1^1E=\frac{d\ln(4h^2)}{d\ln h}=2\)
Engineering meaning Absolute sensitivity depends on \(h\) Convergence order \(p=2\) (stable invariant)
Real-world: This is exactly how engineers judge numerical schemes: by the exponent/order, not by absolute slope.

Example 7) Shape measure under scaling: perimeter vs area (isoperimetric-like descriptors)

Many geometric descriptors compare perimeter \(P\) and area \(A\). Under scaling \(s\): \(P\sim s^1\) and \(A\sim s^2\). Let \(P(s)=12s\), \(A(s)=9s^2\). Compute invariants at \(s=2\).

\[ P(2)=24,\quad A(2)=36. \]
Quantity \(D_0^1\) \(D_1^1\)
Perimeter \(D_0^1P=\frac{d}{ds}(12s)=12\) \(D_1^1P=\frac{d\ln(12s)}{d\ln s}=1\)
Area \(D_0^1A=\frac{d}{ds}(9s^2)=18s\Rightarrow D_0^1A(2)=36\) \(D_1^1A=\frac{d\ln(9s^2)}{d\ln s}=2\)
Structural insight Numbers depend on units and scale Exponents \((1,2)\) encode geometry directly
Takeaway: Many Euclidean descriptors become “clean” when expressed through scaling exponents. \(D_1^1\) extracts these exponents directly.

C) Summary: what this page proves (in practice)

  • Euclidean similarity is exponent-based; \(D_1^1\) detects similarity classes immediately.
  • Area and volume scaling exponents (2 and 3) appear naturally as \(D_1^1\) invariants.
  • Curvature scaling is captured by a negative exponent (Example 5).
  • Numerical geometry quality/order is fundamentally an exponent (Example 6).
  • \(D_0^1\) remains valuable for absolute sensitivity, but it is not scale-invariant.
One-sentence conclusion: In Euclidean geometry, whenever the phenomenon is dominated by scaling/similarity, \(D_1^1\) behaves like a “geometric invariant extractor”, while \(D_0^1\) behaves like a unit-dependent slope.
