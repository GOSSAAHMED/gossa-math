Series: Differential, Relative, and Logarithmic

This page unifies three approximation families around a reference point \(x_0\): (1) the classical differential Taylor series (rank \(D_0\)), (2) a relative multiplicative (“product”) Taylor series (rank \(D_1\)), (3) a rank-2 logarithmic exponential series (rank \(D_2\)). We always write derivatives with both rank and degree.

D₀: differential D₁: relative (product) D₂: log-rank (exponential) Function approximation

Core Concepts

Expansion point

Choose \(x_0\) inside the domain. Approximation quality depends on the notion of “closeness”:
— For \(D_0\): additive closeness via \(x-x_0\).
— For \(D_1\): relative closeness via \(x/x_0\).
— For \(D_2\): logarithmic closeness via \(\ln x/\ln x_0\) (equivalently \(\ln(\ln x)\)).

Domain note: The \(D_2\) series typically requires \(x>1\) and \(x_0>1\) so that \(\ln(\ln x)\) is defined. It also uses \(\ln(\ln f)\), hence we usually consider ranges where \(f(x)>1\).

1) Differential series — rank \(D_0\)

The classical Taylor series built from the standard differential operator. We write rank+degree as \(D_0^n\).

Formula (Taylor)
\[ f(x)=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{(D_{0}^{n}f)(x_0)}{n!}\,(x-x_0)^{n}. \]

Here \(D_{0}^{0}f=f\), \(D_{0}^{1}f=df/dx\), \(D_{0}^{2}f=d^2f/dx^2\), etc.

When is it strongest?
  • When \(x\) is very close to \(x_0\) in the additive sense (\(x-x_0\)).
  • Excellent local approximations for analytic functions (sin, cos, exp, …).
  • Limited by nearby poles/singularities.

2) Relative multiplicative series — rank \(D_1\)

A “product Taylor”: sum → product and minus → division. Built from successive relative derivatives of degree \(n\) at rank 1: \(D_1^n\).

Definitions
\[ t=\frac{x}{x_0},\qquad \Delta=\ln\!\left(\frac{x}{x_0}\right)=\ln t. \]
Formula (agreed “Form 2”)
\[ \frac{f(x)}{f(x_0)} = \prod_{n=1}^{\infty} \left(\frac{x}{x_0}\right)^{ \frac{\left(D_{1}^{n}\ln f\right)(x_0)}{n!}\;\Delta^{\,n-1} }. \]

where \[ \left(D_{1}^{n}\ln f\right)(x_0) = \left.\left(\frac{d}{d\ln x}\right)^{n}\ln f(x)\right|_{x=x_0}. \]

Why multiplicative?

We expand \(\ln f\) with respect to \(\ln x\) (additive), then map back to \(f\) (multiplicative). A negative exponent means division: \[ \left(\frac{x}{x_0}\right)^{-a}=\frac{1}{\left(\frac{x}{x_0}\right)^{a}}. \]

3) Rank-2 logarithmic exponential series — rank \(D_2\) (Option A)

In rank \(D_2\), the effective variable is \(\ln(\ln x)\). We adopt Option A: approximating \(\ln f(x)/\ln f(x_0)\).

Definitions
\[ \Delta_2=\ln(\ln x)-\ln(\ln x_0)=\ln\!\left(\frac{\ln x}{\ln x_0}\right), \qquad t_2=\frac{\ln x}{\ln x_0}. \]
Formula (Option A)
\[ \frac{\ln f(x)}{\ln f(x_0)} = \prod_{n=1}^{\infty} \left(\frac{\ln x}{\ln x_0}\right)^{ \frac{\left(D_{2}^{n}\ln^{(2)} f\right)(x_0)}{n!}\;\Delta_2^{\,n-1} }. \]

where \(\ln^{(2)}f=\ln(\ln f)\), and \[ \left(D_{2}^{n}\ln^{(2)} f\right)(x_0) = \left.\left(\frac{d}{d\,\ln(\ln x)}\right)^{n}\ln(\ln f(x))\right|_{x=x_0}. \]

Interpretation

Rank \(D_2\) measures closeness through \(\ln(\ln x)\), hence \(\ln x/\ln x_0\) appears naturally. Negative exponents again correspond to division.

How to use these series for approximation

Practical recipe
  • Choose \(x_0\) inside your region of interest.
  • Select the closeness scale: additive (\(D_0\)), relative (\(D_1\)), or log-scale (\(D_2\)).
  • Pick a truncation degree \(N\) (e.g., 3 or 4) and use the truncated formula.
  • Reminder: \(D_0^n\) are ordinary derivatives, while \(D_1^n\) and \(D_2^n\) here are successive derivatives of \(\ln f\) and \(\ln(\ln f)\) as defined above.
Quick conclusion rule:
— Power-law behavior (\(x^p\), \(\sqrt{x}\)) → \(D_1\) is typically strongest.
— Oscillations (sin/cos) → \(D_0\) is typically strongest locally.
— Log-scale behavior → \(D_2\) is typically strongest.

Seven familiar functions: which approximation is strongest?

The goal is structural reasoning (which series captures the function shape more efficiently). Assume \(x_0>1\) (e.g., \(x_0=10\)) and compare near \(x_0\).

1) \(f(x)=x^p\) (power law)

Strongest: \(D_1\) (relative multiplicative). Since \(\ln f=p\ln x\), the scale structure is captured very efficiently (often with very few terms).
When \(D_0\) wins: only for extremely small additive neighborhoods.

2) \(f(x)=\sqrt{x}\)

Strongest: \(D_1\) — a special case of power law with \(p=\tfrac12\).

3) \(f(x)=e^x\)

Typically strongest: \(D_1\) for relative changes (e.g., \(x=1.1x_0\)) because growth is well-described via \(\ln f\).
\(D_0\): excellent in very small additive neighborhoods.
\(D_2\): may help across broader scales if \(\ln(\ln f)\) is well-defined.

4) \(f(x)=\ln x\) (for \(x>1\))

Strongest: \(D_2\) (Option A), since \(\ln x\) naturally aligns with the \(\ln(\ln x)\) scale.
Locally: \(D_0\) also works well if \(x-x_0\) is small.

5) \(f(x)=\dfrac{1}{1-\alpha x}\) (away from the pole)

Strongest: \(D_0\) (Taylor), provided \(x_0\) is safely away from the pole \(x=1/\alpha\). Singularities typically control convergence radii in additive expansions.

6) \(f(x)=\sin x + 2\)

Strongest: \(D_0\) locally, since oscillations are best captured additively.
\(D_1\) is usually less economical for non-scale-invariant oscillations.

7) \(f(x)=1+\dfrac{1}{x}\)

Often strongest: \(D_1\) for relative changes because the variation is scale-driven and slow.
\(D_0\) is also good locally, but may require higher degree for the same accuracy over larger relative changes.

Final takeaway: There is no universally best series. The best choice depends on the function type (power / oscillatory / logarithmic) and the notion of closeness (difference / ratio / log-log scale).
