1) Additive Taylor Series (order \(n\))

We use the classical differential derivative \(D_0=\dfrac{d}{dx}\).

\[ \boxed{ f(x)\approx\sum_{k=0}^{n}\frac{D_0^{\,k}f(x_0)}{k!}\,(x-x_0)^k } \]
Proof idea: This is the standard Taylor expansion about \(x_0\) using the additive notion of closeness \((x-x_0)\).

2) Serbi Relative Series (order \(n\)) — powers of \(\frac{x}{x_0}\) only

Define the relative ratio \(r=\frac{x}{x_0}\). The Serbi series is written using only powers of \(r\) (no \(\ln\), no \(-1\)).

\[ \boxed{ \frac{f(x)}{f(x_0)} \approx \sum_{k=0}^{n}\frac{A_k(x_0)}{k!}\left(\frac{x}{x_0}\right)^k } \]

Coefficients are normalized to ensure the zeroth term equals \(1\):

\[ \boxed{ A_k(x_0)=\frac{D_1^{\,k}f(x_0)}{f(x_0)} \qquad\text{and}\qquad D_1^{\,0}f=f } \]
Proof idea: The series is built on the relative variable \(r=\frac{x}{x_0}\). The normalization by \(f(x_0)\) makes the expansion directly represent the ratio \(\frac{f(x)}{f(x_0)}\) with leading term \(1\).

3) When to use additive vs relative series?

  • Additive Taylor is suitable when \(|x-x_0|\) is naturally small.
  • Relative Serbi is suitable when scaling is natural and \(\frac{x}{x_0}\) is the meaningful measure of change.

In the numerical examples below we choose large additive gaps but moderate ratios (e.g., \(r=5\) or \(r=3\)) to emphasize the role of scale.
