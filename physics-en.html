Thermodynamic Application: Adiabatic Process (Ideal Gas)

This example shows why rank 1 (relative) language is often the natural form of many physical laws: physics frequently links ratios rather than differences.
Notation rule: every derivative is written with explicit rank and degree \(\;D_r^n\). Here we use degree \(1\): \(\;D_0^{1}, D_1^{1}\).

Concept DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.17917302

1) The physical law

\[ P\,V^{\gamma}=\text{constant}, \qquad \gamma=\frac{C_p}{C_v}. \]

\(P\) is pressure, \(V\) is volume, and \(\gamma\) is the heat-capacity ratio (ideal-gas adiabatic exponent).

2) Classical differential form (rank 0 — \(D_0^{1}\))

Take logarithms then differentiate with respect to \(V\):

\[ \ln P + \gamma \ln V = \text{constant}. \]
\[ \frac{dP}{P}+\gamma\frac{dV}{V}=0 \quad\Rightarrow\quad \frac{dP}{dV}=-\gamma\frac{P}{V}. \]
Remark: This is perfectly standard, but it hides the “ratio-structure” of the law inside the fractions \(\frac{dP}{P}\), \(\frac{dV}{V}\).

3) Hierarchical relative form (rank 1 — \(D_1^{1}\))

Define the relative derivative (rank 1, degree 1):

\[ \boxed{ D_{1}^{1} P(V)=\frac{d\ln P}{d\ln V}. } \]

From \(\ln P = -\gamma \ln V + \text{constant}\), we immediately obtain:

\[ \boxed{ D_{1}^{1} P = -\gamma. } \]
Main point: In rank-1 (relative) space, the law becomes a constant. This is exactly “linearity in log-space”.

4) Recovering the solution

Solving \(D_{1}^{1}P=-\gamma\) gives a power law:

\[ P(V)=P_0\left(\frac{V}{V_0}\right)^{-\gamma}. \]

Equivalent to \(P\,V^\gamma=\text{constant}\).

5) Quick comparison

Approach Equation Nature
Classical (rank 0) \(\dfrac{dP}{dV}=-\gamma\dfrac{P}{V}\) Differential equation in \(P(V)\)
Hierarchical (rank 1) \(D_{1}^{1}P=-\gamma\) Constant (linear in log-space)

6) Quick numerical check (validation + relative error)

Choose \(\gamma=1.4\) (air-like). Reference point: \(V_0=1.00\,\mathrm{L}\), \(P_0=100.00\,\mathrm{kPa}\). Define:

\[ K = P_0\,V_0^{\gamma} = 100.00\;(\mathrm{kPa}\cdot \mathrm{L}^{\gamma}). \]

Compute \(P(V)=P_0\left(\frac{V}{V_0}\right)^{-\gamma}\), then check \(K_i=P_iV_i^\gamma\). To illustrate a small “numerical error”, we round \(P_i\) to 2 decimals (measurement-like rounding).

\(V\) (L) \(P\) exact (kPa) \(P\) rounded \(K_i=P_iV^\gamma\) relative error
1.20 \(\approx 77.4723\) 77.47 \(\approx 99.9971\) \(\approx 0.0029\%\)
1.50 \(\approx 56.6855\) 56.69 \(\approx 100.0079\) \(\approx 0.0079\%\)
2.00 \(\approx 37.8929\) 37.89 \(\approx 99.9923\) \(\approx 0.0077\%\)
Result: \(K_i\) stays extremely close to \(K=100\) after rounding, with relative error below \(0.01\%\).

Extra check: constant relative derivative \(D_1^{1}P\)

Using two points \((V_1,P_1)\), \((V_2,P_2)\), a discrete estimate is:

\[ D_{1}^{1}P \approx \frac{\ln(P_2/P_1)}{\ln(V_2/V_1)}. \]
  • Between \(V=1.20\) and \(V=1.50\): \(D_{1}^{1}P \approx -1.3995\) (close to \(-1.4\)).
  • Between \(V=1.50\) and \(V=2.00\): \(D_{1}^{1}P \approx -1.4005\) (close to \(-1.4\)).

7) Interpretation

Hierarchical Calculus does not change physics; it selects the most appropriate “language of change”. For adiabatic processes, the natural language is relative (rank 1): the law is linear in log-space.
