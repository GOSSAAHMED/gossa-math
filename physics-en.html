Applying Hierarchical Calculus to Physics

This page is a conceptual reference. It does not replace established physics, nor does it claim experimental revision. Instead, it proposes a structured “ladder of change”: absolute change (\(D_0^1\)), relative/scale change (\(D_1^1\)), structural/logarithmic change (\(D_2^1\)), and higher ranks \(D_n^1\).
This lens helps clarify why different theories dominate different regimes, and why frontier questions (dark energy and quantum gravity) may signal deeper structural transitions.

Rank 0: \(D_0^1\) Rank 1: \(D_1^1\) Rank 2: \(D_2^1\) Higher ranks: \(D_n^1\)
Interpretation note: The physics equations below are standard. The hierarchical framework is a way to organize physical regimes by the type of change that best captures the phenomenon.

1) The derivative ladder of change

Many “conflicts” between physical theories can be interpreted as a mismatch in the type of change being modeled. Hierarchical Calculus formalizes this idea through a derivative ladder:

\[ D_0^1 f(t)=\frac{df}{dt},\qquad D_1^1 f(t)=\frac{d\ln f}{d\ln t},\qquad D_2^1 f(t)=\frac{d\ln(\ln f)}{d\ln(\ln t)} \]
Physical intuition:
Rank 0: local absolute change.
Rank 1: scale / relative change (laws stable across frames).
Rank 2: structural change in the scale-law itself (the rule evolves).
Higher ranks describe deeper structural transitions.
Framework Dominant change type Natural rank (conceptual)
Newtonian physics Absolute local change \(D_0^1\)
Relativity Relative / scale-dependent change \(D_1^1\)
Quantum mechanics Structural change (states & measurement) \(D_2^1\) (or higher)

2) Newton: rank-0 physics and core equations

Newtonian physics is built on the rank-0 derivative \(D_0^1=\dfrac{d}{dt}\) and assumes (approximately) absolute time and space for everyday regimes.

2.1 Newton’s laws

\[ \mathbf{F}=m\mathbf{a}=m\frac{d^2\mathbf{x}}{dt^2} \]

2.2 Newtonian gravity

\[ \mathbf{F}=-G\frac{Mm}{r^2}\,\hat{\mathbf{r}},\qquad \nabla^2\Phi = 4\pi G\rho \]
Hierarchical reading: Rank-0 descriptions excel when scales remain stable and measurement structure does not dominate the dynamics.

3) Einstein: relativity as scale physics

Relativity reorganizes physics around invariants and frame dependence. Conceptually, it aligns with rank-1 thinking: laws that remain meaningful under transformations and rescalings.

3.1 Special relativity

\[ ds^2 = -c^2dt^2 + dx^2 + dy^2 + dz^2,\qquad E^2=(pc)^2+(mc^2)^2 \]

3.2 General relativity

\[ G_{\mu\nu}+\Lambda g_{\mu\nu}=\frac{8\pi G}{c^4}\,T_{\mu\nu} \]
Direct link to dark energy: The term \(\Lambda\) is the standard entry point for modeling cosmic acceleration as an effective vacuum energy.

4) Quantum mechanics: structural change

Quantum mechanics replaces classical trajectories with states \(|\psi\rangle\), operators, and measurement. This is a structural transition: the “object being evolved” becomes a state in Hilbert space, and physical predictions arise from amplitudes.

4.1 Schrödinger equation

\[ i\hbar\frac{\partial}{\partial t}\,|\psi(t)\rangle=\hat{H}\,|\psi(t)\rangle \]

4.2 Uncertainty principle

\[ \Delta x\,\Delta p \ge \frac{\hbar}{2} \]

4.3 Dirac equation

\[ (i\gamma^\mu \partial_\mu - m)\psi = 0 \]
Hierarchical reading: Quantum theory introduces a new structure of description (superposition, operators, measurement), consistent with moving up the rank ladder.

5) Dark energy: acceleration as a higher-rank signal

Cosmic expansion is described by a scale factor \(a(t)\). This is intrinsically relative: distances scale rather than add. A rank-1 quantity is therefore natural:

\[ D_1^1 a(t)=\frac{d\ln a}{d\ln t} \]

Observations indicate accelerated expansion. In hierarchical terms, this may reflect a change in the scale-law itself, suggesting a rank-2 viewpoint:

\[ D_2^1 a(t)=\frac{d\ln(\ln a)}{d\ln(\ln t)} \]
Key idea: Dark energy can be interpreted as evidence that the expansion’s relative law evolves structurally (a rank transition), while in standard GR it is modeled through \(\Lambda\).

5.1 Standard symbolic link

\[ \rho_\Lambda = \frac{\Lambda c^2}{8\pi G} \]

A commonly used symbolic relation connecting \(\Lambda\) to an effective vacuum energy density.

6) Quantum gravity: where rank mismatch appears

Quantum gravity aims to unify a geometric theory (GR: smooth spacetime curvature) with a structural theory (QM: states, operators, measurement). Conceptually, it mixes different “levels of change.”

Hierarchical viewpoint: If gravity is encoded in spacetime structure, and quantum theory encodes reality via state structure, the unification may require a framework that treats structural transitions explicitly (higher-rank description), rather than forcing everything into one rank.

6.1 Wheeler–DeWitt (symbolic)

\[ \hat{H}\,\Psi[g_{\mu\nu}] = 0 \]

In some approaches, \(\Psi[g_{\mu\nu}]\) is interpreted as a “wave function of geometry,” written symbolically.

6.2 Where the ladder matters

Near the Planck regime, spacetime may not remain smooth. A hierarchical ladder suggests that describing such regimes may require ranks beyond classical geometry and beyond standard quantum evolution—capturing changes in the rules of description themselves.

Conclusion

This framework does not alter experimental truths. It organizes regimes by the type of change that best captures the phenomenon: Newton remains valid at rank 0, relativity dominates when frame/scale structure matters, and quantum theory governs when state/measurement structure is essential. Dark energy and quantum gravity appear as frontier signals suggesting deeper structural transitions.
