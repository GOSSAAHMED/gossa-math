Hierarchical Calculus as a “Ladder of Change” in Physics

This page is a conceptual reference. It does not replace established theories, nor does it claim experimental revisions. Instead, it proposes a structured way to describe physical behavior through a hierarchy of change operators: absolute change (\(D_0^1\)), relative (scale) change (\(D_1^1\)), structural logarithmic change (\(D_2^1\)), and higher ranks \(D_n^1\).

Important note: The mapping between ranks and physical frameworks is interpretative. The equations shown below are standard; the hierarchical layer is a lens for organizing regimes and modeling intuition.

1) The derivative ladder of change

Many physical laws focus on different notions of “change.” Hierarchical Calculus makes this explicit by organizing derivatives into a ladder (here shown at degree 1):

\[ D_0^1 f(t)=\frac{df}{dt},\qquad D_1^1 f(t)=\frac{d\ln f}{d\ln t},\qquad D_2^1 f(t)=\frac{d\ln(\ln f)}{d\ln(\ln t)}. \]
Quick intuition:
Rank 0: local absolute change.
Rank 1: relative / scale-based change.
Rank 2: structural change in the scale-law itself (“the relative rule evolves”).
Higher ranks describe deeper structural transitions.

Newtonian physics Absolute local dynamics \(D_0^1\)

2) Newton: rank-0 physics (local absolute dynamics)

Newtonian mechanics is naturally formulated in rank 0: \(D_0^1=\dfrac{d}{dt}\), using absolute time as a useful approximation in everyday regimes.

2.1 Newton’s second law

\[ \mathbf{F}=m\mathbf{a}=m\frac{d^2\mathbf{x}}{dt^2}. \]

2.2 Newtonian gravity

\[ \mathbf{F}=-\,G\frac{Mm}{r^2}\,\hat{\mathbf{r}},\qquad \nabla^2\Phi = 4\pi G\rho. \]
Hierarchical reading: Rank-0 models are strongest when the relevant scale does not drift dramatically and when the measurement structure does not dominate the dynamics.

3) Relativity: scale invariance and geometry

Relativity reorganizes physics around invariants across frames. This often aligns with rank-1 thinking: laws that remain meaningful under rescaling and coordinate transformations.

3.1 Special relativity

\[ ds^2 = -c^2dt^2 + dx^2 + dy^2 + dz^2,\qquad E^2=(pc)^2+(mc^2)^2. \]

3.2 General relativity (Einstein field equations)

\[ G_{\mu\nu}+\Lambda g_{\mu\nu}=\frac{8\pi G}{c^4}\,T_{\mu\nu}. \]
Link to cosmic acceleration: The cosmological constant \(\Lambda\) is the standard term used to model accelerated expansion in GR.

4) Quantum theory: state structure and measurement

Quantum mechanics replaces classical trajectories with states \(|\psi\rangle\), operators, amplitudes, and measurement. This is a structural shift: the object being evolved is a state in Hilbert space, and probabilities arise from amplitudes. Such structural changes are naturally described by higher-rank viewpoints.

4.1 Schrödinger equation

\[ i\hbar\frac{\partial}{\partial t}\,|\psi(t)\rangle=\hat{H}\,|\psi(t)\rangle. \]

4.2 Uncertainty principle

\[ \Delta x\,\Delta p \ge \frac{\hbar}{2}. \]

4.3 Dirac equation (relativistic quantum theory)

\[ (i\gamma^\mu \partial_\mu - m)\psi = 0. \]
Hierarchical reading: Quantum theory is not simply “more accurate classical mechanics.” It introduces a different mathematical structure (superposition, operator algebra, measurement).

5) Dark energy: acceleration and scale-law evolution

In cosmology, expansion is modeled using the scale factor \(a(t)\). Since expansion is relative, a rank-1 quantity appears naturally:

\[ D_1^1 a(t)=\frac{d\ln a}{d\ln t}. \]

Observations indicate accelerated expansion. Hierarchically, this may be interpreted as a change in the growth law itself—suggesting a rank-2 diagnostic:

\[ D_2^1 a(t)=\frac{d\ln(\ln a)}{d\ln(\ln t)}. \]
Interpretative idea: One may treat dark energy as evidence that the expansion law evolves structurally, while standard GR models it effectively through \(\Lambda\).

5.1 Standard symbolic relation

\[ \rho_\Lambda = \frac{\Lambda c^2}{8\pi G}. \]

(A standard symbolic relation connecting \(\Lambda\) to an effective vacuum energy density.)

6) Quantum gravity: a possible “rank mismatch”

Quantum gravity aims to unify a geometric theory (GR: spacetime curvature) with a structural theory (QM: states and operators). This is not only technically difficult; it may also reflect a mismatch in the mathematical “level of description.”

Hierarchical viewpoint: If gravity is encoded geometrically while quantum theory is encoded structurally, a successful unification may require explicit tools for structural transitions (higher-rank descriptions), rather than forcing everything into a single rank.

6.1 Wheeler–DeWitt (symbolic)

\[ \hat{H}\,\Psi[g_{\mu\nu}] = 0. \]

Here \(\Psi[g_{\mu\nu}]\) is often interpreted as a “wave function of geometry” in canonical approaches.

6.2 Where the ladder may matter

Near the Planck regime, spacetime may not remain smooth. A hierarchical ladder suggests that describing such behavior might require ranks beyond classical geometry and beyond standard quantum evolution— capturing changes in the rule-structure of description itself.

Conclusion

This framework does not modify experimental physics. It organizes physical regimes by the type of change being modeled: Newton remains effective at rank 0, relativity dominates when geometry and scale invariance matter, and quantum theory governs when state structure and measurement are essential. Dark energy and quantum gravity remain frontier regimes where a higher-rank language may offer useful modeling intuition.
