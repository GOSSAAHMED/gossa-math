Thermodynamic Application: Adiabatic Process (Ideal Gas)

This example shows why rank 1 (relative) language is often the natural form of many physical laws: laws frequently link ratios rather than differences.
Notation rule: every derivative is written with explicit rank and degree \(\;D_r^n\). In this page we use only degree 1: \(\;D_0^{1}, D_1^{1}\).

1) The physical law

\[ P\,V^{\gamma}=\text{constant}, \qquad \gamma=\frac{C_p}{C_v} \]

where \(P\) is pressure, \(V\) is volume, and \(\gamma\) is the heat-capacity ratio.

2) Classical differential form (rank 0 — \(D_0^{1}\))

Take the logarithm and differentiate:

\[ \ln P + \gamma \ln V = \text{constant} \]
\[ \frac{dP}{P}+\gamma\frac{dV}{V}=0 \quad\Rightarrow\quad \frac{dP}{dV}=-\gamma\frac{P}{V}. \]

This is a differential equation in \(P(V)\). It is standard but not “linear” in the original variables.

3) Hierarchical relative form (rank 1 — \(D_1^{1}\))

Define the relative derivative (rank 1, degree 1):

\[ \boxed{ D_{1}^{1} P(V)=\frac{d\ln P}{d\ln V} } \]

From \(\ln P = -\gamma \ln V + \text{constant}\), we immediately get:

\[ \boxed{ D_{1}^{1} P = -\gamma } \]
Main point: In rank-1 (relative) space, the law becomes a constant. This reflects that the adiabatic law is “linear” in log-space.

4) Recovering the solution

\[ P(V)=P_0\left(\frac{V}{V_0}\right)^{-\gamma} \]

5) Quick comparison

Approach Equation Nature
Classical (rank 0) \(\dfrac{dP}{dV}=-\gamma\dfrac{P}{V}\) Differential equation in \(P(V)\)
Hierarchical (rank 1) \(D_{1}^{1}P=-\gamma\) Constant (linear in log-space)

5-b) Quick numerical check: validation + relative error

Choose \(\gamma=1.4\) (air-like). Reference point: \(V_0=1.00\,\text{L}\), \(P_0=100.00\,\text{kPa}\). Then:

\[ K = P_0\,V_0^{\gamma} = 100.00 \; (\text{kPa}\cdot \text{L}^{\gamma}) \]

Compute \(P(V)=P_0\left(\frac{V}{V_0}\right)^{-\gamma}\), then check \(K_i=P_iV_i^\gamma\). To illustrate an “approximation error”, use \(P_i\) rounded to 2 decimals.

\(V\) (L) \(P\) exact (kPa) \(P\) rounded \(K_i=P_iV^\gamma\) relative error %
1.20 \(\approx 77.4723\) 77.47 \(\approx 99.9971\) \(\approx 0.0029\%\)
1.50 \(\approx 56.6855\) 56.69 \(\approx 100.0079\) \(\approx 0.0079\%\)
2.00 \(\approx 37.8929\) 37.89 \(\approx 99.9923\) \(\approx 0.0077\%\)
Result: \(K_i\) stays extremely close to \(K=100\) even after rounding, with relative error below \(0.01\%\).

Extra check: constant relative derivative \(D_1^{1}P\)

\[ D_{1}^{1}P \approx \frac{\ln(P_2/P_1)}{\ln(V_2/V_1)} \]
  • Between \(V=1.20\) and \(V=1.50\): \(D_1^{1}P \approx -1.3995\) (very close to \(-1.4\)).
  • Between \(V=1.50\) and \(V=2.00\): \(D_1^{1}P \approx -1.4005\) (very close to \(-1.4\)).

6) Interpretation

Hierarchical Calculus does not change physics; it selects the most appropriate “language of change”. For adiabatic processes, the change is naturally relative: pressure follows volume on a logarithmic scale.
