Every derivative has a rank and a degree

In Hierarchical Calculus, no derivative is written without specifying its rank (type/layer) and degree (how many successive applications). We use the standard notation:

\[ \boxed{D_{r}^{n}} \]

where \(r\) is the rank (0: differential, 1: relative, 2: logarithmic, …) and \(n\) is the degree (0,1,2,3,…).

1) Core definitions

Rank

Rank \(r\) selects the “hierarchical variable” with iterated logs:

\[ u_0(x)=x,\quad u_1(x)=\ln x,\quad u_2(x)=\ln(\ln x),\;\dots,\; u_r(x)=\ln^{(r)}(x) \]

Similarly for the function side: \[ v_r(f)=\ln^{(r)}(f) \] with appropriate domain constraints.

Base hierarchical derivative (degree 1)

The rank-\(r\), degree-1 derivative is:

\[ \boxed{ D_{r}^{1} f(x)=\frac{d\,\ln^{(r)}(f(x))}{d\,\ln^{(r)}(x)} } \]

Special cases:

\[ D_{0}^{1}f=\frac{df}{dx}, \qquad D_{1}^{1}f=\frac{d\ln f}{d\ln x}, \qquad D_{2}^{1}f=\frac{d\ln(\ln f)}{d\ln(\ln x)}. \]
Degree = successive application

Degree \(n\) means applying the same rank derivative \(n\) times:

\[ \boxed{ D_{r}^{n}(\cdot)=\underbrace{D_{r}^{1}\circ D_{r}^{1}\circ\cdots\circ D_{r}^{1}}_{n\ \text{times}}(\cdot) } \]

In particular, \(D_{r}^{0}\) is the identity operator: \[ D_{r}^{0}f=f. \]

Domain note: For ranks \(r\ge 1\), iterated logarithms appear, so domain constraints such as \(x>0\), \(f>0\) (and sometimes \(x>1\), \(f>1\) for rank 2) must be respected.

2) Relations connecting ranks and degrees

2.1 Linking \(D_0\) and \(D_1\) (different ranks)

The rank-1 relative derivative connects directly to the standard derivative:

\[ \boxed{ D_{1}^{1}f(x)=\frac{d\ln f}{d\ln x}=\frac{x}{f(x)}\,\frac{df(x)}{dx} =\frac{x}{f(x)}\,D_{0}^{1}f(x) } \]

Hence: \[ \boxed{ D_{0}^{1}f(x)=\frac{f(x)}{x}\,D_{1}^{1}f(x) } \]

2.2 A scale differential operator (useful for raising degree in \(D_1\))

Define: \[ \delta_1 := \frac{d}{d\ln x}=x\frac{d}{dx}. \] Then:

\[ \boxed{ D_{1}^{1}f=\delta_1(\ln f) } \]

Higher degrees are: \[ \boxed{ \big(D_{1}^{n}\ln f\big)=\delta_1^{\,n}(\ln f) } \]

2.3 The rank-2 operator \(D_2\) (raising degree in \(D_2\))

Define: \[ \delta_2 := \frac{d}{d\ln(\ln x)}. \] Then:

\[ \boxed{ D_{2}^{1}f=\delta_2(\ln(\ln f)) } \]

Higher degrees: \[ \boxed{ \big(D_{2}^{n}\ln^{(2)} f\big)=\delta_2^{\,n}\big(\ln(\ln f)\big) } \]

2.4 General idea: moving from rank \(r\) to \(r+1\)

Increasing rank introduces one more logarithm: \[ \ln^{(r+1)}(x)=\ln(\ln^{(r)}(x)). \] Higher ranks therefore match phenomena whose natural scaling happens over nested logarithmic scales.

3) The rank–degree grid (organizational view)

A compact symbolic table
\[ \begin{array}{c|ccc} \text{Rank } r & \text{Degree 1} & \text{Degree 2} & \text{Degree 3}\\ \hline 0 & D_0^{1}f & D_0^{2}f & D_0^{3}f\\ 1 & D_1^{1}f & D_1^{2}f & D_1^{3}f\\ 2 & D_2^{1}f & D_2^{2}f & D_2^{3}f\\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots \end{array} \]

This grid ensures every formula is written precisely: fix the rank, then increase the degree for successive derivatives.

2.5 Degree-to-degree relations within the same rank (raising degree by repetition)

Within a fixed rank, increasing the degree means repeating differentiation with respect to the corresponding hierarchical variable. In practice, we use auxiliary differential operators to get explicit, computable formulas.

\[ \text{Rank } D_1:\quad \delta_1:=\frac{d}{d\ln x}=x\frac{d}{dx}. \qquad \text{Rank } D_2:\quad \delta_2:=\frac{d}{d\ln(\ln x)}=x\ln(x)\frac{d}{dx}. \]

It is important to distinguish two sequences (the Series page uses the second one):

  • (A) Degrees applied to \(f\) itself: \(D_r^n f = D_r^1(D_r^{n-1}f)\) (direct successive definition).
  • (B) Degrees applied to \(\ln^{(r)}(f)\): the most practical for multiplicative/exponential series: \(\;A_n^{(r)} := (D_r^n\,\ln^{(r)} f)\).
\[ \boxed{ A_{1}^{(1)}(x)=\big(D_{1}^{1}\ln f\big)(x)=\delta_1(\ln f) =\frac{x}{f(x)}\,\frac{df}{dx} } \]
\[ \boxed{ A_{n+1}^{(1)}(x)=\big(D_{1}^{n+1}\ln f\big)(x)=\delta_1\!\left(A_{n}^{(1)}(x)\right) =x\frac{d}{dx}A_{n}^{(1)}(x) } \]

Explicit early degrees within rank \(D_1\):

\[ \boxed{ \big(D_{1}^{2}\ln f\big)(x) =x\frac{d}{dx}\!\left(\frac{x f'(x)}{f(x)}\right) } \]
\[ \boxed{ \big(D_{1}^{3}\ln f\big)(x) =x\frac{d}{dx}\!\left[x\frac{d}{dx}\!\left(\frac{x f'(x)}{f(x)}\right)\right] } \]

Similarly for rank \(D_2\) (where \(\ln^{(2)}f=\ln(\ln f)\)):

\[ \boxed{ A_{1}^{(2)}(x)=\big(D_{2}^{1}\ln^{(2)} f\big)(x)=\delta_2\!\big(\ln(\ln f)\big) } \]
\[ \boxed{ A_{n+1}^{(2)}(x)=\big(D_{2}^{n+1}\ln^{(2)} f\big)(x)=\delta_2\!\left(A_{n}^{(2)}(x)\right) =x\ln(x)\frac{d}{dx}A_{n}^{(2)}(x) } \]
Why this matters: it gives a direct computational recipe for raising the degree inside the same rank (especially \(D_1\) and \(D_2\)), which are exactly the coefficients used in the multiplicative/exponential series on the Series page.
Practical remark: These relations are exactly what was used to build the differential Taylor series (rank 0), the relative multiplicative Taylor series (rank 1), and the rank-2 logarithmic exponential series (rank 2) on the Series page.
