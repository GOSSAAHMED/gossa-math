Difference Between the Classical Derivative \(D_0^1\) and the Relative Derivative \(D_1^1\)

In classical calculus, the derivative measures infinitesimal linear change. Hierarchical Calculus introduces the relative derivative \(D_1^1\) to measure structural / relative variation. Although both operators describe “change”, they answer different mathematical questions.
This page explains the difference using one carefully chosen example, with a clear interpretation and practical takeaways.

Notation rule: In this website, every derivative is written with explicit rank and degree as \(D_r^n\). The classical derivative is always \(D_0^1\). The relative derivative (rank \(1\), degree \(1\)) is \(D_1^1\).

Quick Summary

  • \(D_0^1\) measures local linear change (tangent slope).
  • \(D_1^1\) measures relative / structural change inside a hierarchical framework.
  • \(D_0^1\) depends on absolute scaling, while \(D_1^1\) is designed to normalize change.
  • In Hierarchical Calculus, derivatives are written as \(D_r^n\) (explicit rank \(r\) and degree \(n\)).
  • The two operators are not interchangeable; they answer different questions.

1) What \(D_0^1\) means (classical interpretation)

The classical derivative \(D_0^1 f(x)\) is the limit of a ratio of infinitesimal differences. Geometrically, it is the slope of the tangent line at a point. Analytically, it measures how fast the function changes with respect to its variable.

Key point: \(D_0^1\) treats change as absolute and local. It does not encode hierarchy, normalization, or structural “level”.

2) What \(D_1^1\) means (relative / structural interpretation)

The relative derivative \(D_1^1\) is defined in Hierarchical Calculus to capture variation relative to the structure of the function. Instead of measuring absolute slope, \(D_1^1\) measures how the function changes in a normalized way, using explicit hierarchical parameters.

Concept Classical calculus Hierarchical calculus
Derivative symbol \(D_0^1\) \(D_r^n\) (explicit rank \(r\) and degree \(n\))
What is measured? Absolute local change Relative / structural change
Hierarchy Not present Central (rank controls level)
Reminder: Here we compare the classical operator \(D_0^1\) to the relative operator \(D_1^1\) using one example.

3) One example: \(f(x)=x^2\) on \(x>0\)

We choose a simple function to isolate the conceptual difference:

\[ f(x)=x^2,\qquad x>0. \]

This example is intentionally simple: it allows us to compare what \(D_0^1\) and \(D_1^1\) measure without extra technical assumptions.

4) Computing \(D_0^1 f(x)\)

Classical calculus gives:

\[ D_0^1 f(x)=\frac{d}{dx}(x^2)=2x. \]

At \(x=2\):

\[ D_0^1 f(2)=4. \]
Interpretation: \(D_0^1 f(2)=4\) is the tangent slope at \(x=2\). It depends on the absolute location \(x\) and changes if the scale changes.

5) Computing \(D_1^1 f(x)\) (relative behavior)

The rank-\(1\), degree-\(1\) relative derivative \(D_1^1\) captures normalized structural change. For power functions \(x^a\), \(D_1^1(x^a)=a\). Therefore, for \(f(x)=x^2\):

\[ D_1^1\big(x^2\big)=2. \]

In particular at \(x=2\):

\[ D_1^1 f(2)=2. \]
Interpretation: \(D_1^1\) returns the structural exponent-like behavior of the function, which remains stable under multiplicative scaling.

6) Why the answers differ (the real reason)

The difference reflects two different questions:

Operator Question answered What changes the value?
\(D_0^1\) “How fast does \(f\) change at this point?” Location \(x\), local slope, absolute scale
\(D_1^1\) “What is the relative structural variation of \(f\)?” Structure class (e.g., exponent in \(x^a\))
Takeaway: \(D_0^1\) is absolute and local. \(D_1^1\) is relative and structural. Their values are expected to differ.

7) Practical guidance: when to use which?

  • Use \(D_0^1\) when you need absolute local sensitivity (classical differential change).
  • Use \(D_1^1\) when you need relative / scale-normalized variation (hierarchical structure).
  • If your phenomenon is naturally multiplicative, \(D_1^1\) can be more stable than \(D_0^1\).
