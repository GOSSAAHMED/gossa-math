Difference Between the Classical Derivative \(D_0\) and the Relative Derivative \(D_1\)

In classical calculus, the derivative measures infinitesimal linear change. Hierarchical Calculus introduces the relative derivative \(D_1\) to measure structural / relative variation. Although both operators describe “change”, they answer different mathematical questions.
This page explains the difference using one carefully chosen example, with a clear interpretation and practical takeaways.

Tip: If you are new, read Theory first, then Rank & Degree.

Quick Summary

  • \(D_0\) measures local linear change (tangent slope).
  • \(D_1\) measures relative / structural change inside a hierarchical framework.
  • \(D_0\) depends on absolute scaling, while \(D_1\) is designed to normalize change.
  • \(D_1\) uses explicit parameters (rank and degree), while \(D_0\) does not.
  • The two operators are not interchangeable; they answer different questions.

1) What \(D_0\) means (classical interpretation)

The classical derivative \(D_0 f(x)\) is the limit of a ratio of infinitesimal differences. Geometrically, it is the slope of the tangent line at a point. Analytically, it measures how fast the function changes with respect to its variable.

Key point: \(D_0\) treats change as absolute and local. It does not encode a notion of hierarchy, normalization, or structural “level”.

2) What \(D_1\) means (relative / structural interpretation)

The relative derivative \(D_1\) is defined in Hierarchical Calculus to capture variation relative to the structure of the function. Instead of measuring absolute slope, \(D_1\) measures how the function changes in a normalized way, using explicit hierarchical parameters.

Concept Classical calculus Hierarchical calculus
Derivative symbol \(D_0\) \(D_r^n\) (explicit rank \(r\) and degree \(n\))
What is measured? Absolute local change Relative / structural change
Hierarchy Not present Central (rank controls level)
Notation reminder: In this website, \(D_0\) denotes the classical differential derivative, and \(D_1\) denotes the relative derivative. When needed, hierarchical derivatives are written as \(D_r^n\).

3) One example: \(f(x)=x^2\) on \(x>0\)

We choose a simple function to isolate the conceptual difference:

\[ f(x)=x^2,\qquad x>0. \]

This example is intentionally simple: it allows us to compare what \(D_0\) and \(D_1\) are measuring without extra technical assumptions.

4) Computing \(D_0 f(x)\)

Classical calculus gives:

\[ D_0 f(x)=\frac{d}{dx}(x^2)=2x. \]

At \(x=2\):

\[ D_0 f(2)=4. \]
Interpretation: \(D_0 f(2)=4\) is the slope of the tangent line at \(x=2\). If we scale the input or evaluate at a different \(x\), the value changes accordingly.

5) Computing \(D_1 f(x)\) (rank 1, degree 1)

In Hierarchical Calculus, the most common relative derivative is rank \(1\), degree \(1\), written \(D_1^1\). For power functions \(x^a\), it yields a constant result equal to the exponent \(a\). Therefore, for \(f(x)=x^2\):

\[ D_1^1\big(x^2\big)=2. \]

In particular at \(x=2\):

\[ D_1^1 f(2)=2. \]
Interpretation: \(D_1^1\) returns the relative structural behavior of \(f\). For a power law \(x^a\), the “relative change” is controlled by the exponent \(a\), not by the absolute scale.

6) Why the answers differ (the real reason)

The difference is not a computational accident; it reflects two different questions:

Operator Question answered What changes the value?
\(D_0\) “How fast does \(f\) change at this point?” Location \(x\), scaling, local slope
\(D_1^1\) “What is the relative structural variation of \(f\)?” Structure class (e.g., exponent in \(x^a\))
Takeaway: \(D_0\) is a local, absolute slope operator. \(D_1\) is a relative, normalized structural operator. Therefore, their values are expected to differ.

7) Practical guidance: when to use which?

Both derivatives are useful, but they serve different modeling goals:

  • Use \(D_0\) when you need absolute local sensitivity (classical differential change).
  • Use \(D_1\) when you need relative / scale-normalized variation (hierarchical structure).
  • If your phenomenon is naturally multiplicative or scale-relative, \(D_1\) can be more stable than \(D_0\).
