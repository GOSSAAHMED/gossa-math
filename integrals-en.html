Hierarchical Integrals

Hierarchical integrals are the natural inverses of hierarchical derivatives. At rank 0 the accumulation is additive, at rank 1 it becomes relative (multiplicative), and at rank 2 it becomes logarithmic (structurally exponential).
Notation rule: every derivative is written with explicit rank and degree: \(\;D_r^n\). Here, most formulas are for degree 1, e.g. \(\;D_n^{1}\).

1) Definition: the hierarchical integral \(\mathcal{I}_n\)

The rank-\(n\) hierarchical integral operator \(\mathcal{I}_n\) is the inverse of the hierarchical derivative at degree 1, i.e. \(D_n^{1}\), on a suitable domain (with a hierarchical integration constant).

\[ \boxed{ D_{n}^{1}\big(\mathcal{I}_n[f]\big)=f } \]
Note: Just as the integration constant is additive at rank 0, it becomes multiplicative at rank 1, and structurally exponential at rank 2, etc.

2) Fundamental theorem (hierarchical)

2.1) First inverse relation

\[ \boxed{ D_{n}^{1}\!\big(\mathcal{I}_n[f]\big)=f } \]

2.2) Second inverse relation (up to a hierarchical constant)

If \(F\) satisfies \(D_n^{1}F=f\), then:

\[ \boxed{ \mathcal{I}_n\!\left[D_{n}^{1}F\right]=F\;\odot\;C_n } \]
Rank Constant form Operation \(\odot\)
0 \(C\) (additive) \(+\)
1 \(C_\times>0\) (multiplicative) \(\times\)
2 \(C_{\exp}>1\) (structural/exponential) exponential composition
Idea: At each rank, the “constant” follows the accumulation structure: sum → product → exponent → ...

3) Relative integral \(\mathcal{I}_1\) (rank 1) — 5 examples

Practical formula:

\[ \boxed{ \mathcal{I}_1[f](x)=\exp\!\left(\int \frac{f(x)}{x}\,dx\right) } \]
Function \(f(x)\) Relative integral \(\mathcal{I}_1[f](x)\) Quick check (explicit \(D_r^n\))
\(f(x)=1\) \(\mathcal{I}_1[1]=x\) \(D_{1}^{1}(x)=1\)
\(f(x)=a\) (constant) \(\mathcal{I}_1[a]=x^a\) \(D_{1}^{1}(x^a)=a\)
\(f(x)=\ln x\) \(\mathcal{I}_1[\ln x]=e^{(\ln x)^2/2}\) \(D_{1}^{1}(\cdot)=\ln x\)
\(f(x)=x^k\) \(\mathcal{I}_1[x^k]=e^{x^k/k}\ (k\neq0)\) \(D_{1}^{1}(\cdot)=x^k\)
\(f(x)=\dfrac{1}{\ln x}\) \(\mathcal{I}_1\!\left[\frac{1}{\ln x}\right]=\ln x\) \(D_{1}^{1}(\ln x)=\frac{1}{\ln x}\)

These examples show how rank-1 integration naturally produces controlled relative growth (products and exponentials appear naturally).

4) Logarithmic integral \(\mathcal{I}_2\) (rank 2) — 5 examples

On the domain \(x>1\) (so that \(\ln x>0\)), we use:

\[ \boxed{ \mathcal{I}_2[f](x)= \exp\!\left( \exp\!\left(\int \frac{f(x)}{x\,\ln x}\,dx\right) \right) } \]
Function \(f(x)\) Logarithmic integral \(\mathcal{I}_2[f](x)\) Quick check (explicit \(D_r^n\))
\(f(x)=1\) \(\mathcal{I}_2[1]=x\) \(D_{2}^{1}(x)=1\)
\(f(x)=a\) (constant) \(\mathcal{I}_2[a]=e^{(\ln x)^a}\) \(D_{2}^{1}(\cdot)=a\)
\(f(x)=\ln\ln x\) \(\mathcal{I}_2[\ln\ln x]=\exp(\exp((\ln\ln x)^2/2))\) \(D_{2}^{1}(\cdot)=\ln\ln x\)
\(f(x)=\ln x\) \(\mathcal{I}_2[\ln x]=e^{x}\) \(D_{2}^{1}(e^x)=\ln x\)
\(f(x)=(\ln\ln x)^n\) \(\mathcal{I}_2[(\ln\ln x)^n]=\exp(\exp((\ln\ln x)^{n+1}/(n+1)))\) \(D_{2}^{1}(\cdot)=(\ln\ln x)^n\)

Rank-2 integration builds a “structural accumulation”: the double exponential appears naturally because the measuring scale is \(\ln\ln x\).
