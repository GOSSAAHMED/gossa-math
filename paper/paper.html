Abstract

In this work, we introduce Hierarchical Calculus, a unified framework that organizes different notions of variation—additive, relative, logarithmic, and higher-order—into a structured hierarchy. Classical differentiation appears as the lowest level of this hierarchy, while higher levels correspond to relative and iterated logarithmic derivatives. A general relation linking all hierarchical derivatives is derived, showing that each level can be obtained from the previous one through a systematic rescaling. This framework provides a natural language for laws exhibiting scale invariance or multi-scale behavior. As an illustration, an adiabatic process in thermodynamics is reformulated using the relative derivative, leading to a remarkably simple representation. Numerical experiments confirm the robustness of the hierarchical formulation with respect to approximation errors. The proposed approach does not replace classical analysis but complements it by offering an organized method to select the most appropriate level of description.

1. Introduction

The concept of differentiation is one of the central pillars of mathematical analysis and its applications in physics, engineering, and the natural sciences. Classically, differentiation measures additive variations of quantities with respect to one another and has proven extremely successful in modeling continuous phenomena.

However, many laws encountered in physics, biology, economics, and other applied fields are not naturally expressed in terms of additive changes. Instead, they involve relative variations, ratios, or scale-invariant relations. Typical examples include power laws, exponential growth, thermodynamic relations, and multi-scale processes. In such cases, reformulating the problem in logarithmic or relative coordinates often leads to simpler and more transparent expressions.

Several approaches exploiting logarithmic derivatives or scale-invariant formulations already exist in the literature. Nevertheless, these tools are usually introduced in an ad hoc manner, without a unifying framework that clarifies their mutual relations or guides the choice of the most appropriate formulation.

The aim of this work is to propose a systematic organization of different notions of variation into a single coherent structure, referred to as Hierarchical Calculus. In this framework, classical differentiation appears as the lowest level of a hierarchy. Higher levels correspond to relative derivatives and iterated logarithmic derivatives, each representing a change of measurement language rather than a fundamentally new operation.

A central result of this work is the derivation of a general relation linking all hierarchical derivatives. This relation shows that moving from one level to the next amounts to a well-defined rescaling governed by the natural scale of the variables involved. As a consequence, equations that are nonlinear or cumbersome at the differential level may reduce to remarkably simple forms at higher hierarchical levels.

2. Formal Definitions of Hierarchical Derivatives

We provide rigorous definitions. Logarithms are defined only where their arguments are positive and nonzero.

\[ D_0 f(x) := \frac{df}{dx}(x). \] \[ D_1 f(x) := \frac{d\ln f(x)}{d\ln x} = \frac{x}{f(x)}\,\frac{df}{dx}(x). \] \[ D_2 f(x) := \frac{d\ln(\ln f(x))}{d\ln(\ln x)}. \] \[ D_n f(x) := \frac{d\,\ln^{(n)}(f(x))}{d\,\ln^{(n)}(x)}\quad (n\ge 1), \qquad \ln^{(n)}=\underbrace{\ln\circ\cdots\circ\ln}_{n\ \text{times}}. \]
Domain conditions
Each level requires that all iterated logarithms \(\ln^{(k)}(x)\) and \(\ln^{(k)}(f(x))\) are well defined and nonzero. Higher levels impose stricter domains (e.g. \(x>1\), \(f(x)>1\) for log–log constructions).

3. The Golden Theorem

The following identity is the structural core of Hierarchical Calculus.

\[ \boxed{ D_{n+1} f(x) = D_n f(x)\, \frac{\ln^{(n)}(x)}{\ln^{(n)}(f(x))} } \]

Proof (sketch): By definition \(D_{n+1} f = \dfrac{d\ln(\ln^{(n)} f)}{d\ln(\ln^{(n)} x)}\). Apply the chain rule to numerator and denominator and rearrange terms to obtain the formula. (Full proof is identical to the website theorem proof and follows directly from the chain rule.)

\[ D_n f(x) = D_{n+1} f(x)\, \frac{\ln^{(n)}(f(x))}{\ln^{(n)}(x)}. \]

4. Hierarchical Equations

A hierarchical equation of level \(n\) is any relation involving \(x\), \(f(x)\), and one or more derivatives \(D_k f(x)\) with \(0\le k\le n\). It may be written:

\[ \mathcal{F}\bigl(x,f(x),D_0 f(x),D_1 f(x),\dots,D_n f(x)\bigr)=0. \]

Example (power-law equation):

\[ \frac{df}{dx} = k\,\frac{f(x)}{x} \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad D_1 f(x)=k \quad\Longrightarrow\quad f(x)=C\,x^k. \]

5. Physical Application: Adiabatic Process (Ideal Gas)

For an ideal gas undergoing an adiabatic process:

\[ P\,V^{\gamma}=\text{constant}, \qquad \gamma=\frac{C_p}{C_v}>1. \]

Classical (level 0) form:

\[ \frac{dP}{dV}=-\gamma\frac{P}{V}. \]

Hierarchical (level 1) form:

\[ D_1 P(V)=\frac{d\ln P}{d\ln V} \quad\Rightarrow\quad \boxed{D_1P=-\gamma.} \]

Solution:

\[ P(V)=P_0\left(\frac{V}{V_0}\right)^{-\gamma}. \]

6. Numerical Validation

We take \(\gamma=1.4\), \(V_0=1.00\) L, \(P_0=100.00\) kPa. Then \(K=P_0V_0^\gamma=100.00\). For \(P(V)=P_0(V/V_0)^{-\gamma}\), we compute \(K_i=P_iV_i^\gamma\) using pressures rounded to 2 decimals.

\[ D_1P \approx \frac{\ln(P_2/P_1)}{\ln(V_2/V_1)}. \]

7. Scope and Limitations

Hierarchical Calculus organizes existing notions of variation into a structured hierarchy and helps select an appropriate language of change for scale-dependent phenomena. It does not replace classical analysis, nor does it guarantee simplification for arbitrary equations.

  • Scope: scale invariance, power laws, multi-scale behavior, robust ratio-based numerics.
  • Not claimed: new physical laws, universal simplification, domain-free applicability.
  • Domain restrictions: iterated logarithms must be well-defined and nonzero at the chosen level.
  • Higher levels: represent iterated logarithmic scaling; avoid confusion with classical tetration as iterated exponentials.

8. Conclusion and Perspectives

We introduced Hierarchical Calculus as a coherent framework linking differential, relative, and iterated-logarithmic derivatives. The Golden Relation provides the structural identity connecting levels and is grounded in classical differentiation and the chain rule. Applications to hierarchical equations and an adiabatic thermodynamics example illustrate how scale-invariant laws can become constant relations at the appropriate level. Numerical checks suggest robustness of ratio/log-based formulations.

Future directions include extensions to PDEs, systematic numerical schemes at higher levels, and broader applications to multi-scale modeling across physics, biology, and economics.
