Hierarchical Equations

A generalization of classical differential equations toward higher levels (relative/logarithmic/tetrational...) to simplify structure via hierarchical lifting.

1) General Definition

A level-\(n\) hierarchical equation is any relation linking a function \(f\) with its hierarchical derivatives up to level \(n\).

\[ \mathcal{E}_n\big(x,\; f,\; D_0 f,\; D_1 f,\; \dots,\; D_n f\big)=0 \]

Using your notation: D0 = differential and D1 = relative. Typical forms:

\[ D_0 f(x)=\frac{df}{dx}, \qquad D_1 f(x)=\frac{d\ln f(x)}{d\ln x}=\frac{x}{f(x)}\,\frac{df}{dx} \]
\[ D_2 f(x)=\frac{d\ln(\ln f(x))}{d\ln(\ln x)}, \qquad D_3 f(x)=\frac{d\ln^{(3)}(f(x))}{d\ln^{(3)}(x)} \]
Key idea
Many equations that look highly nonlinear at one level become structurally simpler (often closer to linear/separable) after a suitable lift to a higher level.

2) Quick Classification

Differential (Level 0)

\[ F\big(x,f,D_0 f,\dots\big)=0 \]
Base structure: additive change.

Relative (Level 1)

\[ F\big(x,f,D_1 f,\dots\big)=0 \]
Base structure: ratios/self-scaling.

Logarithmic (Level 2)

\[ F\big(x,f,D_2 f,\dots\big)=0 \]
Base structure: iterated logs.

Tetrational (Level 3)

\[ F\big(x,f,D_3 f,\dots\big)=0 \]
Base structure: higher-level layering.

3) Canonical Examples

These examples show how to express equations at different levels and how lifting can reduce structural complexity.

Example A — Level 0

\[ D_0 f(x)=g(x) \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad \frac{df}{dx}=g(x) \]

Example B — Level 1

\[ D_1 f(x)=g(x) \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad \frac{d\ln f}{d\ln x}=g(x) \]
\[ \frac{d\ln f}{dx}= \frac{g(x)}{x} \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \ln f(x)=\int \frac{g(x)}{x}\,dx + C \]

Example C — Level 2

\[ D_2 f(x)=g(x) \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad \frac{d\ln(\ln f)}{d\ln(\ln x)}=g(x) \]

Example D — Lifting sketch

\[ u=\ln f \;\Rightarrow\; D_1 f=\frac{d u}{d\ln x} \]
\[ v=\ln(\ln f) \;\Rightarrow\; D_2 f=\frac{d v}{d\ln(\ln x)} \]
Reviewer note
A strict version should always state domain assumptions: \(x>0\), \(f(x)>0\), and avoid singular points where \(\ln x=0\) or \(\ln f=0\) when using \(\ln(\ln(\cdot))\).

4) Hierarchical Lifting

The goal is not to “replace calculus”, but to choose the right level representation of change so that the equation becomes structurally simpler (often separable/linearizable).

\[ \mathcal{E}_k\big(x,f,D_0 f,\dots,D_k f\big)=0 \quad \xrightarrow{\;\text{Lift}\;} \widetilde{\mathcal{E}}_{k+1}\big(x,\,\ln^{(k+1)}f,\, D_{k+1}f\big)=0 \]

Practical workflow

  • Identify the growth/variation type (power? exponential? multi-scale?).
  • Choose a suitable level \(k\).
  • Transform via \(u=\ln^{(k)} f\).
  • Solve in \(u\), then invert (iterated exponentiation).
