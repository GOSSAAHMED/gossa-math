Fully Solved Examples (Analytic)

Notation: \(D_0\) differential, \(D_1\) relative, \(D_2\) logarithmic. Domain assumptions apply when using logs: \(x>0\), \(f(x)>0\), and for \(D_2\): \(\ln f(x)>0\).

Example 1 — Level-1 equation

Solve: \[ D_1 f(x)=k \quad (\text{constant}) \]

\[ D_1 f(x)=\frac{d\ln f}{d\ln x}=k \Rightarrow d\ln f = k\, d\ln x \] \[ \ln f = k\ln x + C \Rightarrow f(x)=C_0\,x^{k} \]

Thus, a constant relative derivative yields a power law directly.

Example 2 — Nonlinear relative equation becomes linear

Solve: \[ D_1 f(x)=a+b\ln f(x) \] with constants \(a,b\) and \(f>0\).

\[ \frac{d\ln f}{d\ln x}=a+b\ln f \] Let \(u=\ln f\). Then: \[ \frac{du}{d\ln x}=a+bu \Rightarrow \frac{du}{d\ln x}-bu=a \] Solution: \[ u(\ln x)=C\,e^{b\ln x}-\frac{a}{b}=C\,x^{b}-\frac{a}{b} \] Hence: \[ \ln f(x)=C\,x^{b}-\frac{a}{b} \Rightarrow f(x)=\exp\!\left(C\,x^{b}-\frac{a}{b}\right) \]

Example 3 — Level-2 equation

Solve: \[ D_2 f(x)=k \] Assume \(x>1\) and \(f(x)>e\).

\[ \frac{d\ln(\ln f)}{d\ln(\ln x)}=k \Rightarrow d\ln(\ln f)=k\,d\ln(\ln x) \] \[ \ln(\ln f)=k\ln(\ln x)+C \Rightarrow \ln f = C_1(\ln x)^k \] \[ f(x)=\exp\!\big(C_1(\ln x)^k\big) \]

Real Physics Example (Thermodynamics)

For an ideal gas in an adiabatic process: \[ P\,V^\gamma=\text{constant} \] Taking logs: \[ \ln P + \gamma \ln V = \text{constant} \Rightarrow \frac{d\ln P}{d\ln V}=-\gamma \] So in relative-derivative form: \[ D_1^{(V)} P(V)=\frac{d\ln P}{d\ln V}=-\gamma \]

Direct outcome
The adiabatic law is naturally a constant level-1 equation, giving immediately: \( P(V)=C\,V^{-\gamma} \). This illustrates why level-1 is the “native language” for such laws.

Numerical Note (Error Perspective)

Consider the multi-scale model: \[ f(x)=\exp\big((\ln x)^2\big) \] at \(x=10\). The true value is: \[ f(10)=\exp\big((\ln 10)^2\big) \]

\[ \ln 10 \approx 2.302585093 \Rightarrow (\ln 10)^2 \approx 5.30189811 \Rightarrow f(10)\approx e^{5.30189811}\approx 200.336 \]

A level-2 viewpoint computes \(\ln f=(\ln x)^2\) first (smaller-scale error), then exponentiates—often more stable than approximating \(f\) directly.
