Comparison Between Hierarchical Calculus and Existing Calculi

A rank–degree comparison explaining the two-index notation and the unified definition of the hierarchical derivative \(D_n^1\).

This page is designed as both a reference and an educational overview: it positions Hierarchical Calculus relative to classical, fractional, relative/logarithmic, and time-scale calculi, and highlights what is structurally new (rank + degree + a unified definition).

1. Introduction

Classical differential calculus has been extended in multiple directions to address limitations related to scale dependence, structural rigidity, or specific modeling requirements. Examples include fractional calculus, various forms of logarithmic derivatives, and other specialized constructions.

Hierarchical Calculus is proposed as a framework in which differentiation is organized according to a well-defined hierarchy. In this setting, classical differential calculus appears naturally as a special case of a more general structure, and relative/logarithmic behaviors are integrated as specific hierarchical levels rather than separate ad-hoc systems.

Key idea of this comparison

The goal is not to claim that existing calculi are “wrong”, but to show that many frameworks are introduced as separate islands. Hierarchical Calculus aims to provide a single organizing language: rank + degree + a unified definition, from which rules are derived.

2. Rank and Degree in Hierarchical Derivatives

In Hierarchical Calculus, every derivative is uniquely characterized by two numerical indices: a rank and a degree. This resolves a common conceptual issue: multiple “types” of derivatives exist in the literature, but there is no universal labeling system telling you what type you are using and how many times it is iterated.

Rank (subscript) determines the nature of the derivative (the transformation layer):

  • 0 — classical differential derivative (D0)
  • 1 — relative derivative (D1)
  • 2 — logarithmic derivative (higher layer)
  • … — higher ranks can be defined within the same language

Degree (superscript) indicates the number of successive differentiations within the same rank, from \(1\) to \(n\).

Notation (two-index form): \[ D_{r}^{\,n} \] where \(r\) is the rank (subscript) and \(n\) is the degree (superscript).

For example, \(D_{0}^{\,n}\) denotes the classical differential derivative of degree \(n\), while \(D_{1}^{\,1}\) denotes the first-degree relative derivative, and \(D_{2}^{\,1}\) denotes a first-degree logarithmic derivative within the hierarchy.

3. Unified definition of the hierarchical derivative \(D_n^1\)

To eliminate contradictions across pages, the site adopts one unified definition for the hierarchical derivative of order \(n\) and degree \(1\):

\[ \boxed{ D_n^{1} f(x) = \frac{d\,\ln^{(n)}\!\big(f(x)\big)} {d\,\ln^{(n)}\!(x)} } \]
Consistency rule (very important)

This site does not use mixed forms such as \(\frac{d(\ln f)}{d\ln^{(n)}x}\). The adopted definition applies \(\ln^{(n)}\) on both sides: to \(x\) and to \(f(x)\).

3.1 Domain conditions (when the definition is well-posed)

Because \(\ln^{(n)}\) appears explicitly, basic conditions typically include: \[ x>0 \quad \text{and} \quad f(x)>0 \] and higher ranks may impose stronger positivity constraints depending on how many iterated logarithms are applied.

4. Comparison with Classical Differential Calculus

Classical calculus involves a single parameter: the degree of differentiation. It does not explicitly classify different derivative types. In practice, the type is fixed (d/dx) and assumed.

Hierarchical Calculus extends the structure by introducing the rank parameter, allowing multiple derivative types to coexist in one framework while keeping degrees as natural numbers.

Special case: classical differential calculus is recovered when \[ r=0. \]

Example (power function)

Let \(f(x)=x^a\) with \(x>0\). Then:

\[ D_0^1(x^a)=a x^{a-1} \qquad\text{whereas}\qquad D_1^1(x^a)=\frac{d\ln(x^a)}{d\ln(x)}=a. \]

This illustrates the structural role of rank: \(D_0\) measures classical differential change, while \(D_1\) measures relative (log-scale) change.

5. Comparison with Fractional Calculus

Fractional calculus generalizes differentiation by allowing non-integer degrees. These operators are frequently non-local and often require integral representations or memory effects (depending on the chosen definition).

Hierarchical Calculus does not fractionalize the degree; instead, it generalizes the rank (the structural type of derivative), while degrees remain natural numbers within each rank.

Essential difference

Fractional calculus generalizes the degree (possibly to non-integers), whereas Hierarchical Calculus generalizes the rank (type of derivative / transformation level). They target different structural questions.

6. Comparison with Logarithmic and Relative Calculi

Relative and logarithmic derivatives are often introduced as separate constructions designed for particular applications (for example, scale-invariant modeling).

In Hierarchical Calculus, these derivatives correspond to specific ranks (e.g., \(r=1\) for relative behavior, and higher ranks for deeper log layers), and are integrated into the hierarchy rather than treated as independent calculi.

Extra example (exponential function)

Let \(f(x)=e^x\). At rank \(1\), we obtain:

\[ D_1^1(e^x)=\frac{d\ln(e^x)}{d\ln(x)}=\frac{d(x)}{d(\ln x)}=x. \]

The example is simple but revealing: the relative derivative transforms the behavior into a quantity tied directly to the scale of \(x\).

7. Comparison with Time-Scale Calculus

Time-scale calculus aims to unify continuous and discrete analysis by generalizing the underlying domain of the independent variable. In other words, it generalizes the domain.

Hierarchical Calculus generalizes the structure of differentiation itself (how differentiation is defined at multiple ranks), independently of whether the domain is continuous, discrete, or mixed. These can be viewed as complementary directions: one generalizes the domain, the other generalizes the derivative structure.

9. Conclusion + extra examples

Hierarchical Calculus introduces a dual-index structure (rank + degree) and a unified definition based on iterated logarithmic transforms. This provides a systematic way to embed classical differentiation while organizing relative/logarithmic behaviors within one coherent language.

Extra sanity-check examples (strengthening understanding)

(a) If \(f(x)=x\) with \(x>0\), then:

\[ D_1^1(x)=\frac{d\ln(x)}{d\ln(x)}=1. \]

(b) If \(f(x)=c\) is a positive constant, then:

\[ D_1^1(c)=\frac{d\ln(c)}{d\ln(x)}=0. \]
