Comparison Between Hierarchical Calculus and Existing Calculi

A rank–degree based comparison of hierarchical differentiation frameworks

1. Introduction

Classical differential calculus relies on a single notion of differentiation order and has been extended in various directions in order to model more complex phenomena. Examples include fractional calculus, logarithmic derivatives, and other specialized frameworks.

Hierarchical Calculus is proposed as a unified framework in which differentiation is organized according to a well-defined hierarchy. In this setting, classical differential calculus appears naturally as a special case of a more general structure.

This page presents a structural comparison between Hierarchical Calculus and several existing calculi, emphasizing the defining role of rank and degree.

2. Rank and Degree in Hierarchical Derivatives

In Hierarchical Calculus, every derivative is uniquely characterized by two numerical indices: a rank and a degree.

The subscript represents the rank of the derivative and determines its mathematical nature:

  • 0 — Differential derivative
  • 1 — Relative derivative
  • 2 — Logarithmic derivative

The superscript represents the degree of the derivative and indicates the number of successive differentiations, ranging from 1 to n.

Notation. A hierarchical derivative is denoted by Drn, where r is the rank and n is the degree.

For example, D0n denotes the classical differential derivative of degree n, while D21 denotes the first-degree logarithmic derivative.

3. Comparison with Classical Differential Calculus

Classical calculus involves only one parameter: the degree of differentiation. It does not distinguish between different types or ranks of derivatives.

In contrast, Hierarchical Calculus extends this structure by introducing a rank parameter, allowing multiple derivative types to coexist within a single framework.

Classical differential calculus is recovered exactly when r = 0.

4. Comparison with Fractional Calculus

Fractional calculus generalizes differentiation by allowing non-integer degrees. Such derivatives are often non-local and rely on integral representations.

Hierarchical Calculus does not modify the degree into fractional values. Instead, it introduces distinct ranks while keeping the degree as a natural number.

Essential difference: Fractional calculus generalizes the degree, whereas Hierarchical Calculus generalizes the rank.

5. Comparison with Logarithmic and Relative Calculi

Logarithmic and relative derivatives are typically introduced as independent constructions for specific applications.

Within Hierarchical Calculus, such derivatives correspond to specific ranks and are integrated into the general hierarchical structure rather than treated as separate calculi.

6. Comparison with Time-Scale Calculus

Time-scale calculus focuses on unifying continuous and discrete domains by modifying the independent variable.

Hierarchical Calculus instead focuses on the structure of differentiation itself, independently of the underlying domain.

8. Conclusion

Hierarchical Calculus introduces a dual-index structure based on rank and degree, providing a clear and systematic generalization of classical differentiation.

By embedding classical calculus as the rank-zero case, the theory preserves established methods while enabling coherent extensions to new derivative types.
