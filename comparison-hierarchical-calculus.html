Comparison Between Hierarchical Calculus and Existing Calculi

A rank–degree based comparison emphasizing the two-index notation of Hierarchical Derivatives.

1. Introduction

Classical differential calculus has been extended in multiple directions to address limitations related to scale dependence, structural rigidity, or modeling requirements. Examples include fractional calculus, logarithmic derivatives, and other specialized frameworks.

Hierarchical Calculus is proposed as a unified framework in which differentiation is organized according to a well-defined hierarchy. In this setting, classical differential calculus appears naturally as a special case of a more general structure.

This page presents a structural comparison between Hierarchical Calculus and several existing calculi, emphasizing the defining role of rank and degree.

2. Rank and Degree in Hierarchical Derivatives

In Hierarchical Calculus, every derivative is uniquely characterized by two numerical indices: a rank and a degree.

Rank (subscript) determines the nature of the derivative:

  • 0 — Differential derivative
  • 1 — Relative derivative
  • 2 — Logarithmic derivative

Degree (superscript) indicates the number of successive differentiations, from \(1\) to \(n\).

Notation (Two-index form): \[ D_{r}^{\,n} \] where \(r\) is the rank (subscript) and \(n\) is the degree (superscript).

For example, \(D_{0}^{\,n}\) denotes the classical differential derivative of degree \(n\), while \(D_{2}^{\,1}\) denotes the first-degree logarithmic derivative.

3. Comparison with Classical Differential Calculus

Classical calculus involves a single parameter: the degree of differentiation, and it does not distinguish between different derivative types.

Hierarchical Calculus extends this structure by introducing a rank parameter, enabling several derivative types to coexist within a unified framework.

Special case: Classical differential calculus is recovered exactly when \(r=0\). \[ D_{0}^{\,n} \;\;\text{is the classical differential derivative of degree } n. \]

4. Comparison with Fractional Calculus

Fractional calculus generalizes differentiation by allowing non-integer degrees. These operators are frequently non-local and often require integral representations.

Hierarchical Calculus does not generalize the degree into fractional values; instead, it generalizes the rank while maintaining the degree as a natural number \(n\).

Essential difference: Fractional calculus generalizes the degree (possibly to non-integers), whereas Hierarchical Calculus generalizes the rank (type of derivative).

5. Comparison with Logarithmic and Relative Calculi

Logarithmic and relative derivatives are often introduced as separate constructions designed for specific applications.

In Hierarchical Calculus, such derivatives correspond to specific ranks (e.g., \(r=1\) or \(r=2\)) and are integrated into the hierarchical structure rather than treated as independent calculi.

6. Comparison with Time-Scale Calculus

Time-scale calculus focuses on unifying continuous and discrete domains by generalizing the underlying domain of the independent variable.

Hierarchical Calculus focuses instead on the structure of differentiation itself, independently of the underlying domain.

8. Conclusion

Hierarchical Calculus introduces a dual-index structure based on rank and degree, providing a clear and systematic generalization of classical differentiation.

By embedding classical calculus as the rank-zero case, the theory preserves established methods while enabling coherent extensions to additional derivative types.
