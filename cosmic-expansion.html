Application: Cosmic Expansion

An illustrative application of the relative derivative \(D_1\) and hierarchical operators \(D_n\) to the cosmic scale factor \(a(t)\).

1. Motivation

In cosmology, the large-scale evolution of the universe is captured by the scale factor \(a(t)\). Many cosmological behaviors are naturally expressed through ratios (e.g., \(a(t)/a(t_0)\)) and relative change, rather than absolute increments. Hierarchical calculus emphasizes this multiplicative viewpoint.

Important: This page is an analytical illustration. It does not propose a new physical theory and does not replace standard General Relativity or the Friedmann equations. It shows how hierarchical calculus can represent scale-dependent dynamics in a clean, local, and ratio-based form.

2. Cosmological setup and notation

Let \(t>0\) denote cosmic time and let \(a(t)\) be the cosmic scale factor. Choose a reference time \(t_0>0\) and define \(a_0=a(t_0)\). We use:

  • Classical derivative: \(D_0 a(t)=\dfrac{da}{dt}\)
  • Relative derivative: \(D_1 a(t)=t\,\dfrac{da}{dt}\)
  • Hierarchical level: \(D_n = \underbrace{D_1\circ\cdots\circ D_1}_{n\ \text{times}}\)

3. Relative derivative and scale-aware growth

The operator \(D_1\) measures change relative to the scale of the independent variable \(t\):

\[ D_1 a(t)=t\,\frac{da}{dt}. \]

This is useful in settings where the evolution is naturally described by scaling laws and power behavior.

4. Model I: First-level hierarchical law

Consider the first-level hierarchical differential equation:

\[ D_1 a(t)=\lambda\,a(t), \] where \(\lambda\in\mathbb{R}\) is dimensionless.

Using \(D_1 a=t a'(t)\), we obtain:

\[ t\,\frac{da}{dt}=\lambda a(t) \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \frac{1}{a(t)}\frac{da}{dt}=\frac{\lambda}{t}. \]

Integrating gives a power-law expansion:

\[ a(t)=a_0\left(\frac{t}{t_0}\right)^{\lambda}. \]

This highlights a key feature of hierarchical dynamics: power-law behavior appears as a natural eigen-structure of \(D_1\).

5. Model II: Second-level hierarchical law

A second-level illustrative model uses \(D_2=D_1\circ D_1\):

\[ D_2 a(t)=\mu\,a(t), \] where \(\mu\in\mathbb{R}\).

For trial functions \(a(t)=t^p\), hierarchical calculus yields:

\[ D_1(t^p)=p\,t^p,\qquad D_2(t^p)=p^2\,t^p. \]

Hence the characteristic relation becomes:

\[ p^2=\mu. \]

When \(\mu>0\), representative solutions can be written as:

\[ a(t)=C_1\,t^{\sqrt{\mu}}+C_2\,t^{-\sqrt{\mu}}. \]

This illustrates how higher hierarchical levels produce algebraic spectra (powers) rather than exponential spectra.

6. Hierarchical series around \(t_0\)

Using the hierarchical series concept (ratio-based representation), one may formally expand \(a(t)\) around a reference time \(t_0\):

\[ a(t) = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{D_k a(t_0)}{k!} \left(\frac{t}{t_0}\right)^k. \]

This representation uses only the dimensionless ratio \(t/t_0\), aligning with the multiplicative nature of cosmological scaling. (A rigorous convergence study can be developed separately.)
