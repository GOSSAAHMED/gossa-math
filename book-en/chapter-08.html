8.1 Formal Definition of the Relative Derivative

\[ D_1 y \;=\; \frac{d(\ln y)}{d(\ln x)}. \]
Domain conditions: To ensure \(\ln x\) and \(\ln y\) are defined, we require: \[ x>0,\qquad y>0. \]

What does this derivative measure?

The relative derivative measures the relative change of \(y\) with respect to the relative change of \(x\).

8.2 Relation between \(D_1\) and the classical derivative \(D_0\)

If the classical derivative \(D_0y=\dfrac{dy}{dx}\) exists, then: \[ D_1y =\frac{\dfrac{d(\ln y)}{dx}}{\dfrac{d(\ln x)}{dx}} =\frac{\dfrac{1}{y}\dfrac{dy}{dx}}{\dfrac{1}{x}} =\frac{x}{y}\,\frac{dy}{dx}. \]
Hence the fundamental identity: \[ D_1y=\frac{x}{y}\,D_0y, \qquad D_0y=\frac{y}{x}\,D_1y. \]

Numerical example

Let \(y=x^2\). At \(x=3\): \(D_0y=6\), \(y=9\). Thus: \[ D_1y=\frac{3}{9}\cdot 6=2, \] which coincides with the power exponent.

8.3 Interpretation: Elasticity and Local Exponent

For small variations: \[ d(\ln y)\approx \frac{dy}{y},\qquad d(\ln x)\approx \frac{dx}{x}. \] Thus: \[ D_1y\approx \frac{\dfrac{dy}{y}}{\dfrac{dx}{x}}. \]
If \(D_1y=1.2\) at a given point, a 1% increase in \(x\) produces approximately a 1.2% increase in \(y\) near that point.

8.4 Central Result: Power Laws Become Linear

If \[ y=Cx^a,\quad C>0, \] then \[ \ln y=\ln C+a\ln x \Rightarrow D_1y=a \quad (\text{constant}). \]

Example 1

\(y=5x^{1.5}\Rightarrow D_1y=1.5\) everywhere.

Example 2 (inverse power)

\(y=10x^{-2}\Rightarrow D_1y=-2\). The negative sign indicates a decreasing relative response.

8.5 Discrete Approximation from Data

Between two data points \((x_i,y_i)\) and \((x_{i+1},y_{i+1})\): \[ \widehat{D_1}= \frac{\ln y_{i+1}-\ln y_i}{\ln x_{i+1}-\ln x_i}. \]
If \(\widehat{D_1}\) is nearly constant across segments, the data strongly suggests a power-law behavior.

8.6 More Numerical Examples

Example 4: \(y=x^3+1\)

\[ D_1y=\frac{3x^3}{x^3+1}. \] At \(x=1\): \(D_1=1.5\). At \(x=10\): \(D_1\approx 2.997\).

Example 5: \(y=e^x\)

\(D_1y=x\). The relative exponent grows with \(x\), so this is not a power law.

Example 6: \(y=\ln x\) (for \(x>1\))

\[ D_1y=\frac{1}{\ln x}. \] At \(x=e\): \(D_1=1\); at \(x=e^2\): \(D_1=0.5\).

Example 7: \(y=\sqrt{x}\)

\(D_1y=\frac12\), a constant, confirming the power exponent.

Example 8: Composite growth \(y=x^a(\ln x)^b\)

\[ \ln y=a\ln x+b\ln\ln x \Rightarrow D_1y=a+\frac{b}{\ln x}. \] As \(x\to\infty\), \(D_1y\to a\).

8.7 Estimating Parameters from Data

Assuming \(y\approx Cx^a\), from two data points: \[ a=\frac{\ln(y_2/y_1)}{\ln(x_2/x_1)},\qquad C=\frac{y_1}{x_1^a}. \]

Example 9 (full computation)

Data: \((10,50)\), \((40,127.3)\). \[ a=\frac{\ln(127.3/50)}{\ln(4)}\approx 0.674, \qquad C\approx 10.6. \] Thus \(y\approx 10.6\,x^{0.674}\).

8.8 Chapter Summary

  • \(D_1=\dfrac{d(\ln y)}{d(\ln x)}\) is the true relative derivative.
  • \(D_1\) links directly to \(D_0\) via \(D_1=\dfrac{x}{y}y'\).
  • Constant \(D_1\) ‚áî power-law behavior.
  • Discrete \(\widehat{D_1}\) efficiently reveals power exponents from data.
