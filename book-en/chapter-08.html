8.1 From \(D_0\) to \(D_1\): Why Relative Measurement?

The classical derivative \(D_0\) measures absolute change: how much \(y\) changes when \(x\) changes. In many systems, absolute change is insufficient because the meaning of variation depends on the magnitude of the quantity itself.

We therefore introduce a measure that captures relative change: change in proportion, not just in amount.

Relative measurement is a natural language for growth, economics, data analysis, and scale-sensitive systems.

8.2 Definition of the Relative Derivative \(D_1\) (No Logarithms)

Definition: Let \(y=f(x)\) with \(x\neq 0\) and \(y\neq 0\). The relative derivative is defined by \[ D_1 y \;=\; \frac{\dfrac{dy}{y}}{\dfrac{dx}{x}} \;=\; \frac{x}{y}\,\frac{dy}{dx}. \]

Thus \(D_1\) measures the relative change of \(y\) with respect to the relative change of \(x\). It can be interpreted as an elasticity-like measure: how many percent \(y\) changes when \(x\) changes by one percent.

The direct bridge between levels: \[ D_1 y = \frac{x}{y}\,D_0 y. \] This is a first explicit example of a ‚Äútransition relation‚Äù between hierarchy levels.

8.3 Interpretation and Dimensionless Nature

While \(D_0 y\) typically carries physical units, \(D_1\) often becomes dimensionless because it compares ratios. This makes it especially useful in scaling laws and sensitivity analysis.

8.4 Core Properties of \(D_1\)

8.4.1 Invariance under multiplication by a constant

If \(c\neq 0\) is constant, then

\[ D_1(cy)=D_1(y). \]

8.4.2 Product rule in relative form

If \(y=uv\) with \(u\neq 0\) and \(v\neq 0\), then

\[ D_1(uv)=D_1(u)+D_1(v). \]

This is fundamental: at the relative level, multiplication becomes additive.

8.4.3 Quotient rule

\[ D_1\left(\frac{u}{v}\right)=D_1(u)-D_1(v)\quad (v\neq 0). \]

8.4.4 Power rule

If \(y=u^a\) with \(u\neq 0\), then

\[ D_1(u^a)=a\,D_1(u). \]

8.4.5 Chain rule (relative form)

If \(y=f(u)\) and \(u=g(x)\), then

\[ D_1 y \;=\; \frac{u}{y}\frac{dy}{du}\;\cdot\;\frac{x}{u}\frac{du}{dx}. \]

8.5 Useful Special Cases

1) If \(y=x^a\) (with \(x\neq 0\)):
\[ D_1 y=a. \]

A key fact: the relative derivative of a power law is the exponent itself.

2) If \(y=Cx^a\) (with \(C\neq 0\)):
\[ D_1 y=a. \]

Constants cancel out under relative measurement.

3) If \(y=e^{kx}\):
\[ D_1 y=kx. \]

8.6 Worked Examples

Example 1: Compute \(D_1(x^5)\).
Solution: \[ D_1(x^5)=5. \]
Example 2: If \(y=3x^2\), compute \(D_1 y\).
Solution: \[ D_1(3x^2)=D_1(x^2)=2. \]
Example 3: If \(y=x^2(1+x)\) (with \(x\neq 0\) and \(1+x\neq 0\)), compute \(D_1 y\).
Solution: \[ D_1(x^2(1+x))=D_1(x^2)+D_1(1+x)=2+\frac{x}{1+x}. \]

8.7 Bridge to the Next Level \(D_2\)

If \(D_1\) measures relative change, the next question is natural: what if relative change itself changes in a structured way? This motivates the next level \(D_2\), introduced later as a deeper measurement layer.

Summary: \(D_1\) is the first step beyond absolute measurement, and it anchors the hierarchy.
