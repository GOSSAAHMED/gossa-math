15.1 What ‚ÄúHierarchical Stability‚Äù Means

A function/system is said to be hierarchically stable at level \(n\) if the law becomes constant or nearly constant (or at least much slower-varying) when expressed at that scale.

15.2 Two Practical Numerical Metrics

(A) Distance-to-1 stability index: \[ S_n(x)=|D_n y(x)-1|. \] Small \(S_n\) over a wide domain indicates strong stability at level \(n\).
(B) Variation across a domain: \[ V_n(x_1,x_2)=|D_n y(x_2)-D_n y(x_1)|. \] Small \(V_n\) indicates that level \(n\) is more stable than lower levels.

15.3 Decision Rule: When to Climb

  • Compute \(D_1\). If it varies fast or becomes huge, try \(D_2\).
  • If \(D_2\) still varies clearly and the structure involves \(\ln\ln\), try \(D_3\).
  • If \(\ln\ln\ln\) appears naturally, try \(D_4\).
  • Stop at the first level where the law becomes constant/linear/directly integrable.

15.4 Typical Calibration Facts

  • If \(y=x^a\): \(D_1=a\) and \(D_n=1\) for all \(n\ge2\).
  • If \(y=\exp((\ln x)^m)\): \(D_2=1\) identically.
  • If \(y=\exp(\exp((\ln\ln x)^p))\): \(D_3\) becomes constant (often \(=1\) or \(=p\)).
  • If \(y=\exp(\exp(\exp((\ln\ln\ln x)^p)))\): \(D_4=1\) identically.

15.5 Fourteen Numerical Examples

We choose points such as \(x=e^2,e^3,e^5,e^{e^2}\) so logs become clean.

Group (A): Stability begins at \(D_2\)

Ex.1 \(y=x^7\): \(D_1=7\), \(D_2=D_3=1\).

Ex.2 \(y=x^{1/2}\): \(D_1=1/2\), \(D_2=D_3=1\).

Ex.3 \(y=\exp((\ln x)^3)\): \(D_1=3(\ln x)^2\) varies (12 at \(e^2\), 75 at \(e^5\)), but \(D_2=1\).

Ex.4 \(y=\exp((\ln x)^{10})\): \(D_1=10(\ln x)^9\) is huge (5120 at \(e^2\), 196830 at \(e^3\)), but \(D_2=1\).

Group (B): \(D_3\) stabilizes nested-log structures

Ex.5 \(y=\exp(\exp((\ln\ln x)^2))\): \(D_3=2\) constant; at \(x=e^{e^2}\), \(D_3=2\).

Ex.6 \(y=\exp(\exp((\ln\ln x)^5))\): \(D_3=1\) constant.

Ex.7 \(y=\exp(\exp(\ln\ln x))\): stable behavior is most visible in \(z=\ln\ln y\); at \(x=e^{e^2}\), \(y=\exp(\exp(2))\approx 1618.177\).

Ex.8 \(y=\exp(\exp((\ln\ln x)^2+\ln\ln x))\): \(D_2\) varies; deeper-scale linearity emerges more naturally at level \(D_3\).

Group (C): Need \(D_4\) for a third nested scale

Ex.9 \(y=\exp(\exp(\exp(\ln\ln\ln x)))\): \(D_4=1\) clearly.

Ex.10 \(y=\exp(\exp(\exp((\ln\ln\ln x)^3)))\): \(D_4=1\).

Ex.11 \(y=\exp(\exp(\exp((\ln\ln\ln x)^2)))\): \(D_3\) varies strongly but \(D_4=1\).

Group (D): Metrics \(S_n\) and \(V_n\)

Ex.12 \(y=\exp((\ln x)^3)\): between \(x_1=e^2\) and \(x_2=e^5\), \(V_1=|75-12|=63\) while \(V_2=0\).

Ex.13 \(y=\exp(\exp((\ln\ln x)^2))\): between \(x_1=e^{e^2}\) and \(x_2=e^{e^3}\), \(D_3\) stays constant (=2) so \(V_3=0\).

Ex.14 \(y=\exp(\exp(\exp((\ln\ln\ln x)^3)))\): \(D_4=1\Rightarrow S_4=0\) and \(V_4=0\) on the domain.

Core pattern: the deeper the nested-log structure of the phenomenon, the higher the level at which stability becomes visible.

15.6 The ‚ÄúDepth Rule‚Äù

If the natural law contains an iterated logarithm \(\ln^{(k)}\), then choosing level \(D_{k+1}\) often yields the simplest (constant/linear) description.
