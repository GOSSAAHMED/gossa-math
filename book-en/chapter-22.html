Chapter 22 — Classical Differential Equations

Absolute-change equations based on \(D_0=\dfrac{dy}{dx}\)

22.1 Introduction

Classical differential equations form the foundation of traditional mathematical modeling. They are entirely built on the standard derivative \(D_0\).

22.2 General Definition

A classical differential equation has the form: \[ F(x,y,y',y'',\dots)=0. \]

22.3 First-Order Examples

Example 1: \(y'=2x \Rightarrow y=x^2+C\)
Example 2: \(y'=y \Rightarrow y=Ce^x\)
Example 3: \(y'=xy \Rightarrow y=Ce^{x^2/2}\)

22.4 Second-Order Equations

Example 4: \(y''+y=0\)
Example 5: \(y''-y=0\)

22.5 Key Properties

  • Based on absolute variables.
  • Unit-dependent.
  • Often locally valid.

22.6 Fundamental Limitations

  • Poor handling of relative growth.
  • Highly nonlinear equations become intractable.
  • Multi-scale systems are difficult to model.

22.7 Role in This Book

  • Serves as a classical reference.
  • Prepares comparison with hierarchical equations.

22.8 Summary

  • Classical ODEs are powerful but limited.
  • They motivate hierarchical generalization.
