10.1 The Idea of \(D_2\): When \(D_1\) Is Not Enough

Chapters 8‚Äì9 introduced \(D_1\) as a measure of relative change: how \(y\) changes relatively when \(x\) changes relatively. In many phenomena, however, this ‚Äúelasticity‚Äù is not constant‚Äîit evolves with scale.

This leads to a new question: how do we measure the change of \(D_1\) itself? In other words, how do we measure the evolution of relative structure?

If \(D_1\) is a ‚Äúrelative slope,‚Äù then \(D_2\) plays the role of a ‚Äúrelative acceleration‚Äù or ‚Äúchange of elasticity.‚Äù

10.2 A Practical Definition of Level \(D_2\)

The simplest consistent hierarchical move is to take the quantity \(D_1 y\) and measure its relative change with respect to \(x\).

Definition: If \(D_1 y \neq 0\) and \(x\neq 0\), define \[ D_2 y \;=\; D_1\!\big(D_1 y\big) \;=\; \frac{\dfrac{d(D_1 y)}{(D_1 y)}}{\dfrac{dx}{x}} \;=\; \frac{x}{D_1 y}\,\frac{d(D_1 y)}{dx}. \]

Thus \(D_2\) measures relative change of the relative derivative. If \(D_1 y\) is constant, then \(D_2 y=0\).

10.3 Linking Formulas: \(D_2\) in Terms of \(D_1\) and \(D_0\)

From the definition, \[ D_2 y = \frac{x}{D_1 y}\frac{d(D_1 y)}{dx}. \] Since \[ D_1 y = \frac{x}{y}\frac{dy}{dx}, \] we can compute \(D_2\) by: compute \(D_1 y\), differentiate it classically, then scale.

Practical computation rule: \[ D_2 y=\frac{x}{D_1 y}\,D_0(D_1 y). \]

10.4 Interpretation: Constant vs Evolving Elasticity

  • If \(y=Cx^a\), then \(D_1 y=a\) is constant, hence \(D_2 y=0\).
  • If \(D_1 y\) varies with \(x\), then \(D_2 y\) captures its relative rate of change.
\(D_1\) answers: ‚Äúwhat is the relative change?‚Äù \(D_2\) answers: ‚Äúis that relative change constant, and how does it evolve?‚Äù

10.5 Basic Properties

10.5.1 If \(D_1 y\) is constant, then \(D_2 y=0\)

This is immediate because \(d(D_1 y)/dx=0\).

10.5.2 Invariance under scaling

Since \(D_1(cy)=D_1(y)\) for \(c\neq 0\), it follows that \[ D_2(cy)=D_2(y). \]

10.5.3 Product behavior (indirect view)

Because \(D_1(uv)=D_1(u)+D_1(v)\), we have \[ D_2(uv)=\frac{x}{D_1(u)+D_1(v)}\frac{d\left(D_1(u)+D_1(v)\right)}{dx}, \] meaning \(D_2\) captures how the total elasticity changes with scale.

10.6 Worked Examples

Example 1: If \(y=x^a\) (with \(x\neq 0\)), compute \(D_2 y\).
Solution: \[ D_1 y=a \Rightarrow D_2 y=0. \]
Example 2: If \(y=e^{kx}\), compute \(D_2 y\).
Solution: \[ D_1 y=kx,\qquad D_2 y=\frac{x}{kx}\frac{d(kx)}{dx}=\frac{x}{kx}\cdot k=1. \]
Example 3: If \(y=e^{x^2}\) (with \(x\neq 0\)), compute \(D_2 y\).
Solution: \[ D_0 y = 2x e^{x^2}, \quad D_1 y=\frac{x}{y}D_0 y=2x^2, \quad D_2 y=\frac{x}{2x^2}\frac{d(2x^2)}{dx}=2. \]
Example 4: If \(y=x^a e^{kx}\) (with \(x\neq 0\)), compute \(D_2 y\).
Solution: \[ D_1 y = D_1(x^a)+D_1(e^{kx})=a+kx, \quad D_2 y=\frac{x}{a+kx}\frac{d(a+kx)}{dx}=\frac{kx}{a+kx}. \]

10.7 A Logarithmic Reading (Interpretation Only)

Using the classical identity \[ d(\ln y)=\frac{dy}{y}, \] one may interpret \(D_1 y\) as the derivative of \(\ln y\) with respect to \(\ln x\) (conceptually). This helps explain why logarithmic tools naturally appear when moving to higher levels.

In this book, the defining construction of \(D_2\) is kept operator-based: \(D_2 y = D_1(D_1 y)\).

10.8 Toward the General Level \(D_n\)

The construction here suggests a general hierarchical stacking:

\[ D_2 y = D_1(D_1 y). \]

A natural general pattern is \[ D_{n+1}y = D_1(D_n y), \] with domain conditions (non-zero quantities) stated precisely later.

10.9 Chapter Summary

  • \(D_2\) measures the relative change of \(D_1 y\).
  • If \(D_1 y\) is constant, then \(D_2 y=0\).
  • For \(y=e^{kx}\), \(D_2 y=1\).
  • For \(y=e^{x^2}\), \(D_2 y=2\).

Chapter 11 develops properties and computational rules of \(D_2\) in a more systematic form.
