7.1 Why Start with \(D_0\)?

Although this book builds a hierarchy of measurement levels, the natural starting point is classical calculus. This is not only historical but methodological: \(D_0\) provides the baseline notion of change, and higher levels will extend or reinterpret it.

We treat \(D_0\) as the level-zero operator in the derivative hierarchy: absolute measurement of change.

7.2 The Basic Definition (Difference Quotient)

If \(y=f(x)\) is differentiable, the classical derivative at a point \(x\) is defined by:

\[ D_0 f(x)=\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}. \]

This definition formalizes slope: the ratio of output change to input change, taken in the limit of infinitesimal increments.

Methodological note: \(D_0\) is an absolute measure because it compares \(dy\) and \(dx\) without normalization by the magnitudes of \(x\) or \(y\).

7.3 Geometric Meaning

Geometrically, \(D_0 f(x)\) is the slope of the tangent line to the curve \(y=f(x)\) at the point \(x\). Positive slope indicates local increase; negative slope indicates local decrease.

The derivative is inherently local: it describes behavior in a small neighborhood of a point.

7.4 Physical Meaning

In physics, \(D_0\) is a rate of change:

  • If \(x=t\) is time and \(y=s(t)\) is distance, then \(D_0 s(t)=v(t)\) is velocity.
  • And \(D_0 v(t)=a(t)\) is acceleration.

This makes \(D_0\) central for modeling motion and dynamics.

7.5 Fundamental Rules of \(D_0\)

7.5.1 Linearity

\[ D_0(af+bg)=a\,D_0 f + b\,D_0 g. \]

7.5.2 Product Rule

\[ D_0(fg)=f\,D_0 g + g\,D_0 f. \]

7.5.3 Quotient Rule

\[ D_0\!\left(\frac{f}{g}\right)=\frac{g\,D_0 f - f\,D_0 g}{g^2},\quad g\neq 0. \]

7.5.4 Chain Rule

If \(y=f(u)\) and \(u=g(x)\), then: \[ D_0(f(g(x))) = (D_0 f)(g(x))\cdot D_0 g(x). \]
These rules make classical derivatives computable in complex models. Yet they do not resolve the scale problem, which motivates \(D_1\).

7.6 Short Worked Examples

Example 1: If \(f(x)=x^n\) (positive integer \(n\)), then: \[ D_0(x^n)=n x^{n-1}. \]
Example 2: If \(f(x)=e^{kx}\), then: \[ D_0(e^{kx})=k e^{kx}. \]

Notice: absolute change is proportional to the function valueâ€”this naturally motivates relative measurement in the next chapter.

Example 3: If \(f(x)=\ln x\) (for \(x>0\)), then: \[ D_0(\ln x)=\frac{1}{x}. \]

7.7 Where Does \(D_0\) Fall Short?

Despite its power, \(D_0\) may miss meaning when significance depends on proportions. In population growth or inflation, what matters is often the percentage change, not the raw increase.

This naturally raises the question: can we define a derivative that directly captures relative change?

This question leads to the next level in the hierarchy: \(D_1\) (the relative derivative).
