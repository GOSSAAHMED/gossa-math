Chapter 23 — Hierarchical Reformulation of Differential Equations

From absolute change to multi-scale structure

23.1 Conceptual Introduction

Many differential equations appear complicated because they are written at the wrong scale. Hierarchical reformulation changes the scale of description.

23.2 Core Principle

Any differential equation can be reformulated using hierarchical derivatives \(D_0, D_1, D_2, \dots\).

23.3 Example: Exponential Growth

\[ y'=ky \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad D_1y=k \]

23.4 Example: Power Law

\[ y'=a\frac{y}{x} \Rightarrow D_1y=a \]

23.5 Deep Nonlinearity

\[ y'=y\ln y \Rightarrow D_2y=1 \]

23.6 Extreme Case

\[ y'=y\ln y\ln\ln y \Rightarrow D_3y=1 \]

23.7 Numerical Examples

\(y'=y^2 \Rightarrow D_2y=1\)
\(y'=y(\ln y)^2 \Rightarrow D_2y=2\)
\(y'=xy\ln y \Rightarrow D_2y=x\)
\(y'=y(\ln y)(\ln\ln y) \Rightarrow D_3y=1\)

23.8 Structural Interpretation

  • Each level removes one layer of nonlinearity.
  • Constants reveal deep invariants.
  • The hierarchy identifies the natural scale.

23.9 Summary

  • Hierarchical reformulation simplifies equations.
  • Intractable ODEs become linear or constant.
  • This sets the stage for a new theory.
