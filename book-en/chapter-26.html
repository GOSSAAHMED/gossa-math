Chapter 26 — Transformations Between Hierarchical Equation Levels

Changing the scale of description to simplify dynamics

26.1 Conceptual Introduction

Hierarchical calculus allows systematic transformation between equation levels, revealing the natural scale of a system.

26.2 Core Transformation Rule

\[ D_{n+1}y = \frac{d(\ln^{(n+1)}y)}{d(\ln^{(n+1)}x)}. \]

26.3 Examples

\(y'=ky \Rightarrow D_1y=k\)
\(D_1y=\ln y \Rightarrow D_2y=1\)
\(y'=a\,y/x \Rightarrow D_1y=a \Rightarrow D_2y=0\)
\(y'=y\ln y \Rightarrow D_2y=1\)
\(y'=y^2 \Rightarrow D_2y=1\)
\(y'=y(\ln y)^2 \Rightarrow D_2y=2\)
\(y'=y\ln y\ln\ln y \Rightarrow D_3y=1\)
\(y'=x y \Rightarrow D_1y=x \Rightarrow D_2y=\ln x\)
\(y'=x^n y \Rightarrow D_2y=(n+1)\ln x\)
\(y'=y e^{e^x} \Rightarrow D_3y=x\)

26.4 Structural Meaning

  • Each transformation removes explosive growth.
  • Constants mark stability levels.
  • The hierarchy identifies the natural equation level.

26.5 Summary

  • Hierarchical transformations are systematic.
  • They simplify extreme nonlinear equations.
  • They prepare the general \(D_n\) theory.
