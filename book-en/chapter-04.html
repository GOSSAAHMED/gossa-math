4.1 Why Document the Foundations?

Mathematical theories do not appear fully formed. They emerge as ideas, become definitions, then evolve into coherent structures with properties, identities, and applications. Documenting the historical foundations is not ceremonial‚Äîit is part of the theory-building process because it:

  • clarifies the logical sequence of the concepts,
  • exposes the original problem statement and motivation,
  • preserves the early formulations as foundational sources,
  • helps the reader understand the ‚Äúwhy‚Äù before the ‚Äúhow.‚Äù
In this book we emphasize two foundational milestones: Relative Calculus (2011) as the conceptual basis of \(D_1\), and Logarithmic Calculus (2012) as the conceptual basis of \(D_2\).

4.3 What ‚ÄúRelative Calculus‚Äù Meant in 2011

The central idea of Relative Calculus is that absolute measurement of change (the classical derivative \(D_0\)) can be insufficient in many systems because the meaning of change depends on scale and magnitude. A new measure is needed, one that captures the change of a quantity relative to itself.

4.3.1 The conceptual nucleus

Instead of only evaluating \[ D_0 y = \frac{dy}{dx}, \] one asks: what is the relative variation of \(y\) compared to the relative variation of \(x\)? This leads naturally to expressions of the form \[ \frac{dy/y}{dx/x}. \] This is not yet the final definition, but it clearly indicates a transition: from an absolute level of measurement to a relative level of measurement.

The rigorous definition of the relative derivative \(D_1\) will be developed later (Chapter 8). Here we highlight that its motivation and direction were already explicit in the 2011 foundational work.

4.4 From Relative to Logarithmic (the 2012 step)

Once relative measurement is introduced, a deeper question arises: what if relative behavior itself becomes structured and multi-regime? Here, the logarithm becomes a natural mathematical tool because it:

  • turns multiplicative structures into additive ones, simplifying growth laws,
  • appears naturally in scale analysis and normalization,
  • provides a stable lens for interpreting ratios.

4.4.1 The classical bridge

The well-known identity \[ d(\ln y) = \frac{dy}{y} \] shows that relative change is directly linked to changes in the logarithm of a quantity. This makes it natural to build a higher measurement level on a logarithmic structure.

The 2012 work prepares the ground for \(D_2\): a deeper layer of relative measurement where logarithmic structure becomes part of the calculus itself.

4.5 Toward the ‚ÄúHierarchy‚Äù Idea

What makes the 2011 and 2012 documents seeds of Hierarchical Calculus? They introduce a crucial shift: the idea of levels. Instead of one universal derivative, measurement is organized in layers:

  • \(D_0\): absolute change,
  • \(D_1\): relative change,
  • \(D_2\): logarithm-structured relative change (deeper than \(D_1\)),
  • \(\dots\): higher levels built progressively.

This layered view becomes the backbone of the derivative hierarchy developed later.

4.6 Continuity Across Versions

The foundational documents are not presented as final formulations. They are an early stage. The book enforces continuity on three levels:

  • Conceptual continuity: the same motivation‚Äîmeasurement that respects scale.
  • Structural continuity: the transition \(D_0 \to D_1 \to D_2\).
  • Notational continuity: a unified notation system within one book.
