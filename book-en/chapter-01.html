1.1 Change as the Core of Scientific Knowledge

There is no science without change. Since the earliest attempts to understand nature, the fundamental question has always been: How does something change? Motion of bodies, evolution of organisms, growth of economies, transfer of energy, and transitions between physical states are all manifestations of the same underlying question: what is change?

Natural sciences, across all disciplines, are structured efforts to measure change, describe it, and predict its behavior. Consequently, genuine scientific progress is inseparable from the development of precise tools for measuring change.

1.2 From Qualitative Description to Quantitative Measurement

In early stages of knowledge, change was described qualitatively: faster, hotter, larger. While such descriptions are indispensable, they are insufficient for rigorous science.

The decisive transition occurred when description was replaced by measurement, intuition by number. Mathematics became the language of science, culminating in the development of differential calculus as a systematic theory of change.

1.3 The Classical Derivative and Its Historical Success

The classical derivative

\[ D_0 y = \frac{dy}{dx} \]

represents one of the greatest achievements in mathematics. It provides a precise measure of instantaneous change and enables the formulation of physical laws, differential equations, and mathematical models across science and engineering.

1.4 Limits of Absolute Measurement

Despite its power, the classical derivative measures change in an absolute sense: how much \(y\) changes with respect to \(x\), without regard to the scale or magnitude of \(y\) itself.

This raises a fundamental question: Is absolute change sufficient to describe all phenomena? In many real systems, the answer is no.

1.5 The Need for Relative Change

In practice, we are often more interested in how much a quantity changes relative to its size. A small variation in a sensitive system may be more significant than a large variation in a massive one.

Such information is invisible to absolute measurement but becomes evident through relative change.

1.6 From Relative Change to Relative Calculus

This insight motivates the introduction of a new mathematical tool that does not replace classical calculus but extends it. This tool is the relative derivative, denoted in this book by \(D_1\).

1.7 Why a Single Level of Measurement Is Not Enough

Complex systems often exhibit change across multiple layers. Relative change itself may vary, and the appropriate scale depends on the observational level.

1.8 Hierarchy as a Natural Principle

Hierarchy is a fundamental feature of nature: atoms form molecules, molecules form cells, cells form organisms. Each level has its own laws, yet remains connected to others.

1.9 From Natural Hierarchy to Hierarchical Calculus

Hierarchical Calculus is based on a simple principle: if phenomena are hierarchical, then the measurement of change must also be hierarchical.

1.10 The Role of This Chapter

This chapter establishes the conceptual foundation of the book. It prepares the reader for the formal definitions and mathematical structures developed in the following chapters.
