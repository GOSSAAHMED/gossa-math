Chapter 30 — Mechanics and Relative Change

Rewriting mechanics using \(D_1\) and \(D_2\)

Conceptual Introduction

Many mechanical laws are fundamentally relative. Hierarchical calculus provides a natural formulation.

Examples (1–20)

1. \(x=vt \Rightarrow D_1x=1\)
2. \(x=t^2 \Rightarrow D_1x=2\)
3. \(x=e^t \Rightarrow D_1x=t\)
4. \(x=t^n \Rightarrow D_2x=0\)
5. \(x=e^t \Rightarrow D_2x=1\)
6. Newton: \(F=ma \Rightarrow D_1v=\text{const}\)
7. Drag: \(F\sim v \Rightarrow D_1v=\text{const}\)
8. \(E_k\sim v^2 \Rightarrow D_1E=2\)
9. \(E\sim x^n \Rightarrow D_1E=n\)
10. \(x=A\sin\omega t\)
11. \(x=t^3 \Rightarrow D_1=3\)
12. \(x=e^{2t} \Rightarrow D_2=1\)
13. \(v\sim t^{-1} \Rightarrow D_1=-1\)
14. \(E\sim t^4 \Rightarrow D_1=4\)
15. \(x=\exp(t^2) \Rightarrow D_2=2\)
16. \(p\sim v \Rightarrow D_1=1\)
17. \(E\sim e^t \Rightarrow D_2=1\)
18. \(x=\exp(\exp t) \Rightarrow D_3=1\)
19. \(x\sim \ln t \Rightarrow D_1<0\)
20. Free fall: \(x\sim t^2 \Rightarrow D_1=2\)
