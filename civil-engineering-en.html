Hierarchical Calculus in Civil Engineering

Civil engineering problems are rarely “about numbers only”. They are about scale, ratios, accumulation, and accelerating risk. The classical derivative \(D_0^1\) measures absolute change. The relative derivative \(D_1^1\) measures structural change (percentage-like behavior). The logarithmic hierarchical derivative \(D_2^1\) captures acceleration of risk (a change of the change-rate itself, at a higher hierarchical level).

This page provides 20 real civil engineering applications with numeric examples and a practical guide for choosing ranks \(D_0^1, D_1^1, D_2^1\), and beyond.

Notation rule: Every derivative is written as \(D_r^1\). Classical differential derivative is \(D_0^1\), relative derivative is \(D_1^1\), logarithmic derivative is \(D_2^1\), and the hierarchy continues up to \(D_n^1\).

How to read the numeric examples

Rank Operator Engineering meaning Quick numeric approximation used here
0 \(D_0^1 y \approx \dfrac{\Delta y}{\Delta x}\) Absolute change per unit Plain slope
1 \(D_1^1 y \approx \dfrac{\Delta y / y}{\Delta x / x}\) Relative structural change (scale invariant) “% change of y” over “% change of x”
2 \(D_2^1 y \approx \dfrac{\Delta \ln\ln y}{\Delta \ln\ln x}\) (domains apply) Acceleration of structural change (hierarchical risk) Use it when the relative rate itself is changing fast

Note: \(D_2^1\) requires domain constraints (e.g., \(x>1\), \(y>1\)) in its strict form. In engineering practice, it is used as a rank-2 descriptor for “acceleration of relative change” once variables are mapped to a suitable positive domain (e.g., risk indices, normalized health metrics).

Applications and numeric examples (1–20)

Tip: For each case, read “What \(D_0^1\) says” vs “What \(D_1^1\) says” first. Then look at the rank-2 note: “When \(D_2^1\) matters”.

1) Concrete crack length growth

Crack length \(L\) increases from 20 mm to 22 mm in one inspection interval.

\[ \Delta L = 2 \text{ mm} \qquad D_0^1:\; \text{absolute growth} = 2 \text{ mm/interval} \] \[ D_1^1:\; \text{relative growth} \approx \frac{\Delta L}{L} = \frac{2}{20}=0.10=10\% \]

Engineering reading: 10% per interval is a stronger warning than “+2 mm”.

2) Acceleration of cracking (risk turning point)

If relative crack growth increased from 5%/interval to 10%/interval, the relative rate doubled.

\[ D_1^1\text{(rate)}:\; \frac{10\%}{5\%}=2 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \text{accelerating risk (rank-2 behavior)} \]

This is where \(D_2^1\) is conceptually the right level: it detects “growth of the growth-rate”.

3) Concrete creep strain

Creep strain \(\varepsilon\) increases from 400 με to 460 με over a fixed time window.

\[ D_0^1:\; \Delta \varepsilon = 60\ \mu\varepsilon \qquad D_1^1:\; \frac{60}{400}=15\% \]

If this 15% becomes 25% in the next window, risk acceleration suggests rank-2 monitoring.

4) Drying shrinkage (serviceability)

Shrinkage strain increases from 250 με to 300 με.

\[ D_0^1:\; 50\ \mu\varepsilon \qquad D_1^1:\; \frac{50}{250}=20\% \]

Relative change connects better to crack risk and restraint conditions than the absolute microstrain number.

5) Rebar corrosion loss (diameter reduction)

Bar diameter drops from 16.0 mm to 15.2 mm due to corrosion.

\[ D_0^1:\; \Delta d = -0.8\text{ mm} \qquad D_1^1:\; \frac{-0.8}{16}=-5\% \]

Engineering reading: 5% diameter loss is already serious because steel area scales with \(d^2\).

\[ \frac{\Delta A}{A} \approx 2\frac{\Delta d}{d} \approx -10\% \]

6) Foundation settlement (absolute vs relative)

Settlement \(S\) goes from 12 mm to 15 mm. If reference permissible is 25 mm.

\[ D_0^1:\; \Delta S = 3\text{ mm} \qquad D_1^1\text{ (vs current)}:\; \frac{3}{12}=25\% \] \[ \text{Relative to limit:}\; \frac{15}{25}=0.60 \]

Relative indicators (25% jump; 60% of limit) are more decision-relevant than “+3 mm”.

7) Differential settlement across span

Left support settles 8 mm; right support settles 14 mm. Span is 10 m.

\[ \Delta S = 14-8=6\text{ mm} \qquad \text{Slope} \approx \frac{6\text{ mm}}{10\text{ m}} = 0.6\text{ mm/m} \]

Track the relative growth of this distortion over time (rank 1), and its acceleration (rank 2) for early warning.

8) Consolidation progress (time-dependent)

Settlement progressed from 40% to 55% of final estimate.

\[ D_0^1:\; +15 \text{ percentage points} \qquad D_1^1:\; \frac{55-40}{40}=37.5\% \]

A rising relative progress rate may indicate unexpected drainage/softening mechanisms (rank-2 monitoring).

9) Slope stability factor of safety (FoS) degradation

FoS decreases from 1.35 to 1.20 after heavy rainfall.

\[ D_0^1:\; \Delta \text{FoS}=-0.15 \qquad D_1^1:\; \frac{-0.15}{1.35} \approx -11.1\% \]

Relative drop is a clearer alarm signal. If such drops become faster across events → rank-2 risk acceleration.

10) Bearing capacity margin

Demand load \(Q_d\) rises from 900 kN to 980 kN; capacity estimate \(Q_u=1200\) kN.

\[ D_0^1:\; +80\text{ kN} \qquad \text{Utilization}=\frac{980}{1200}=0.817 \] \[ D_1^1\text{ (utilization change)}:\; \frac{0.817-0.75}{0.75}\approx 8.9\% \]

Utilization is a natural positive domain for rank-2 descriptors if the utilization growth accelerates.

11) Pile load test: stiffness change

At 600 kN, settlement used to be 6 mm. Now it is 8 mm (same load level).

\[ D_0^1:\; \Delta s = +2\text{ mm} \qquad D_1^1:\; \frac{2}{6}\approx 33.3\% \]

A 33% relative stiffness loss is a strong sign of degradation or soil condition change.

12) Bridge deck condition index

Condition index drops from 82 to 74 (out of 100) in one year.

\[ D_0^1:\; -8 \text{ points/year} \qquad D_1^1:\; \frac{-8}{82}\approx -9.76\%/\text{year} \]

If the yearly relative drop increases over years (e.g., -5%, then -10%), rank-2 is the early-warning layer.

13) Fatigue damage accumulation (Miner’s rule-like)

Damage index \(D\) increases from 0.30 to 0.36 in a monitoring period.

\[ D_0^1:\; +0.06 \qquad D_1^1:\; \frac{0.06}{0.30}=20\% \]

Relative rise indicates how quickly you are approaching the critical threshold \(D\to1\).

14) Serviceability deflection growth

Midspan deflection increases from 18 mm to 21 mm under comparable conditions.

\[ D_0^1:\; +3\text{ mm} \qquad D_1^1:\; \frac{3}{18}=16.7\% \]

Relative deflection growth is often more meaningful than the absolute increment when comparing spans/structures.

15) Dynamic amplification (vibration response)

RMS acceleration increases from 0.08g to 0.11g after a change in support conditions.

\[ D_0^1:\; +0.03g \qquad D_1^1:\; \frac{0.03}{0.08}=37.5\% \]

If this relative increase itself accelerates across days/weeks → use rank-2 health index tracking.

16) Seismic drift ratio monitoring

Drift ratio demand rises from 0.9% to 1.2% after updating site hazard or retrofit changes.

\[ D_0^1:\; +0.3\% \text{ points} \qquad D_1^1:\; \frac{1.2-0.9}{0.9}=33.3\% \]

Relative rise is more interpretable for performance-based decision making.

17) Liquefaction-related index change (geotechnical risk index)

A normalized liquefaction risk index \(R\) increases from 1.10 to 1.40 (dimensionless, positive).

\[ D_0^1:\; +0.30 \qquad D_1^1:\; \frac{0.30}{1.10}\approx 27.3\% \]

Indices like \(R>1\) are suitable for rank-2 descriptors (log-log domain is naturally defined).

18) Scour depth at bridge piers

Scour depth increases from 0.80 m to 0.92 m after flood events.

\[ D_0^1:\; +0.12\text{ m} \qquad D_1^1:\; \frac{0.12}{0.80}=15\% \]

If seasonal relative increases rise (e.g., 10%, then 15%, then 25%), the “risk acceleration” layer is rank-2.

19) FEM convergence order (numerical civil engineering)

If discretization step \(h\) reduces from 0.20 to 0.10 and the error reduces from 0.040 to 0.010.

\[ \frac{h_2}{h_1}=\frac{0.10}{0.20}=0.5,\quad \frac{E_2}{E_1}=\frac{0.010}{0.040}=0.25 \] \[ D_1^1 E \approx \frac{\ln(E_2/E_1)}{\ln(h_2/h_1)}=\frac{\ln(0.25)}{\ln(0.5)}=2 \]

Rank-1 here returns the true engineering quantity: convergence order \(p=2\).

20) Cost growth (project control)

Project cost increases from 10.0M to 12.0M (same scope window). Compare absolute vs relative.

\[ D_0^1:\; +2.0\text{M} \qquad D_1^1:\; \frac{2.0}{10.0}=20\% \]

Relative growth is scale-aware. If the 20% becomes 35% in the next window, rank-2 “acceleration” becomes the alarm layer.

Practical decision guide: which rank should a civil engineer use?

Question you ask Recommended operator Typical use
“How many mm / kN / points changed?” \(D_0^1\) Field reporting, raw monitoring, short-term comparisons
“How large is the change relative to the current state?” \(D_1^1\) Health indices, scale-invariant comparisons, early warnings
“Is the relative change itself accelerating?” \(D_2^1\) Turning points, irreversible regimes, near-failure detection
“Are we in multi-phase cascading behavior?” \(D_n^1\) Complex systems, long-term asset management across regimes
Engineering takeaway: Many failures are not “big numbers” problems—they are accelerating structural change problems. That is exactly what hierarchical ranks are designed to capture.
