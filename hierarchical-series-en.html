Hierarchical Series

A conceptual extension associated with Hierarchical Calculus.

1) Why hierarchical series?

In classical analysis, Taylor series expand a function \(f(x)\) around a point \(x_0\) using its derivatives:

\[ f(x) \approx f(x_0) + f'(x_0)(x-x_0) + \frac{f''(x_0)}{2!}(x-x_0)^2 + \cdots \]

However, for functions exhibiting compound growth or multi-scale behavior, expanding \(f(x)\) itself may be inefficient. Hierarchical calculus suggests a natural alternative: expand the function after lifting it to a suitable hierarchical level, such as \(\ln f(x)\) or \(\ln\ln f(x)\), and then reconstruct.

2) Core idea

The essential idea is simple: perform approximation at the level where the structure becomes simpler.

Instead of approximating \(f(x)\) directly (level \(D_0\)), we may approximate a transformed function \(g(x)=T_k(f(x))\) at a higher hierarchical level \(D_k\), and then apply the inverse transformation.

3) Informal definition

Informal definition. Let \(f(x)\) be a positive function on a suitable domain. Define hierarchical transformations

\[ T_0(f)=f,\quad T_1(f)=\ln f,\quad T_2(f)=\ln(\ln f),\ \dots \]

Choose a level \(k\) and form a Taylor expansion of \(g(x)=T_k(f(x))\) around \(x_0\):

\[ g(x) \approx \sum_{n=0}^{N} \frac{g^{(n)}(x_0)}{n!}(x-x_0)^n \]

An approximation of \(f(x)\) is then obtained by applying the inverse transformation \(T_k^{-1}\).

4) Illustrative example

Consider the function \[ y(x)=\exp\!\big(e^x\big). \]

\[ \ln y(x)=e^x,\qquad \ln\ln y(x)=x. \]

At level \(D_2\), the transformed function is simply linear: \(g(x)=x\). Its Taylor expansion around \(x_0\) is exact, and reconstruction yields a highly accurate approximation of \(y(x)\).

5) How to use hierarchical series

  1. Identify whether the function exhibits multi-scale or compound growth.
  2. Select a hierarchical level \(k\) where the structure simplifies.
  3. Expand the transformed function \(T_k(f(x))\) via Taylor series.
  4. Reconstruct the approximation using \(T_k^{-1}\).
  5. Verify domain conditions (positivity, logarithmic constraints).

6) Domain and remarks

  • Level \(D_1\) requires \(f(x)>0\).
  • Level \(D_2\) requires \(\ln f(x)>0\).
  • Hierarchical series are not meant to replace Taylor series universally.
  • They are most effective when the hierarchical transformation simplifies the functional structure.

7) Future directions

Possible future developments include:

  • Rigorous convergence analysis of hierarchical series.
  • Error estimates after inverse hierarchical reconstruction.
  • Numerical comparisons with Taylor and Padé approximations.
  • Applications to differential equations that linearize at higher hierarchical levels.
