Chapter 24 – Applications in Medicine

Medical processes span a wide range of time scales: fast drug concentrations in blood, intermediate disease progression, and ultra–slow changes in risk factors or resistance. Hierarchical Calculus provides a language to separate absolute dynamics (\(D_0\)), relative dynamics (\(D_1\)) and ultra–slow changes (\(D_2\)) in a unified framework.

In this chapter we discuss:

  • tumour growth models,
  • drug dosing and relative concentration changes,
  • chronic disease progression and ultra–slow risk drift.

24.1 Tumour Growth: From Exponential to Relative Models

A simple model of tumour volume \(V(t)\) treats growth as exponential:

\[ D_0 V = r V, \]

with growth rate \(r\). The solution:

\[ V(t) = V_0 e^{r(t-t_0)} \]

is often accurate only for early stages. In later stages, growth may follow a power law in time, or depend on the tumour radius rather than its volume. Hierarchical derivatives help express these scaling regimes.

Example 24.1 – Power-Law Tumour Growth

Suppose empirical data fit a power law:

\[ V(t) \approx C t^{\alpha}. \]

Then:

\[ D_1^{(t)} V(t) = \frac{t}{V}\frac{dV}{dt} = \alpha. \]

The tumour obeys a scale–invariant law where each decade in time multiplies the volume by the same factor. The equation

\[ D_1^{(t)} V = \alpha \]

is a compact hierarchical model for such growth.

24.2 Relative Growth of Biomarkers

Many medical quantities are monitored as relative changes: fold–increase in tumour markers, percentage drop in viral load, etc. For a biomarker \(B(t)\), the relative derivative with respect to time is:

\[ D_1^{(t)} B = \frac{t}{B}\frac{dB}{dt}. \]

If \(D_1^{(t)} B\) is approximately constant over a range of \(t\), then \(B\) behaves like a power of time. Changes in \(D_1^{(t)} B\) can reveal the effect of therapy or the onset of resistance.

Example 24.2 – Monitoring Treatment Response

Let \(B(t)\) be a tumour marker level under treatment. If

\[ D_1^{(t)} B(t) \approx -\beta \]

for some \(\beta>0\), then

\[ B(t) \approx B_0 \left(\frac{t}{t_0}\right)^{-\beta}. \]

Every factor of 10 in time reduces the marker by a factor \(10^{\beta}\). Deviations of \(D_1^{(t)} B\) from \(-\beta\) may indicate treatment failure or a new regime of response.

24.3 Pharmacokinetics: Relative Concentration Profiles

In pharmacokinetics, the concentration \(C(t)\) of a drug is often modelled by exponential decay or sums of exponentials. Over longer periods, relative changes can be more informative than absolute ones, especially when comparing doses or routes of administration.

The relative derivative of \(C(t)\) with respect to time is:

\[ D_1^{(t)} C = \frac{t}{C}\frac{dC}{dt}. \]

For an exponential decay \(C(t) = C_0 e^{-kt}\), one finds:

\[ D_1^{(t)} C(t) = -k t. \]

Thus \(|D_1^{(t)} C|\) grows linearly with \(t\), distinguishing exponential behaviour from power–law pharmacokinetics where \(D_1^{(t)} C\) would be constant.

Example 24.3 – Distinguishing Exponential vs Power-Law Clearance

Suppose long-term drug measurements show that \(\ln C\) is nearly linear in \(\ln t\). This suggests

\[ C(t) \approx Ct^{-\gamma}, \]

which has constant \(D_1^{(t)} C = -\gamma\). Hierarchical analysis using \(D_1\) therefore helps detect non-exponential “heavy tail” clearance, with implications for dosing intervals and toxicity.

24.4 Chronic Disease Progression and D₂

Many chronic conditions—such as atherosclerosis, neurodegeneration or chronic kidney disease—progress extremely slowly. Changes are often visible only over years or decades. This is a natural domain for the level–2 derivative \(D_2\), which captures ultra–slow logarithmic evolution.

Let \(R(t)\) be a risk score or structural damage index. The level–2 derivative is:

\[ D_2^{(t)} R = \frac{t\ln t}{R\,\ln R}\,\frac{dR}{dt}. \]

A simple hierarchical model of ultra–slow progression is:

\[ D_2^{(t)} R(t) = \varepsilon, \]

with small constant \(\varepsilon\).

Example 24.4 – Long-Term Risk Accumulation

Consider a lifetime cardiovascular risk index \(R(t)\) that accumulates slowly due to lifestyle and age. If

\[ \ln(\ln R(t)) \]

is approximately linear in \(\ln(\ln t)\), then \(D_2^{(t)} R\) is nearly constant. This representation separates the ultra–slow, almost invisible drift (level 2) from faster fluctuations (levels 0 and 1).

24.5 Multi-Level Time Scales in Medicine

Medical phenomena can be roughly organised as:

  • Level 0 (D₀): minutes to days – drug kinetics, acute responses, vital sign changes.
  • Level 1 (D₁): weeks to years – disease progression, tumour growth, average biomarker trends.
  • Level 2 (D₂): years to decades – ageing, cumulative risk, emergence of resistance, chronic damage.

Hierarchical differential equations can couple these levels, for example by letting a slowly changing parameter at level 2 modulate relative dynamics at level 1, which in turn drive fast processes at level 0.

24.6 Conceptual Advantages in Medical Modelling

Using hierarchical derivatives in medicine offers:

  • A clear mathematical distinction between fast, relative and ultra–slow processes.
  • More flexible growth and decay models than pure exponentials.
  • A formal way to encode long-term trends and adaptation in disease and therapy.
  • Tools to interpret log–log plots and double–log plots that are common in biomedical data analysis.

24.7 Summary of Chapter 24

In this chapter we:

  • Modelled tumour growth using \(D_0\) and \(D_1\) to distinguish exponential and power–law regimes.
  • Applied \(D_1\) to treatment monitoring and biomarker dynamics.
  • Discussed pharmacokinetics in terms of relative derivatives.
  • Introduced \(D_2\) for chronic disease progression and ultra–slow risk accumulation.
  • Organised medical time scales into levels corresponding to \(D_0, D_1, D_2\).

These applications show that Hierarchical Calculus can structure medical models across many time scales, from acute care to long-term chronic evolution.
