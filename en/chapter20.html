Chapter 20 – Applications in Physics

Physics provides many examples of processes that evolve on multiple time and length scales. Classical calculus, based on the single derivative \(D_0\), captures absolute rates of change. Hierarchical Calculus adds the relative and logarithmic derivatives \(D_1, D_2, \dots\), which are well suited for phenomena with scale–invariance, power–law dynamics and ultra–slow evolution.

In this chapter we present several illustrative applications:

  • relative motion and power–law damping,
  • ultra–slow relaxation on logarithmic time scales,
  • multi–scale transport where different levels dominate different regimes.

20.1 Relative Motion and D₁

Consider a quantity \(x(t)\) (position, radius, energy…) evolving in time. The classical velocity is:

\[ D_0 x = \frac{dx}{dt}. \]

The level–1 derivative of \(x\) with respect to time is:

\[ D_1 x = \frac{t}{x}\,\frac{dx}{dt}. \]

This quantity measures how fast \(x\) changes per unit of relative time and relative state: that is, the derivative of \(\ln x\) with respect to \(\ln t\). Constant \(D_1 x\) corresponds to a power–law relation between \(x\) and \(t\).

Example 20.1 – Power-Law Expansion

Suppose a radius \(R(t)\) of a spherical shock grows according to

\[ D_1 R(t) = k, \]

where \(k\) is a constant. Solving this equation gives:

\[ R(t) = R_0 \left(\frac{t}{t_0}\right)^k, \]

which is the standard power–law law often observed in blast waves and self–similar fluid flows. Here the equation is written directly in terms of the relative derivative \(D_1\), making the scale–invariance explicit.

20.2 Power-Law Damping and Energy Loss

In many dissipative systems the energy \(E(t)\) decays not exponentially but as a power law. A simple hierarchical model is:

\[ D_1 E(t) = -\alpha, \]

with constant \(\alpha > 0\). This equation reads:

\[ \frac{t}{E}\frac{dE}{dt} = -\alpha, \]

and has the solution:

\[ E(t) = E_0 \left(\frac{t}{t_0}\right)^{-\alpha}. \]

This describes a scale-free decay where no characteristic time scale appears; every decade in time brings the same relative loss of energy. Classical exponential models cannot capture this behaviour naturally, while the \(D_1\) formulation does.

Example 20.2 – Long-Time Tail in a Relaxation Process

Suppose a material relaxes after a sudden deformation. At short times the stress may decay exponentially, but at long times experiments show a power–law “tail”. Modelling the tail using

\[ D_1 \sigma(t) = -\beta \]

gives

\[ \sigma(t) = \sigma_\infty + C \left(\frac{t}{t_0}\right)^{-\beta}, \]

where \(\sigma_\infty\) is a residual stress. The hierarchical derivative \(D_1\) thus offers a compact way to encode power–law relaxation.

20.3 Ultra-Slow Relaxation and D₂

Some physical processes are even slower than power laws. Their evolution depends on \(\ln t\) or \(\ln(\ln t)\). Examples include:

  • glassy relaxation and ageing,
  • creep of materials over astronomical times,
  • certain diffusion processes in disordered media.

The level–2 derivative with respect to time is:

\[ D_2 x(t) = \frac{t \ln t}{x(t)\,\ln x(t)}\,\frac{dx}{dt}. \]

Constant \(D_2 x\) corresponds to a relation where \(\ln(\ln x)\) is linear in \(\ln(\ln t)\). This is suitable for ultra–slow growth or decay.

Example 20.3 – Double-Logarithmic Growth

Consider a domain size \(L(t)\) growing according to:

\[ D_2 L(t) = \gamma, \]

with constant \(\gamma>0\). Then

\[ \ln(\ln L(t)) = \ln(\ln L_0) + \gamma \,\ln\!\left( \frac{\ln t}{\ln t_0} \right), \]

so

\[ L(t) = \exp\!\left( \exp\!\left( \ln(\ln L_0) + \gamma\, \ln\!\left( \frac{\ln t}{\ln t_0} \right) \right) \right). \]

This growth is extraordinarily slow: even huge changes in \(t\) produce only modest changes in \(L\). Such models can describe systems where rearrangements become rarer and rarer over time.

20.4 Hierarchical Time Scales

Many physical systems exhibit several distinct regimes:

  • an initial fast transient,
  • a power–law intermediate regime,
  • an ultra–slow asymptotic regime.

Hierarchical Calculus provides a natural language for these regimes:

  • \(D_0\)-models for fast, exponential-like dynamics,
  • \(D_1\)-models for scale-free, power-law behaviour,
  • \(D_2\)-models for logarithmically slow evolution.

A complete model might switch from one level to another as time grows, or combine them in a single hierarchical differential equation with terms involving \(D_0, D_1\) and \(D_2\).

Example 20.4 – Three-Regime Relaxation Model

Let \(X(t)\) denote an observable (stress, magnetization, …). A simple hierarchical model is:

\[ D_0 X = -a_0 X \quad (t \ll T_1), \] \[ D_1 X = -a_1 \quad (T_1 \ll t \ll T_2), \] \[ D_2 X = -a_2 \quad (t \gg T_2), \]

where \(T_1, T_2\) mark crossovers between regimes. Each level has an analytic solution (exponential, power law, ultra–slow), and matching conditions at \(T_1, T_2\) fix the constants. This illustrates how hierarchical derivatives describe multi–regime dynamics in a unified way.

20.5 Spatial Scaling and Fields

Hierarchical derivatives can be applied not only to time but also to spatial variables. For a field \(\phi(r)\) depending on a radius \(r\), we can define:

\[ D_1^{(r)} \phi = \frac{r}{\phi}\,\frac{d\phi}{dr}, \qquad D_2^{(r)} \phi = \frac{r\ln r}{\phi\,\ln\phi}\,\frac{d\phi}{dr}, \]

where the superscript \((r)\) indicates that the derivatives act with respect to \(r\). These operators can model radial profiles with power–law or ultra–slow variations, such as:

  • density profiles in astrophysics,
  • correlation functions decaying as powers of distance,
  • screening lengths growing slowly with system size.

20.6 Conceptual Role in Physics

The main ideas of this chapter can be summarized as follows:

  • \(D_0\) describes fast, absolute rates of change.
  • \(D_1\) captures scale–invariant power laws and relative dynamics.
  • \(D_2\) captures double–logarithmic, ultra–slow processes.
  • Physical systems often move from \(D_0\)-dominated regimes to \(D_1\)- or \(D_2\)-dominated regimes at long times or large scales.

Hierarchical Calculus therefore acts as a bridge between classical differential equations and the complex, multi–scale behavior observed in real physical systems.

20.7 Summary of Chapter 20

In this chapter we:

  • Introduced physical interpretations of \(D_1\) and \(D_2\) in terms of relative and logarithmic time scales.
  • Modelled power–law expansion and damping using \(D_1\).
  • Modelled ultra–slow, double–logarithmic growth using \(D_2\).
  • Outlined multi–regime relaxation combining \(D_0, D_1\) and \(D_2\).
  • Discussed hierarchical derivatives with respect to spatial variables.

These examples show how Hierarchical Calculus can be embedded into physical modelling. In the next chapters we will explore similar ideas in cosmology, biology, economics and other fields, where multi–scale dynamics and ultra–slow evolution are equally important.
