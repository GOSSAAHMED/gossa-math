Chapter 19 – Comparison with Classical Taylor Series

Hierarchical series extend the classical Taylor expansion by incorporating multiple scales of change through the derivatives \(D_0, D_1, D_2, \dots\). In this chapter we compare hierarchical series with the classical Taylor series, highlight their similarities and differences, and indicate when each type of expansion is most appropriate.

19.1 Classical Taylor Series: A Short Reminder

Let \(f\) be sufficiently smooth near a point \(x_0\). The classical Taylor series is:

\[ f(x) = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{D_0^k f(x_0)}{k!}\,(x-x_0)^k, \]

where \(D_0^k f\) is the \(k\)-th ordinary derivative. The expansion uses the absolute coordinate \((x-x_0)\) and approximates \(f\) by a polynomial in this coordinate.

Classical Taylor series work extremely well when:

  • the function is analytic in a neighbourhood of \(x_0\),
  • the important behaviour is captured by absolute change in \(x\),
  • no large scale variations in powers or logarithms of \(x\) occur.

19.2 Hierarchical Series: A New Family

Hierarchical series use coordinates and coefficients adapted to the derivatives \(D_1, D_2,\dots\). The first levels are:

  • Relative series (level 1):
    Coordinate: \(\displaystyle u_1 = \ln\!\left(\frac{x}{x_0}\right)\). Expansion of: \(\ln f(x)\). Coefficients: derivatives of \(\ln f\) with respect to \(\ln x\), linked to \(D_1 f\).
  • Logarithmic series (level 2):
    Coordinate: \(\displaystyle u_2 = \ln(\ln x) - \ln(\ln x_0)\). Expansion of: \(\ln(\ln f(x))\). Coefficients: derivatives of \(\ln(\ln f)\) with respect to \(\ln(\ln x)\), linked to \(D_2 f\).

The general hierarchical series combines these levels and higher ones to approximate functions whose behaviour is governed by multiple scales simultaneously.

19.3 A Conceptual Comparison

Aspect Classical Taylor (Level 0) Hierarchical Series (Levels ≥ 1)
Local coordinate \(x-x_0\) \(\ln(x/x_0)\), \(\ln(\ln x)-\ln(\ln x_0)\), …
Derivative used Repeated \(D_0\) Derived from \(D_1, D_2,\dots\) and their iterates
Best for Analytic functions with absolute scaling Power laws, ultra–slow growth, multi–scale behaviour
Typical term \(\frac{D_0^k f(x_0)}{k!}(x-x_0)^k\) Expressions in powers of \(\ln(x/x_0)\) or \(\ln(\ln x)-\ln(\ln x_0)\)
Geometry Linear space around \(x_0\) Curved space of logarithmic scales

19.4 Example: Power Law Function

Consider the function

\[ f(x) = x^k, \]

with base point \(x_0 > 0\).

19.4.1 Classical Taylor Expansion

The derivatives are:

\[ D_0^n f(x) = k(k-1)\cdots(k-n+1)\,x^{k-n}. \]

The Taylor series at \(x_0\) is:

\[ f(x) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{k(k-1)\cdots(k-n+1)}{n!} x_0^{k-n}\,(x-x_0)^n. \]

This expression is correct but not very transparent: all powers of \((x-x_0)\) appear, and the power–law structure is not obvious.

19.4.2 Relative Series Expansion

Using the coordinate \(u = \ln(x/x_0)\), we have:

\[ f(x) = x^k = x_0^k e^{k u}. \]

Expanding the exponential:

\[ e^{k u} = \sum_{m=0}^{\infty} \frac{k^m}{m!}\,u^m. \]

Hence the relative series is:

\[ f(x) = x_0^k \sum_{m=0}^{\infty} \frac{k^m}{m!} \bigl(\ln(x/x_0)\bigr)^m. \]

Here the coefficients depend only on the exponent \(k\), and the structure of the power law is completely explicit. For many purposes, this expansion is more natural than the classical Taylor series.

19.5 Example: Ultra-Slow Growth

Consider the function

\[ f(x) = \exp\!\bigl(A (\ln x)^2\bigr), \]

with \(A>0\). This function grows much faster than any power of \(x\), but its growth is controlled by \(\ln x\).

19.5.1 Classical Taylor View

Expanding \(f(x)\) in powers of \((x-x_0)\) produces extremely complicated coefficients involving chained derivatives of \(\ln x\). The resulting Taylor series does not reveal the simple structure \(\exp(A (\ln x)^2)\).

19.5.2 Logarithmic Series View

Define \(v = \ln(\ln x)\) and observe that:

\[ \ln f(x) = A (\ln x)^2, \qquad \ln(\ln f(x)) = \ln A + 2 \ln(\ln x). \]

Thus:

\[ \ln(\ln f(x)) = \ln(\ln f(x_0)) + 2\Bigl(\ln(\ln x) - \ln(\ln x_0)\Bigr), \]

so the logarithmic Taylor series around \(x_0\) truncates after the first order term: the function is exactly linear in \(v\). The level–2 derivative satisfies

\[ D_2 f(x) = 2, \]

showing that \(f\) is a “perfect” example of a logarithmic series of order 1.

19.6 Convergence and Domains of Validity

Classical Taylor series and hierarchical series are both local approximations, but their natural domains of validity differ:

  • Taylor series converge in a disk \(|x-x_0| < R\) in the complex plane, limited by the nearest singularity of \(f\).
  • Relative and logarithmic series converge in regions where \(|\ln(x/x_0)|\) or \(|\ln(\ln x) - \ln(\ln x_0)|\) are small. This can correspond to very large ranges of \(x\) when \(x\) grows exponentially or double–exponentially.

In practice this means:

  • For moderate \(x\), classical Taylor often suffices.
  • For very large or very small \(x\), hierarchical series may give more stable approximations because they track the function on logarithmic scales.

19.7 When to Use Which Expansion?

The choice between classical and hierarchical series depends on the structure of the problem:

  • Use Taylor series when:
    • the function is analytic and well-behaved near \(x_0\),
    • dominant behaviour is polynomial or exponential in \(x\),
    • no strong scale invariance or logarithmic effects are present.
  • Use relative series when:
    • the function is close to a power law,
    • data is naturally expressed in percentages or ratios,
    • you are modelling scale-invariant systems.
  • Use logarithmic series when:
    • growth is ultra–slow and driven by \(\ln x\) or \(\ln(\ln x)\),
    • classical derivatives are almost zero over wide ranges,
    • you need asymptotics over extremely large scales of \(x\).

19.8 Unified View

We can think of classical Taylor and hierarchical series as different projections of the same underlying function onto different coordinate systems:

  • Level 0: linear coordinate in \(x\),
  • Level 1: logarithmic coordinate in \(x\),
  • Level 2: logarithmic coordinate in \(\ln x\),
  • Higher levels: deeper nested logarithms.

Each projection reveals different aspects of the function, and together they provide a rich multi–scale description. Hierarchical Calculus therefore does not replace classical Taylor theory, but extends and complements it.

19.9 Summary of Chapter 19

In this chapter we:

  • Reviewed the classical Taylor series as a level–0 expansion.
  • Compared it with relative and logarithmic series built from \(D_1\) and \(D_2\).
  • Showed that power laws are naturally captured by relative series, while ultra–slow laws such as \(\exp((\ln x)^2)\) are captured by logarithmic series.
  • Discussed the different domains of convergence and when each expansion is most useful.
  • Presented a unified picture where all series correspond to different coordinates on the same function.

Classical Taylor series remain central for local analytic behaviour, while hierarchical series provide powerful tools for multi–scale and scale–invariant phenomena. Together, they form a complete toolbox for describing change across all levels.
