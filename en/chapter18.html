Chapter 18 – Logarithmic Series and Ultra-Slow Growth

In Chapter 17 we developed relative series expansions based on the derivative \(D_1\) and the coordinate \(\ln(x/x_0)\). In this chapter we move one level higher and consider series adapted to the logarithmic derivative \(D_2\), which naturally describes ultra–slow growth controlled by \(\ln(\ln x)\).

18.1 The Logarithmic Level and D₂

Recall that the level–2 derivative is defined by

\[ D_2 f(x) = \frac{x \ln x}{f(x)\,\ln f(x)} \frac{df}{dx}. \]

This operator compares the logarithmic rate of change of \(f\) to the logarithmic rate of change of \(x\), and is closely related to derivatives with respect to \(\ln(\ln x)\). To see this, set:

\[ u = \ln x,\quad v = \ln u = \ln(\ln x). \]

Then:

\[ \frac{dv}{dx} = \frac{1}{u} \frac{du}{dx} = \frac{1}{\ln x} \frac{1}{x} = \frac{1}{x\ln x}. \]

Similarly, let:

\[ w = \ln f(x), \]

so that:

\[ \frac{dw}{dx} = \frac{1}{f} \frac{df}{dx}. \]

Then:

\[ \frac{d}{dx}\bigl(\ln w\bigr) = \frac{1}{w}\frac{dw}{dx} = \frac{1}{\ln f(x)}\frac{1}{f(x)}\frac{df}{dx}. \]

Comparing with the expression of \(D_2 f\), we see that:

\[ D_2 f(x) = \frac{d}{dv}\bigl(\ln(\ln f(x))\bigr), \]

i.e. \(D_2 f\) is the derivative of \(\ln(\ln f)\) with respect to \(\ln(\ln x)\). This is the natural setting for logarithmic series.

18.2 Logarithmic Coordinate Around a Base Point

At level 1 we used the relative coordinate:

\[ u_1 = \ln\!\left(\frac{x}{x_0}\right) = \ln x - \ln x_0. \]

At level 2 we use a logarithmic relative coordinate:

\[ u_2(x) = \ln\!\left( \frac{\ln x}{\ln x_0} \right) = \ln(\ln x) - \ln(\ln x_0). \]

When \(\ln x\) changes slowly, the difference \(\ln(\ln x) - \ln(\ln x_0)\) is even smaller, making \(u_2\) an excellent local coordinate for extremely slow dynamics.

18.3 First–Order Logarithmic Approximation

As with relative series, we begin by expanding a transformed version of \(f\). At level 2 the natural object is:

\[ g(x) = \ln(\ln f(x)). \]

Using the variable:

\[ v = \ln(\ln x), \]

we can think of \(g\) as a function of \(v\):

\[ g = g(v). \]

Then:

\[ \frac{d g}{dv} = D_2 f, \]

by the relation above. Around a base point \(x_0\) (with \(v_0 = \ln(\ln x_0)\)), a first–order Taylor approximation in \(v\) is:

\[ g(v) \approx g(v_0) + \left.\frac{dg}{dv}\right|_{v_0}(v-v_0). \]

Translating back to \(x\):

\[ \ln(\ln f(x)) \approx \ln(\ln f(x_0)) + D_2 f(x_0)\,\bigl(\ln(\ln x) - \ln(\ln x_0)\bigr). \]

Exponentiating twice:

\[ f(x) \approx \exp\!\left( \exp\!\left( \ln(\ln f(x_0)) + D_2 f(x_0)\, \ln\!\left(\frac{\ln x}{\ln x_0}\right) \right) \right). \]

Simplifying the inner expression:

\[ \exp\!\left( \ln(\ln f(x_0)) + D_2 f(x_0)\,\ln\!\left(\frac{\ln x}{\ln x_0}\right) \right) = \ln f(x_0) \left(\frac{\ln x}{\ln x_0}\right)^{D_2 f(x_0)}. \]

Thus:

\[ f(x) \approx \exp\!\left( \ln f(x_0) \left(\frac{\ln x}{\ln x_0}\right)^{D_2 f(x_0)} \right). \]

This is the simplest logarithmic series approximation, describing ultra–slow growth controlled by \(D_2 f(x_0)\).

Example 18.1 – Ultra-Slow Law

Consider:

\[ f(x) = \exp\!\bigl(A (\ln x)^k\bigr), \]

for some constants \(A,k > 0\). Then \(\ln f(x) = A (\ln x)^k\) and \(\ln(\ln f(x)) = \ln A + k \ln(\ln x)\). Therefore:

\[ D_2 f(x) = \frac{d}{dv}\bigl(\ln(\ln f(x))\bigr) = k, \]

a constant. The first–order logarithmic approximation with base point \(x_0\) is then exact:

\[ \ln(\ln f(x)) = \ln(\ln f(x_0)) + k\, \ln\!\left(\frac{\ln x}{\ln x_0}\right), \]

which reproduces precisely \(\ln(\ln f(x)) = \ln A + k \ln(\ln x)\).

18.4 Logarithmic Taylor Series for ln ln f

To construct a full series, we expand \(g(v) = \ln(\ln f)\) as a Taylor series in the variable \(v = \ln(\ln x)\) around \(v_0 = \ln(\ln x_0)\):

\[ g(v) = \sum_{m=0}^{\infty} \frac{1}{m!} g^{(m)}(v_0)\,(v-v_0)^m. \]

The first derivative is:

\[ g'(v) = \frac{dg}{dv} = D_2 f. \]

Higher derivatives can be written schematically as:

\[ g^{(m)}(v_0) = \left.\frac{d^{m}}{dv^{m}} \ln(\ln f(x(v))) \right|_{v_0}. \]

Denote these coefficients by:

\[ L_m(x_0) := g^{(m)}(v_0). \]

Then:

\[ \ln(\ln f(x)) = \sum_{m=0}^{\infty} \frac{L_m(x_0)}{m!} \bigl(\ln(\ln x) - \ln(\ln x_0)\bigr)^m, \] \[ f(x) = \exp\!\left( \exp\!\left( \sum_{m=0}^{\infty} \frac{L_m(x_0)}{m!} \bigl(\ln(\ln x) - \ln(\ln x_0)\bigr)^m \right) \right). \]

This is a logarithmic series expansion of \(f\) around \(x_0\) at level 2.

18.5 Relation to D₂ and Ultra-Slow Growth

Just as relative series are controlled by \(D_1 f\) and its derivatives, logarithmic series are controlled by \(D_2 f\):

  • The leading term \(L_1(x_0)\) is simply \(D_2 f(x_0)\).
  • Higher coefficients involve higher derivatives of \(\ln(\ln f)\) with respect to \(\ln(\ln x)\), generalizing \(D_2\).
  • When \(D_2 f\) is small, the function changes extremely slowly, even on logarithmic scales—this is ultra–slow growth.

In such regimes, a very small number of logarithmic series terms may be enough to approximate \(f\) over huge ranges of \(x\).

18.6 Comparison: Absolute, Relative, and Logarithmic Series

We can now summarize the first three levels of series expansions:

  • Level 0 – Absolute (Taylor) Coordinate: \((x-x_0)\) Expansion of: \(f(x)\) Coefficients: \(D_0^k f(x_0)\) Good for: smooth functions dominated by absolute change.
  • Level 1 – Relative Coordinate: \(\ln(x/x_0)\) Expansion of: \(\ln f(x)\) Coefficients: derivatives of \(\ln f\) w.r.t. \(\ln x\) (linked to \(D_1\)) Good for: power laws, multiplicative and scale–invariant behavior.
  • Level 2 – Logarithmic Coordinate: \(\ln(\ln x) - \ln(\ln x_0)\) Expansion of: \(\ln(\ln f(x))\) Coefficients: derivatives of \(\ln(\ln f)\) w.r.t. \(\ln(\ln x)\) (linked to \(D_2\)) Good for: ultra–slow growth, functions like \(\exp((\ln x)^k)\).

Higher hierarchical levels (\(D_3, D_4, \dots\)) would continue this pattern, using deeper nested logarithms.

18.7 Applications and Interpretation

Logarithmic series and ultra–slow growth appear in contexts such as:

  • extreme–time asymptotics in physics (e.g. very slow relaxation),
  • complexity theory and algorithmic growth beyond polynomial time,
  • deep learning or adaptation processes where progress slows double–logarithmically,
  • any system where \(\ln(\ln x)\) changes noticeably while \(x\) grows astronomically.

Hierarchical Calculus provides a natural language for describing and approximating such behaviors.

18.8 Summary of Chapter 18

In this chapter we:

  • Linked the logarithmic derivative \(D_2 f\) to derivatives of \(\ln(\ln f)\) with respect to \(\ln(\ln x)\).
  • Introduced the logarithmic coordinate \(u_2(x) = \ln(\ln x) - \ln(\ln x_0)\).
  • Derived a first–order logarithmic approximation for ultra–slow growth.
  • Defined a full logarithmic Taylor series for \(\ln(\ln f)\) and thereby for \(f\).
  • Compared absolute, relative and logarithmic series as the first three levels of hierarchical series.
  • Discussed applications in ultra–slow and multi–scale dynamics.

Logarithmic series represent the next step in the hierarchy of expansions. In further chapters, hierarchical series can be combined across levels, or used to construct approximate solutions of hierarchical differential equations.
