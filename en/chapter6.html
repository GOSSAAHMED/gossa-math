Chapter 6 – The Relative Derivative D1

The relative derivative D1 is the first genuinely new object introduced by Hierarchical Calculus. While the differential derivative \(D_0\) measures absolute change, \(D_1\) measures change relative to the current state of the system.

In this chapter we define \(D_1\), study its meaning, establish its rules, and show how it connects to growth, scaling and the Golden Relation.

6.1 Definition of the Relative Derivative

Let \(y = f(x)\). The relative derivative \(D_1 y\) is defined by:

\[ D_1 y = \frac{dy \cdot x}{dx \cdot y}. \]

Equivalently, in terms of the classical derivative:

\[ D_1 y = \frac{x}{y}\, D_0 y \quad\text{where}\quad D_0 y = \frac{dy}{dx}. \]

Thus \(D_1\) is a rescaled version of \(D_0\). The factors \(\frac{x}{y}\) express that we are not only interested in how much \(y\) changes, but how that change compares to the size of \(y\) itself.

6.2 Interpretation: Relative Change per Relative Step

Consider a small change \(\Delta x\) in the input and the corresponding change \(\Delta y\) in the output. Then:

  • \(\dfrac{\Delta y}{y}\) measures the relative change in \(y\),
  • \(\dfrac{\Delta x}{x}\) measures the relative change in \(x\).

Informally, the relative derivative compares these two:

\[ D_1 y \approx \frac{\dfrac{\Delta y}{y}}{\dfrac{\Delta x}{x}} \quad\text{as}\quad \Delta x \to 0. \]

In other words:

\(D_1 y\) measures the relative change of \(y\) per relative change of \(x\).

This makes \(D_1\) the natural derivative for phenomena where proportions, percentages or ratios are more meaningful than absolute differences.

6.3 The Golden Relation Revisited

The Golden Relation is:

\[ x\,D_0(y) = y\,D_1(y). \]

Substituting the definition of \(D_1\),

\[ y\,D_1(y) = y \cdot \frac{x}{y} D_0 y = x \, D_0 y, \]

which confirms the identity. Conceptually, this equation states that absolute and relative change are tightly linked. Knowing \(D_0\) and the ratio \(x/y\) is enough to compute \(D_1\), and vice versa.

6.4 Algebraic Rules for D1

Because \(D_1\) is built from \(D_0\), it inherits many of its structural properties. However, the presence of the factor \(\frac{x}{y}\) makes the formulas richer and more informative.

6.4.1 Linearity Does Not Hold in the Same Way

For \(D_0\), we have:

\[ D_0(a u + b v) = a D_0 u + b D_0 v. \]

For \(D_1\), the situation is more subtle because \(y\) appears in the denominator. For \(y = a u + b v\),

\[ D_1 y = \frac{x}{y} D_0 y = \frac{x}{a u + b v} \big(a D_0 u + b D_0 v\big). \]

So we do not get simple linearity; instead we get a weighted combination of derivatives normalized by the current value of \(y\). This is exactly what we expect from a relative concept.

6.4.2 Product Rule for Relative Derivatives

Suppose \(y = u \cdot v\). Then

\[ D_0 y = u\,D_0 v + v\,D_0 u. \]

Using the definition of \(D_1\),

\[ D_1 y = \frac{x}{y} D_0 y = \frac{x}{u v} (u\,D_0 v + v\,D_0 u). \]

We now express \(D_0 u\) and \(D_0 v\) in terms of their relative derivatives:

\[ D_1 u = \frac{x}{u} D_0 u \quad\Rightarrow\quad D_0 u = \frac{u}{x} D_1 u, \] \[ D_1 v = \frac{x}{v} D_0 v \quad\Rightarrow\quad D_0 v = \frac{v}{x} D_1 v. \]

Substituting:

\[ D_1 y = \frac{x}{u v} \left( u \cdot \frac{v}{x} D_1 v + v \cdot \frac{u}{x} D_1 u \right) = \frac{x}{u v} \cdot \frac{u v}{x} (D_1 u + D_1 v) = D_1 u + D_1 v. \]

Therefore, for products:

\[ D_1(u v) = D_1 u + D_1 v. \]

This is a clean and elegant rule: the relative derivative of a product is the sum of the relative derivatives. This mirrors the familiar rule for logarithms:

\[ \ln(u v) = \ln u + \ln v. \]

This connection between \(D_1\) and logarithms will become even more important in higher levels of the hierarchy.

6.5 D1 and Growth Models

Many natural processes are better described by relative rather than absolute change. Examples include:

  • Population growth: rate proportional to population size.
  • Compound interest in finance.
  • Biological growth and decay processes.
  • Learning curves where improvement depends on current knowledge.

A typical differential equation for proportional growth is:

\[ D_0 y = k y, \]

where \(k\) is a constant. The relative derivative here becomes:

\[ D_1 y = \frac{x}{y} D_0 y = \frac{x}{y} \cdot k y = k x. \]

So while \(D_0 y\) is proportional to \(y\), the relative derivative \(D_1 y\) grows linearly with \(x\). This reveals how the same growth law looks different when viewed from absolute vs. relative perspectives.

6.6 Relationship to D0 and D2

The derivative \(D_1\) sits between:

  • \(D_0\): absolute change,
  • \(D_2\): logarithmic change.

In general, hierarchical derivatives are given by:

\[ D_n y = \frac{L_n(x)}{L_n(y)} D_0 y, \]

where \(L_0(x) = x\), \(L_1(x) = \ln x\), \(L_2(x) = \ln(\ln x)\), etc.

For \(n = 1\),

\[ D_1 y = \frac{L_0(x)}{L_0(y)} D_0 y = \frac{x}{y} D_0 y, \]

confirming the definition. For \(n = 2\), we obtain:

\[ D_2 y = \frac{x \ln x}{y \ln y} D_0 y, \]

which can be seen as a “relative derivative of logarithmic scale”. Thus \(D_1\) is the natural bridge from the absolute world of \(D_0\) to the logarithmic world of \(D_2\).

6.7 Examples of the Relative Derivative

6.7.1 Power Functions

Let \(y = x^k\) for a constant \(k\). Then

\[ D_0 y = k x^{k-1}. \]

The relative derivative is:

\[ D_1 y = \frac{x}{y} D_0 y = \frac{x}{x^k} \cdot k x^{k-1} = k. \]

So for power laws, \(D_1 y\) is a constant, equal to the exponent. This shows that \(D_1\) extracts the “power” governing the growth of the function.

6.7.2 Exponential Functions

Let \(y = e^{a x}\). Then

\[ D_0 y = a e^{a x} = a y. \]

The relative derivative becomes:

\[ D_1 y = \frac{x}{y} D_0 y = \frac{x}{y} \cdot a y = a x. \]

Here, \(D_1 y\) grows linearly with \(x\), reflecting how the exponential grows faster and faster over time relative to its own size.

6.7.3 Logarithmic Functions

Let \(y = \ln x\). Then

\[ D_0 y = \frac{1}{x}. \]

The relative derivative is:

\[ D_1 y = \frac{x}{\ln x} \cdot \frac{1}{x} = \frac{1}{\ln x}. \]

This derivative decays very slowly as \(x\) increases, capturing the ultra–slow relative change in logarithmic functions.

6.8 Summary of Chapter 6

In this chapter we:

  • Defined the relative derivative \(D_1 y = \dfrac{dy \cdot x}{dx \cdot y} = \dfrac{x}{y} D_0 y\).
  • Interpreted \(D_1\) as the relative change of \(y\) per relative change of \(x\).
  • Revisited the Golden Relation \(x D_0(y) = y D_1(y)\).
  • Derived a product rule: \(D_1(u v) = D_1 u + D_1 v\).
  • Connected \(D_1\) to growth models and scaling phenomena.
  • Placed \(D_1\) between \(D_0\) (absolute change) and \(D_2\) (logarithmic change).
  • Worked out examples for power, exponential and logarithmic functions.

The relative derivative \(D_1\) is the first true step beyond classical calculus. It provides a natural tool for systems where proportions and scaling are more fundamental than raw differences.
