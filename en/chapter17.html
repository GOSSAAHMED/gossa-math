Chapter 17 – Relative Series Expansions

In the previous chapter we introduced the general idea of hierarchical series. In this chapter we specialize to the relative level, based on the derivative \(D_1\). The goal is to develop series expansions that are naturally adapted to functions governed by relative change rather than absolute change.

17.1 From Classical Taylor to Relative Taylor

The classical Taylor series of a smooth function \(f\) at a base point \(x_0\) is

\[ f(x) = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{D_0^k f(x_0)}{k!}\,(x-x_0)^k, \]

where \(D_0\) is the ordinary derivative. This expansion uses the absolute displacement \((x-x_0)\) as the local coordinate.

For many phenomena (economic growth, population, compounding processes), the natural variable is not \(x\) itself, but \(\ln x\) or relative change \(\tfrac{x}{x_0}\). This leads us to build series in the relative coordinate:

\[ u = \ln\!\left(\frac{x}{x_0}\right). \]

17.2 The Relative Derivative D₁ and the u–Variable

Recall that the level–1 derivative is

\[ D_1 f(x) = \frac{x}{f(x)}\,\frac{df}{dx}. \]

It measures the relative rate of change of \(f\) with respect to the relative change of \(x\). To see this, note that with

\[ u = \ln x \quad\Rightarrow\quad \frac{du}{dx} = \frac{1}{x}, \]

we can write

\[ \frac{d f}{du} = \frac{df}{dx}\,\frac{dx}{du} = x\,\frac{df}{dx}, \] \[ D_1 f = \frac{1}{f}\frac{df}{du} = \frac{d}{du}(\ln f). \]

So \(D_1 f\) is exactly the derivative of \(\ln f\) with respect to \(\ln x\). This suggests expanding \(\ln f\) as a classical Taylor series in the variable \(u-u_0\), where

\[ u_0 = \ln x_0,\qquad u - u_0 = \ln\!\left(\frac{x}{x_0}\right). \]

17.3 First–Order Relative Approximation

For small relative changes \(|u-u_0| \ll 1\), we can write a first–order approximation:

\[ \ln f(x) \approx \ln f(x_0) + \left.\frac{d}{du}\ln f\right|_{u_0} (u-u_0). \]

But \(\tfrac{d}{du}\ln f = D_1 f\), so:

\[ \ln f(x) \approx \ln f(x_0) + D_1 f(x_0)\,\ln\!\left(\frac{x}{x_0}\right), \] \[ f(x) \approx f(x_0)\, \exp\!\left( D_1 f(x_0)\,\ln\!\left(\frac{x}{x_0}\right) \right) = f(x_0)\left(\frac{x}{x_0}\right)^{D_1 f(x_0)}. \]

This is the simplest relative series expansion: near \(x_0\), the function behaves like a power law with exponent \(D_1 f(x_0)\).

Example 17.1 – Recovering a Power Law

If \(f(x) = A x^k\), then:

\[ D_1 f(x) = k \quad (\text{constant}). \]

The first–order relative expansion gives:

\[ f(x) \approx f(x_0) \left(\frac{x}{x_0}\right)^{k} = A x_0^k \left(\frac{x}{x_0}\right)^k = A x^k, \]

which is exact. This shows that relative expansions are perfectly adapted to power laws.

17.4 Relative Taylor Series for ln f

To go beyond first order, we expand \(\ln f\) as a classical Taylor series in the variable \(u=\ln x\):

\[ \ln f(u) = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{1}{k!} \left.\frac{d^k}{du^k}\ln f(u)\right|_{u_0} (u-u_0)^k. \]

The first derivative is:

\[ \frac{d}{du}\ln f = D_1 f. \]

Higher derivatives with respect to \(u\) can be expressed in terms of \(D_1 f\) and its derivatives, but for the conceptual picture we simply denote:

\[ R_k(x_0) := \left.\frac{d^k}{du^k}\ln f(u)\right|_{u_0}. \]

Then:

\[ \ln f(x) = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{R_k(x_0)}{k!} \bigl(\ln(x/x_0)\bigr)^k, \] \[ f(x) = \exp\!\left( \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{R_k(x_0)}{k!} \bigl(\ln(x/x_0)\bigr)^k \right). \]

This is a relative Taylor series for \(f\) around the base point \(x_0\).

17.5 Relative Series in Terms of D₁

Since the first derivative of \(\ln f\) with respect to \(u\) is \(D_1 f\), we have:

\[ R_1(x_0) = D_1 f(x_0). \]

Higher coefficients involve higher derivatives with respect to \(u\), which can be expressed schematically as:

\[ R_k(x_0) = \bigl(D_1^{(k-1)} D_1 f\bigr)(x_0), \]

where the superscript indicates repeated differentiation with respect to \(u = \ln x\). In this sense, the relative series is generated by the hierarchy of relative derivatives of \(f\).

Example 17.2 – Slightly Deformed Power Law

Suppose a function behaves like a power law with a slowly varying exponent:

\[ f(x) = x^{k(x)}, \]

with \(k(x)\) smooth and slowly changing. Then:

\[ \ln f(x) = k(x)\,\ln x, \]

and:

\[ D_1 f(x) = \frac{d}{du}\bigl(\ln f(x)\bigr) = k(x) + (\ln x)\,k'(x). \]

Evaluating at \(x_0\) gives a first–order correction to the pure power law. Higher–order terms in the relative series capture the slow drift of the exponent \(k(x)\).

17.6 Interpretation and Use Cases

Relative series expansions are particularly useful when:

  • the underlying dynamics are multiplicative (compounding, growth in %),
  • functions behave like power laws or nearly power laws,
  • the natural variable is \(\ln x\) instead of \(x\).

In such cases:

  • The coordinate \(\ln(x/x_0)\) is small even for large variations in \(x\),
  • The first relative term already captures most of the behavior,
  • Higher–order terms provide controlled corrections on a logarithmic scale.

Relative series form the bridge between Hierarchical Calculus and practical modelling of power–law and scale–invariant phenomena.

17.7 Summary of Chapter 17

In this chapter we:

  • Moved from classical Taylor series to relative Taylor series.
  • Used the coordinate \(u = \ln(x/x_0)\) as the natural expansion variable.
  • Showed that the first–order relative approximation is \(f(x) \approx f(x_0)(x/x_0)^{D_1 f(x_0)}\).
  • Defined a full relative series by expanding \(\ln f\) in powers of \(\ln(x/x_0)\).
  • Linked the coefficients to derivatives with respect to \(\ln x\), closely related to the operator \(D_1\).
  • Discussed applications to power laws and slowly varying exponents.

Relative series expansions are the first concrete example of hierarchical series. In the next chapter we will extend these ideas further to logarithmic series and ultra–slow growth governed by the higher–level derivatives \(D_2\) and beyond.
