Chapter 25 – Applications in Probability and Information

Probability and information theory are naturally logarithmic: probabilities multiply, while information and entropy add. This makes them an ideal domain for Hierarchical Calculus, where the derivatives \(D_0, D_1, D_2\) are designed to operate on nested logarithmic scales.

In this chapter we explore:

  • hierarchical derivatives on probabilities and odds,
  • information content and entropy,
  • heavy-tailed distributions and relative behaviour in the tails,
  • growth of information and ultra–slow learning.

25.1 Probabilities, Odds and Log-Probabilities

Let \(p(x)\) be a probability density or mass function depending on a parameter \(x\). A central object in statistics is the log–likelihood \(\ln p(x)\), and ratios of probabilities. The level–1 derivative with respect to \(x\) is

\[ D_1^{(x)} p = \frac{x}{p}\,\frac{dp}{dx}. \]

This quantity measures the relative rate of change of probability with respect to the relative change in the parameter. It is the derivative of \(\ln p\) with respect to \(\ln x\).

Example 25.1 – Power-Law Prior

Consider a prior distribution on a positive parameter:

\[ p(x) \propto x^{-\alpha}, \]

with \(\alpha>0\). Then

\[ \ln p(x) = -\alpha \ln x + \text{const} \quad\Rightarrow\quad D_1^{(x)} p = -\alpha. \]

The heavy-tail exponent \(\alpha\) is exactly the hierarchical derivative \(D_1^{(x)} p\). This connects power-law priors directly to the framework of Hierarchical Calculus.

25.2 Information Content and Hierarchical Derivatives

The self-information of an event of probability \(p\) is

\[ I = -\ln p. \]

If \(p\) depends on a parameter \(x\), then:

\[ \frac{dI}{dx} = -\frac{1}{p}\frac{dp}{dx}. \]

Using the level–1 derivative \(D_1^{(x)} p\), we can write:

\[ \frac{dI}{d\ln x} = -\frac{1}{p}\frac{dp}{dx}\,\frac{dx}{d\ln x} = -\frac{x}{p}\frac{dp}{dx} = -D_1^{(x)} p. \]

Thus the change of information with respect to \(\ln x\) is directly related to the hierarchical derivative of \(p\).

25.3 Entropy and Relative Sensitivity

For a discrete distribution \(\{p_i(x)\}\) depending on a parameter \(x\), the Shannon entropy is:

\[ H(x) = -\sum_i p_i(x)\,\ln p_i(x). \]

Differentiating with respect to \(x\) and reorganising terms, one finds expressions involving derivatives of \(\ln p_i\). In hierarchical form, the sensitivity of entropy to relative changes in \(x\) can be written using \(D_1^{(x)} p_i\).

Example 25.2 – Entropy vs Scale Parameter

Consider a family of distributions parameterised by a scale \(\sigma\), such as Gaussian or Laplace families. The level–1 derivative

\[ D_1^{(\sigma)} H = \frac{\sigma}{H}\frac{dH}{d\sigma} \]

measures the percentage change in entropy per percentage change in the scale. In heavy-tailed families this can behave very differently from light-tailed ones, which can be analysed with hierarchical derivatives of the underlying \(p(x;\sigma)\).

25.4 Heavy-Tailed Distributions and Relative Behaviour

Many probabilistic models in physics, finance and information theory involve heavy-tailed distributions, where moments may diverge and tails follow a power law:

\[ P(X > x) \sim C x^{-\gamma}, \]

for large \(x\). The tail exponent \(\gamma\) determines the relative frequency of extreme events.

Using hierarchical derivatives with respect to \(x\):

\[ D_1^{(x)} P(X > x) = \frac{x}{P(X > x)}\frac{d}{dx}P(X > x) \approx -\gamma \]

for large \(x\). Hierarchical Calculus thus encodes the tail exponent as a derivative on logarithmic scales.

Example 25.3 – Slowly Changing Tail Exponent

In some models the exponent \(\gamma\) itself can depend on scale:

\[ D_1^{(x)} P(X > x,t) \approx -\gamma(t). \]

Hierarchical series in \(\ln(t/t_0)\) can track how \(\gamma(t)\) evolves over time, which is relevant for systems where risk profiles slowly change (e.g. networks, markets, or environments).

25.5 Information Growth and D₁, D₂

Let \(K(t)\) be a cumulative measure of information or knowledge, such as the total number of distinct patterns learned by a system over time. We can study its growth on different levels.

  • Absolute growth: \(D_0^{(t)} K = \frac{dK}{dt}\).
  • Relative growth: \(D_1^{(t)} K = \frac{t}{K}\frac{dK}{dt}\).
  • Logarithmic growth: \(D_2^{(t)} K = \frac{t\ln t}{K\,\ln K}\frac{dK}{dt}\).

Exponential learning corresponds to constant \(D_0^{(t)} K / K\), power-law learning to constant \(D_1^{(t)} K\), and ultra-slow saturation to nearly constant \(D_2^{(t)} K\).

Example 25.4 – Ultra-Slow Information Saturation

Suppose a learning system approaches a limit of complexity it can represent. The effective number of distinct patterns \(K(t)\) may grow extremely slowly, perhaps like

\[ K(t) \approx \exp\!\bigl(A(\ln t)^{\beta}\bigr). \]

For such forms, the level–2 derivative \(D_2^{(t)} K\) can become approximately constant, making it a natural descriptor of the remaining learning capacity on double–logarithmic time scales.

25.6 Mutual Information and Relative Change

Mutual information \(I(X;Y)\) measures the dependence between random variables \(X\) and \(Y\). It often changes slowly as a function of some parameter \(\theta\) (e.g. channel quality, training time, model size).

The hierarchical derivative:

\[ D_1^{(\theta)} I = \frac{\theta}{I}\frac{dI}{d\theta} \]

measures the percentage change of information per percentage change in \(\theta\). For ultra–slow regimes, one can move to \(D_2\) acting on \(I\) as a function of \(\theta\) or time.

25.7 Conceptual Summary

The main conceptual links between Hierarchical Calculus and probability / information are:

  • Logarithms of probabilities and tail functions fit naturally with hierarchical coordinates \(\ln x\) and \(\ln(\ln x)\).
  • Heavy-tail exponents, scaling exponents and sensitivity of entropy can be expressed as hierarchical derivatives \(D_1\).
  • Ultra–slow changes in distributions or information content can be modelled with \(D_2\).
  • Information growth over time can be organised into exponential, power-law and ultra–slow regimes linked to \(D_0, D_1, D_2\).

25.8 Summary of Chapter 25

In this chapter we:

  • Applied hierarchical derivatives to probabilities, odds and log–likelihoods.
  • Related information content and entropy changes to \(D_1\).
  • Described heavy-tailed distributions and tail exponents via hierarchical derivatives.
  • Modelled information growth and ultra–slow saturation using \(D_1\) and \(D_2\).
  • Outlined how mutual information and other information-theoretic quantities fit into the hierarchical framework.

These results show that Hierarchical Calculus is deeply compatible with the logarithmic nature of probability and information. In the next chapters we will move from external systems to internal ones: hierarchical models of human understanding and intelligence.
