Chapter 14 – General Hierarchical Integration In

Chapter 13 introduced the logarithmic integral \(\mathcal{I}_2\) as the inverse of the derivative \(D_2\). In this chapter we move to the general case and define the hierarchical integral operator of level \(n\), denoted \(\mathcal{I}_n\), which inverts the derivative \(D_n\).

The goal is to describe a unified pattern that includes the classical integral \(\mathcal{I}_0\), the relative integral \(\mathcal{I}_1\) and the logarithmic integral \(\mathcal{I}_2\) as special cases.

14.1 Reminder: The General Hierarchical Derivative Dₙ

The \(n\)-th level derivative is defined by

\[ D_n y = \frac{L_n(x)}{L_n(y)} D_0 y = \frac{L_n(x)}{L_n(y)} \frac{dy}{dx}, \]

where \(D_0 y = \dfrac{dy}{dx}\) is the classical derivative and \(L_n\) is the \(n\)-th hierarchical scale function:

  • \(L_0(x) = 1\),
  • \(L_1(x) = x\),
  • \(L_2(x) = x\ln x\),
  • \(L_3(x) = x\ln x\ln(\ln x)\),
  • and so on, by multiplying successive logarithmic layers.

The same ladder is used for the dependent variable \(y\) through \(L_n(y)\).

14.2 Inverting Dₙ: Strategy

Suppose we are given a function \(F_n(x)\) and we want to find \(y(x)\) such that

\[ D_n y(x) = F_n(x). \]

Using the definition of \(D_n\), this is equivalent to

\[ \frac{L_n(x)}{L_n(y)} \frac{dy}{dx} = F_n(x), \] \[ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{L_n(y)}{L_n(x)}\, F_n(x). \]

This is a first–order nonlinear differential equation for \(y\). The idea behind \(\mathcal{I}_n\) is to transform it into an equation that is linear in some new variable.

14.3 Choosing the Hierarchical Variable Φₙ(y)

For each level \(n\), we introduce a transformed variable

\[ z_n = \Phi_n(y), \]

chosen so that the derivative of \(z_n\) is proportional to \(D_n y\) with a factor depending only on \(x\). Formally, we want:

\[ D_0 z_n = \Psi_n(x) \, D_n y, \]

where \(\Psi_n(x)\) is some scale function depending only on \(x\). Then:

\[ z_n(x) = \int \Psi_n(x)\, F_n(x)\, dx + C, \] \[ y(x) = \Phi_n^{-1}\bigl(z_n(x)\bigr). \]

This gives the abstract form of the hierarchical integral:

\[ \mathcal{I}_n[F_n](x) = \Phi_n^{-1}\!\left( \int \Psi_n(x)\, F_n(x)\, dx + C \right). \]

14.4 Low-Level Cases as a Guide

14.4.1 Level 0 – Classical Integral

For \(n=0\),

\[ D_0 y = F_0(x), \quad \Phi_0(y) = y, \quad \Psi_0(x) = 1. \]

Then

\[ y(x) = \int F_0(x)\, dx + C. \]

14.4.2 Level 1 – Relative Integral

For \(n=1\),

\[ D_1 y = \frac{x}{y} \frac{dy}{dx}. \]

Setting \(\Phi_1(y) = \ln y\), one checks that

\[ \frac{d}{dx}(\ln y) = \frac{D_1 y}{x}, \] \[ \Psi_1(x) = \frac{1}{x}. \]

Therefore:

\[ \ln y(x) = \int \frac{F_1(x)}{x}\, dx + C, \] \[ \mathcal{I}_1[F_1](x) = \exp\!\left( \int \frac{F_1(x)}{x}\, dx + C \right). \]

14.4.3 Level 2 – Logarithmic Integral

For \(n=2\),

\[ D_2 y = \frac{x\ln x}{y\ln y} \frac{dy}{dx}. \]

Choosing \(\Phi_2(y) = \ln(\ln y)\) gives:

\[ \frac{d}{dx}(\ln(\ln y)) = \frac{D_2 y}{x\ln x}, \] \[ \Psi_2(x) = \frac{1}{x\ln x}, \]

and so:

\[ \ln(\ln y(x)) = \int \frac{F_2(x)}{x\ln x}\, dx + C, \] \[ \mathcal{I}_2[F_2](x) = \exp\!\left( \exp\!\left( \int \frac{F_2(x)}{x\ln x}\, dx + C \right) \right). \]

These three cases illustrate the general pattern clearly.

14.5 Structure of Φₙ and Ψₙ

At each level \(n\), the function \(\Phi_n\) is built by applying \(n\) logarithms to \(y\), while \(\Psi_n\) involves the corresponding hierarchy of logarithms of \(x\). For example:

  • \(n = 0\): no logarithms, \(\Phi_0(y) = y\), \(\Psi_0(x) = 1\).
  • \(n = 1\): one logarithm in \(y\), one in \(x\), \(\Phi_1(y) = \ln y\), \(\Psi_1(x) = 1/x\).
  • \(n = 2\): two logarithms in \(y\), two in \(x\), \(\Phi_2(y) = \ln(\ln y)\), \(\Psi_2(x) = 1/(x\ln x)\).

More generally, one may define:

\[ \Phi_n(y) = \underbrace{\ln(\ln(\cdots \ln y))}_{n\ \text{times}}, \] \[ \Psi_n(x) = \frac{1}{x \ln x \ln(\ln x)\cdots}, \]

where the product in the denominator contains the first \(n-1\) logarithmic layers. This choice aligns the structure of \(\Phi_n\) and \(\Psi_n\) with the scale function \(L_n\).

14.6 Formal Definition of Iₙ

Combining these ideas, we can write the formal expression:

\[ \boxed{ \mathcal{I}_n[F_n](x) = \Phi_n^{-1}\!\left( \int \Psi_n(x)\, F_n(x)\, dx + C \right), } \]

where:

  • \(\Phi_n\) is an \(n\)-fold logarithmic transform of \(y\),
  • \(\Psi_n\) is the corresponding scale factor in \(x\),
  • \(F_n(x)\) plays the role of the hierarchical derivative \(D_n y\).

This formula is not meant as a closed-form expression for all \(n\), but as a structural rule that generalizes the familiar cases \(n = 0, 1, 2\).

14.7 Example: Constant Dₙ

Consider the equation

\[ D_n y = k, \]

with \(k\) constant. Then:

\[ \Phi_n(y(x)) = \int \Psi_n(x)\, k\, dx + C = k \int \Psi_n(x)\, dx + C. \]

At each level, this gives a simple relationship between the hierarchical coordinate \(\Phi_n(y)\) and a primitive of \(\Psi_n(x)\). For:

  • \(n=0\): \(\Phi_0(y)=y\), \(\Psi_0(x)=1\) ⇒ \(y = kx + C\).
  • \(n=1\): \(\Phi_1(y)=\ln y\), \(\Psi_1(x)=1/x\) ⇒ \(\ln y = k\ln x+C\) ⇒ \(y = A x^k\).
  • \(n=2\): \(\Phi_2(y)=\ln(\ln y)\), \(\Psi_2(x)=1/(x\ln x)\) ⇒ \(\ln(\ln y) = k\ln(\ln x)+C\) ⇒ \(y = \exp(A (\ln x)^k)\).

Higher levels continue this pattern with deeper nested exponentials.

14.8 Relationship to Inverse Functions of Dₙ

Since \(D_n\) is defined in terms of \(L_n(x)\) and \(L_n(y)\), and \(\mathcal{I}_n\) is defined through \(\Phi_n\) and \(\Psi_n\), we can view \(\mathcal{I}_n\) as the functional inverse of \(D_n\):

\[ \mathcal{I}_n(D_n y) = y + C_n, \] \[ D_n(\mathcal{I}_n[F_n]) = F_n, \]

whenever the expressions are well-defined. The next chapter will explore these inverse relations in more detail.

14.9 Summary of Chapter 14

In this chapter we:

  • Started from the general definition \(D_n y = \dfrac{L_n(x)}{L_n(y)} D_0 y\).
  • Introduced the transformed variable \(\Phi_n(y)\) and scale factor \(\Psi_n(x)\).
  • Derived the structural formula \(\mathcal{I}_n[F_n](x) = \Phi_n^{-1}\bigl(\int \Psi_n(x) F_n(x)\, dx + C\bigr)\).
  • Showed how \(\mathcal{I}_0\), \(\mathcal{I}_1\) and \(\mathcal{I}_2\) fit this general scheme.
  • Discussed the example \(D_n y = k\) and the resulting families of solutions.
  • Prepared the ground for a detailed study of the inverse relations between \(D_n\) and \(\mathcal{I}_n\) in Chapter 15.

General hierarchical integration completes the basic operator pair \((D_n, \mathcal{I}_n)\) at all levels, giving Hierarchical Calculus a full toolkit for describing and reconstructing multi-scale change.
