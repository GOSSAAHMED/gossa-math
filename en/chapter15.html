Chapter 15 – Inverse Relations Between Dn and In

In Chapters 11–14 we introduced the hierarchical derivative operators \(D_n\) and the corresponding integral operators \(\mathcal{I}_n\). The purpose of this chapter is to make the inverse relations between these operators precise, and to understand the role of hierarchical constants of integration.

15.1 The Classical Prototype (n = 0)

At level \(n = 0\) we recover standard calculus. For a differentiable function \(y(x)\):

\[ D_0 y = \frac{dy}{dx}. \]

The classical integral operator \(\mathcal{I}_0\) satisfies:

\[ \mathcal{I}_0(D_0 y)(x) = y(x) + C, \] \[ D_0(\mathcal{I}_0 f)(x) = f(x), \]

whenever \(f\) is integrable and differentiable in the appropriate sense. These two identities are the guiding pattern we generalize to higher hierarchical levels.

15.2 Formal Inverse Relations at Level n

Let \(D_n\) be the hierarchical derivative of level \(n\) and \(\mathcal{I}_n\) its integral operator as constructed in Chapter 14. Formally, we want:

\[ \mathcal{I}_n(D_n y)(x) = y(x) + C_n, \] \[ D_n(\mathcal{I}_n f)(x) = f(x), \]

where \(C_n\) is a “constant of integration at level \(n\)”. The structure of \(C_n\) depends on the transformation \(\Phi_n\) used to define \(\mathcal{I}_n\).

15.2.1 Using the Transform Φₙ

Recall from Chapter 14 that for each level \(n\) we introduced a transformed variable

\[ z_n = \Phi_n(y), \]

such that

\[ D_0 z_n = \Psi_n(x)\, D_n y, \]

with \(\Psi_n(x)\) depending only on \(x\). If we set

\[ F_n(x) = D_n y(x), \]

then

\[ D_0 z_n(x) = \Psi_n(x)\, F_n(x). \]

Integrating at level 0:

\[ z_n(x) = \int \Psi_n(x)\, F_n(x)\, dx + C, \]

and therefore:

\[ y(x) = \Phi_n^{-1}\!\left( \int \Psi_n(x)\, F_n(x)\, dx + C \right). \]

This is exactly the definition of \(\mathcal{I}_n\), so we get the first inverse relation:

\[ \mathcal{I}_n(D_n y)(x) = \Phi_n^{-1}\!\left( \Phi_n(y(x)) + C \right). \]

In particular, if we choose \(C = 0\), we recover \(y(x)\) exactly. For general \(C\), we obtain a transformed constant \(C_n\) which may be multiplicative or even double–exponential when mapped back through \(\Phi_n^{-1}\).

15.3 Hierarchical Constants of Integration

At level 0, the constant of integration is simply an additive constant in \(y\). At higher levels, the constant appears inside logarithms or exponentials.

  • Level 0: \(\mathcal{I}_0(D_0 y) = y + C\).
  • Level 1: with \(\Phi_1(y) = \ln y\), a constant in \(\Phi_1(y)\) becomes a multiplicative constant in \(y\): \(\ln y = \ln y_0 + C \Rightarrow y = e^C y_0\).
  • Level 2: with \(\Phi_2(y) = \ln(\ln y)\), a constant in \(\Phi_2(y)\) becomes a multiplicative constant in \(\ln y\), and hence a double–exponential constant in \(y\).

In general, a constant in the transformed coordinate \(\Phi_n(y)\) becomes a constant at level \(n\) when mapped back through \(\Phi_n^{-1}\).

15.4 Checking the Inverse Relations at Low Levels

15.4.1 Level 1: D₁ and I₁

For \(D_1\) we have:

\[ D_1 y = \frac{x}{y} \frac{dy}{dx}. \]

The integral operator \(\mathcal{I}_1\) acts on a function \(F_1\) by:

\[ \mathcal{I}_1[F_1](x) = \exp\!\left( \int \frac{F_1(x)}{x}\, dx + C \right). \]

If \(F_1(x) = D_1 y(x)\), then:

\[ \frac{F_1(x)}{x} = \frac{1}{y}\frac{dy}{dx} = D_0(\ln y), \]

so:

\[ \int \frac{F_1(x)}{x}\, dx = \ln y(x) + C_0, \] \[ \mathcal{I}_1(D_1 y)(x) = \exp\!\left(\ln y(x) + C_0 + C\right) = e^{C_0+C} y(x). \]

Thus:

\[ \mathcal{I}_1(D_1 y) = A_1\, y, \]

where \(A_1 > 0\) is a constant. This is the natural inverse relation at level 1.

For the other direction, if we set

\[ y(x) = \mathcal{I}_1[F_1](x) = \exp\!\left( \int \frac{F_1(x)}{x}\, dx \right), \]

then

\[ \ln y(x) = \int \frac{F_1(x)}{x}\, dx, \] \[ \frac{d}{dx}(\ln y) = \frac{F_1(x)}{x}, \] \[ D_1 y = \frac{x}{y} \frac{dy}{dx} = F_1(x). \]

So

\[ D_1(\mathcal{I}_1[F_1]) = F_1. \]

15.4.2 Level 2: D₂ and I₂

For \(D_2\) we have:

\[ D_2 y = \frac{x\ln x}{y\ln y} \frac{dy}{dx}, \] \[ \frac{D_2 y}{x\ln x} = \frac{1}{y\ln y} \frac{dy}{dx} = D_0(\ln(\ln y)). \]

The integral operator \(\mathcal{I}_2\) is:

\[ \mathcal{I}_2[F_2](x) = \exp\!\left( \exp\!\left( \int \frac{F_2(x)}{x\ln x}\, dx + C \right) \right). \]

Setting \(F_2(x) = D_2 y(x)\) gives:

\[ \int \frac{F_2(x)}{x\ln x} dx = \ln(\ln y(x)) + C_0, \] \[ \mathcal{I}_2(D_2 y)(x) = \exp\!\left( \exp\!\left( \ln(\ln y(x)) + C_0 + C \right) \right). \]

The inner exponential yields a multiplicative factor in \(\ln y\), which then becomes a double–exponential factor in \(y\). Thus:

\[ \mathcal{I}_2(D_2 y) = A_2(y)\, y, \]

where \(A_2(y)\) encodes the level–2 constant; in many applications we can fix this constant by imposing a boundary condition such as \(y(x_0) = y_0\).

Conversely, if:

\[ y(x) = \mathcal{I}_2[F_2](x), \]

then

\[ \ln(\ln y(x)) = \int \frac{F_2(x)}{x\ln x}\, dx, \] \[ D_0(\ln(\ln y)) = \frac{F_2(x)}{x\ln x}, \] \[ D_2 y = F_2(x). \]

So again we have:

\[ D_2(\mathcal{I}_2[F_2]) = F_2. \]

15.5 Abstract Inverse Pair (Dₙ, Iₙ)

At level \(n\), the pair \((D_n, \mathcal{I}_n)\) behaves like:

  • \(D_n\) measures change after compressing the variables through the scale functions \(L_n(x)\) and \(L_n(y)\).
  • \(\mathcal{I}_n\) reconstructs the original function by:
    1. transforming to the variable \(\Phi_n(y)\),
    2. integrating against the factor \(\Psi_n(x)\),
    3. and inverting \(\Phi_n\).

Formally they satisfy:

\[ \mathcal{I}_n(D_n y) = y + C_n,\qquad D_n(\mathcal{I}_n f) = f, \]

with \(C_n\) determined by boundary or normalization conditions.

15.6 Interpretation

The inverse relations between \(D_n\) and \(\mathcal{I}_n\) show that Hierarchical Calculus is a complete calculus system:

  • Derivatives \(D_n\) describe multi-scale change.
  • Integrals \(\mathcal{I}_n\) reconstruct multi-scale structure.
  • Constants of integration appear at progressively deeper levels (additive, multiplicative, double exponential, …).

This duality is the key to building hierarchical series, solving hierarchical differential equations, and designing models where dynamics unfold across many layers of scale.

15.7 Summary of Chapter 15

In this chapter we:

  • Stated the formal inverse relations between \(D_n\) and \(\mathcal{I}_n\).
  • Used the transformed variable \(\Phi_n(y)\) and scale factor \(\Psi_n(x)\) to derive these relations.
  • Analyzed hierarchical constants of integration and how they differ from level to level.
  • Verified explicitly the inverse relations for \(n=1\) and \(n=2\).
  • Interpreted the pair \((D_n, \mathcal{I}_n)\) as a full multi-scale generalization of the classical derivative–integral pair.

In the next chapter, we will use these inverse relations to construct hierarchical series, generalizing power series and Taylor expansions to the hierarchical setting.
