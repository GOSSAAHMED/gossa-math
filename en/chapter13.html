Chapter 13 – Simple Hierarchical Integral Examples

In Chapters 11 and 12 we introduced hierarchical integrals as inverse operators to the derivatives \(D_n\). In this chapter we work through a series of concrete examples, starting from very simple cases and gradually moving to more structured ones.

The goal is not to exhaust all possibilities, but to give a clear feeling for what it means to “integrate” at levels \(n = 0, 1, 2\).

13.1 Warm–Up: Classical Integral (𝓘₀)

At level \(n = 0\), the hierarchical integral is simply the usual indefinite integral. If

\[ D_0 y = F_0(x), \]

then

\[ y(x) = \mathcal{I}_0[F_0](x) = \int F_0(x)\, dx + C. \]

Example 13.1 – Polynomial Integrals

Let

\[ D_0 y = 3x^2 - 4x + 1. \]

Then

\[ y(x) = x^3 - 2x^2 + x + C. \]

This is pure standard calculus, serving as our baseline.

13.2 Simple Integrals at Level 1 (Relative Integrals)

At level \(n = 1\), the hierarchical derivative is

\[ D_1 y = \frac{x}{y}\, D_0 y. \]

If we are given a function \(F_1(x)\) and we want \(D_1 y = F_1(x)\), then:

\[ \frac{x}{y}\, \frac{dy}{dx} = F_1(x) \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \frac{1}{y} \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{F_1(x)}{x}. \]

But

\[ \frac{1}{y} \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{d}{dx}(\ln y). \]

So we obtain:

\[ \frac{d}{dx}(\ln y) = \frac{F_1(x)}{x}, \] \[ \ln y(x) = \int \frac{F_1(x)}{x}\, dx + C, \] \[ y(x) = \exp\!\left( \int \frac{F_1(x)}{x}\, dx + C \right). \]

This is the basic recipe for the level–1 integral \(\mathcal{I}_1\).

Example 13.2 – Constant Relative Derivative

Suppose

\[ D_1 y = k \]

for some constant \(k \in \mathbb{R}\). Then

\[ \frac{d}{dx}(\ln y) = \frac{k}{x}. \]

Integrating:

\[ \ln y = k \ln x + C, \quad y = e^{C} x^k. \]

Denoting \(A = e^C\), we obtain

\[ y(x) = A x^k. \]

So a constant relative derivative generates a pure power law. This is consistent with Chapter 6: for \(y = A x^k\), we have \(D_1 y = k\).

Example 13.3 – Relative Derivative Proportional to ln x

Now suppose

\[ D_1 y = a \ln x, \]

where \(a\) is a constant. Then

\[ \frac{d}{dx}(\ln y) = \frac{a \ln x}{x}. \]

Integrating:

\[ \ln y = a \int \frac{\ln x}{x} dx + C. \]

We recall that

\[ \int \frac{\ln x}{x} dx = \frac{(\ln x)^2}{2}. \]

Hence:

\[ \ln y = a \frac{(\ln x)^2}{2} + C, \quad y = \exp\!\left( \frac{a}{2} (\ln x)^2 + C \right). \]

Writing \(A = e^C\), we obtain:

\[ y(x) = A\, \exp\!\left( \frac{a}{2} (\ln x)^2 \right). \]

This function grows faster than a power, but slower than an exponential in \(x\). It is a natural object in the relative–integral world.

13.3 Simple Integrals at Level 2 (Logarithmic Integrals)

At level \(n = 2\), we have:

\[ D_2 y = \frac{x \ln x}{y \ln y} D_0 y. \]

Let \(F_2(x) = D_2 y(x)\). Rearranging gives:

\[ \frac{D_2 y}{x \ln x} = \frac{1}{y\ln y} \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{d}{dx}(\ln(\ln y)). \]

Thus:

\[ \frac{d}{dx}(\ln(\ln y)) = \frac{F_2(x)}{x\ln x}, \] \[ \ln(\ln y(x)) = \int \frac{F_2(x)}{x\ln x}\, dx + C, \] \[ y(x) = \exp\!\left( \exp\!\left( \int \frac{F_2(x)}{x\ln x}\, dx + C \right) \right). \]

Example 13.4 – Constant Logarithmic Derivative

Suppose

\[ D_2 y = k, \]

with \(k\) a constant. Then:

\[ \frac{d}{dx}(\ln(\ln y)) = \frac{k}{x\ln x}. \]

Integrating:

\[ \ln(\ln y) = k \int \frac{dx}{x\ln x} + C = k \ln(\ln x) + C. \]

Exponentiating:

\[ \ln y = e^{k \ln(\ln x) + C} = e^C (\ln x)^k. \]

Let \(A = e^C\). Then:

\[ \ln y = A (\ln x)^k, \] \[ y = \exp\!\left( A (\ln x)^k \right). \]

For suitable \(A\) and \(k\), this describes a family of functions whose growth sits between very slow and very fast, controlled by logarithmic powers. They are natural “eigenfunctions” for \(D_2\).

13.4 Combined View: D₀, D₁, D₂ for One Function

It is instructive to examine a function whose derivatives at different levels have simple forms.

Example 13.5 – A Power–Like Function

Consider

\[ y(x) = x^k, \]

with \(k > 0\). We already know:

\[ D_0 y = k x^{k-1},\quad D_1 y = k,\quad D_2 y = 1. \]

Working in the opposite direction:

  • Starting from \(D_2 y = 1\), integrating at level 2 leads to a function whose logarithm of logarithm is proportional to \(\ln(\ln x)\); under suitable constants, this leads back to a power–like behavior.
  • Starting from \(D_1 y = k\) and using \(\mathcal{I}_1\) reproduces exactly the power law \(y = A x^k\).
  • Starting from \(D_0 y = k x^{k-1}\) and using \(\mathcal{I}_0\) also gives \(y = x^k + C\).

This shows that the same function can be reconstructed consistently from different hierarchical levels, provided we know which derivative we are given and we use the corresponding integral operator.

13.5 Interpretation and Applications

These examples suggest the following interpretation:

  • \(\mathcal{I}_0\) reconstructs a function from its absolute rate of change.
  • \(\mathcal{I}_1\) reconstructs a function from its relative rate of change.
  • \(\mathcal{I}_2\) reconstructs a function from its logarithmic rate of change.

In practice:

  • In finance, knowing the relative growth rate (in %) naturally calls for a level–1 reconstruction.
  • In information theory or deep learning dynamics, extremely slow changes could be naturally modeled via level–2 derivatives and integrals.
  • In population or cosmological models, different phases of evolution might be best described at different hierarchical levels.

13.6 Summary of Chapter 13

In this chapter we:

  • Reviewed the basic inversion schemes for \(\mathcal{I}_0\), \(\mathcal{I}_1\) and \(\mathcal{I}_2\).
  • Solved simple examples at level 1, including constant and logarithmic relative derivatives.
  • Solved a basic example at level 2 with constant logarithmic derivative \(D_2 y = k\).
  • Showed how the same power–like function can be reconstructed from different hierarchical levels.
  • Outlined how hierarchical integrals naturally correspond to reconstruction from absolute, relative and logarithmic dynamics.

These simple examples prepare the ground for more advanced applications: hierarchical series expansions, hierarchical differential equations, and models of multi–scale physical and informational systems.
