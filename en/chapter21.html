Chapter 21 – Applications in Cosmology

Cosmology studies the evolution of the universe on the largest possible scales. The key dynamical quantity is the scale factor \(a(t)\), describing how distances between comoving galaxies change with cosmic time \(t\). Classical models use ordinary derivatives of \(a(t)\). In this chapter we show how hierarchical derivatives \(D_0, D_1, D_2\) provide a natural language for power–law expansion, accelerated expansion and ultra–slow variations of cosmological quantities.

21.1 Scale Factor and Hubble Parameters

The classical Hubble parameter is defined by

\[ H(t) = \frac{1}{a(t)}\frac{da}{dt}. \]

In terms of the level–1 derivative of the scale factor with respect to time,

\[ D_1^{(t)} a = \frac{t}{a}\,\frac{da}{dt}, \]

we can write:

\[ D_1^{(t)} a(t) = t\,H(t). \]

Thus \(D_1^{(t)} a\) is a dimensionless Hubble parameter measuring relative expansion per logarithmic time step \(\ln t\). It is particularly convenient for power–law cosmologies.

21.2 Power-Law Cosmologies and D₁

Many simple cosmological models have a scale factor of the form

\[ a(t) = a_0 \left(\frac{t}{t_0}\right)^{p}, \]

where the exponent \(p\) depends on the dominant energy component (radiation, matter, dark energy…). For this power–law form:

\[ H(t) = \frac{p}{t}, \qquad D_1^{(t)} a(t) = p. \]

The level–1 derivative is constant and directly equal to the cosmological exponent. This makes \(D_1^{(t)} a\) a very compact descriptor of the expansion regime.

Example 21.1 – Radiation and Matter Eras

In standard cosmology:

  • Radiation domination: \(a(t) \propto t^{1/2}\) ⇒ \(D_1^{(t)} a = 1/2\).
  • Matter domination: \(a(t) \propto t^{2/3}\) ⇒ \(D_1^{(t)} a = 2/3\).

A change of \(D_1^{(t)} a\) from \(1/2\) to \(2/3\) signals the transition from radiation to matter domination. Instead of tracking \(H(t)\) and \(a(t)\) separately, the hierarchical derivative \(D_1^{(t)} a\) encodes the expansion regime in a single number.

21.3 Slowly Varying Exponents

In more realistic models, the expansion is not a pure power law: the effective exponent can vary slowly with time. Suppose

\[ a(t) = a_0 \left(\frac{t}{t_0}\right)^{p(t)}, \]

where \(p(t)\) is a smooth, slowly varying function. Then:

\[ \ln a(t) = \ln a_0 + p(t)\,\ln\!\left(\frac{t}{t_0}\right), \]

and after some algebra one finds that \(D_1^{(t)} a\) contains both \(p(t)\) and its logarithmic derivative. Hierarchical series in \(\ln(t/t_0)\) (Chapter 17) can be used to approximate such models, with coefficients expressed directly in terms of \(D_1^{(t)} a\) and its derivatives.

21.4 Ultra-Slow Evolution and D₂

Certain cosmological quantities may change so slowly that their evolution is visible only on double–logarithmic scales. Examples include:

  • very slow running of fundamental constants,
  • late-time corrections to dark energy,
  • cumulative effects over many orders of magnitude in time.

For a quantity \(X(t)\) depending on cosmic time, the level–2 derivative is:

\[ D_2^{(t)} X = \frac{t \ln t}{X\,\ln X}\,\frac{dX}{dt}. \]

This is the derivative of \(\ln(\ln X)\) with respect to \(\ln(\ln t)\). A model of the form

\[ D_2^{(t)} X(t) = \varepsilon, \]

with small constant \(\varepsilon\), describes a double–logarithmic drift of \(X\) over cosmic time.

Example 21.2 – Ultra-Slow Drift of a Cosmological Parameter

Let \(G(t)\) be an effective gravitational coupling allowed to vary extremely slowly. If

\[ D_2^{(t)} G(t) = \varepsilon, \]

then

\[ \ln(\ln G(t)) = \ln(\ln G_0) + \varepsilon \ln\!\left( \frac{\ln t}{\ln t_0} \right). \]

This evolution is so slow that it would manifest only over enormous cosmic time intervals, yet it is naturally captured by the level–2 derivative \(D_2^{(t)}\).

21.5 Hierarchical View of Cosmological Time Scales

The history of the universe spans many orders of magnitude in time: from fractions of a second after the Big Bang to billions of years. Hierarchical Calculus lets us organise these scales as:

  • Level 0: absolute time \(t\), suitable for early fast processes.
  • Level 1: logarithmic time \(\ln t\), relevant for power–law expansion and many epochs of “scale–invariant” behaviour.
  • Level 2: double–logarithmic time \(\ln(\ln t)\), relevant for very late–time corrections and ultra–slow drifts.

Differential equations written with \(D_0^{(t)}, D_1^{(t)}, D_2^{(t)}\) can model different epochs and connect them smoothly.

Example 21.3 – Piecewise Hierarchical Expansion

A schematic model for the cosmic scale factor could be:

\[ D_0^{(t)} a = f_{\text{early}}(a) \quad (t \ll T_1), \] \[ D_1^{(t)} a = p_{\text{mid}} \quad (T_1 \ll t \ll T_2), \] \[ D_2^{(t)} a = \varepsilon_{\text{late}} \quad (t \gg T_2), \]

representing an early epoch governed by ordinary differential equations, a middle epoch with power–law expansion, and a late epoch with ultra–slow deviations. Hierarchical integration techniques from previous chapters can be used to construct approximate solutions.

21.6 Density Parameters and Relative Derivatives

Cosmic densities (\(\rho_m, \rho_r, \rho_\Lambda\)) often follow power or exponential laws with respect to the scale factor. In terms of \(a\), the relative derivative

\[ D_1^{(a)} \rho = \frac{a}{\rho}\,\frac{d\rho}{da} \]

measures how many percent the density changes per factor of \(a\). For example:

  • Non-relativistic matter: \(\rho_m \propto a^{-3}\) ⇒ \(D_1^{(a)} \rho_m = -3\).
  • Radiation: \(\rho_r \propto a^{-4}\) ⇒ \(D_1^{(a)} \rho_r = -4\).

Any deviation from \(-3\) or \(-4\) can be interpreted as a hierarchical correction captured by higher–order relative series in \(\ln a\).

21.7 Summary of Chapter 21

In this chapter we:

  • Expressed the Hubble parameter and power–law cosmologies in terms of the level–1 derivative \(D_1^{(t)} a\).
  • Showed how \(D_1\) compactly encodes different expansion regimes (radiation, matter, etc.).
  • Introduced level–2 models \(D_2^{(t)} X\) for ultra–slow evolution of cosmological quantities.
  • Discussed hierarchical organisation of cosmic time scales using \(t\), \(\ln t\) and \(\ln(\ln t)\).
  • Applied relative derivatives with respect to the scale factor to standard density scalings.

These ideas illustrate how Hierarchical Calculus can be used as a conceptual framework for cosmology, complementing the traditional differential equations of relativistic cosmology. In the next chapters we will apply similar reasoning to biology, economics, medicine and other complex systems, where multi–scale dynamics are also fundamental.
