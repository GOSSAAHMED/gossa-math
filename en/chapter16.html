Chapter 16 – Hierarchical Series

Classical calculus uses power series and Taylor expansions to represent smooth functions locally. These series are built from repeated applications of the differential operator \(D_0\). In Hierarchical Calculus we have an entire family of derivatives \(D_0, D_1, D_2, \dots, D_n\). It is natural to ask:

Can we build hierarchical series that expand a function using the whole ladder of derivatives?

This chapter introduces the basic idea of hierarchical series and outlines their structure and interpretation.

16.1 Motivation: Beyond Classical Taylor Series

The classical Taylor expansion of a smooth function \(f\) at a point \(x_0\) is:

\[ f(x) = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{f^{(k)}(x_0)}{k!} (x - x_0)^k, \]

where the coefficients involve repeated derivatives \(D_0^k f\). This expansion is well–adapted to functions whose behavior is governed by absolute change.

But many systems are controlled by relative or logarithmic change:

  • growth rates in percentage,
  • ultra–slow dynamics driven by \(\ln x\) or \(\ln(\ln x)\),
  • multi–scale processes with cross–level interactions.

Hierarchical series aim to capture such behavior by using the derivatives \(D_n\) and the associated scale functions \(L_n\).

16.2 Hierarchical Coordinates Around a Base Point

In classical analysis, we expand around a point \(x_0\) using the coordinate \((x - x_0)\). In the hierarchical setting, we introduce a family of coordinates adapted to different levels:

  • Level 0: \(u_0 = x - x_0\).
  • Level 1: \(u_1 = \ln\!\left(\frac{x}{x_0}\right)\).
  • Level 2: \(u_2 = \ln\!\left(\frac{\ln x}{\ln x_0}\right)\).
  • In general: \(u_n\) involves ratios of hierarchical scales \(L_n(x)\) to \(L_n(x_0)\).

These coordinates measure distance not only in absolute space, but also along logarithmic and higher layers.

16.3 Prototype of a Hierarchical Series

Informally, a hierarchical series for a function \(f\) near \(x_0\) takes the form:

\[ f(x) \approx \sum_{n=0}^{N} \sum_{k=0}^{K_n} a_{n,k}\, \bigl(u_n(x)\bigr)^k, \]

where:

  • \(u_n(x)\) is a level–\(n\) coordinate (absolute, relative, logarithmic, …),
  • the coefficients \(a_{n,k}\) depend on hierarchical derivatives \(D_n f\) evaluated at \(x_0\).

In this way, the standard Taylor series is the special case where only the level–0 coordinate is used:

\[ f(x) = \sum_k a_{0,k}\, u_0(x)^k. \]

16.4 Level-1 Series: Relative Expansions

A simple hierarchical series at level 1 expands not in powers of \((x - x_0)\), but in powers of the relative coordinate

\[ u_1(x) = \ln\!\left(\frac{x}{x_0}\right). \]

For functions whose natural variable is \(\ln x\), such as power laws \(x^k\), this choice leads to much simpler expansions.

Example 16.1 – Power Law Around x₀

Consider:

\[ f(x) = x^k = e^{k\ln x}. \]

Around \(x_0\), we can write:

\[ \ln x = \ln x_0 + u_1(x), \] \[ f(x) = e^{k(\ln x_0 + u_1(x))} = x_0^k \, e^{k u_1(x)}. \]

Expanding the exponential in a classical Taylor series:

\[ e^{k u_1} = \sum_{m=0}^{\infty} \frac{k^m}{m!}\, u_1^m. \]

Thus:

\[ f(x) = x_0^k \sum_{m=0}^{\infty} \frac{k^m}{m!} \bigl(\ln(x/x_0)\bigr)^m. \]

This is a relative series in powers of \(\ln(x/x_0)\), naturally aligned with the derivative \(D_1\).

16.5 Level-2 Series: Logarithmic Expansions

At level 2, we introduce the coordinate:

\[ u_2(x) = \ln\!\left( \frac{\ln x}{\ln x_0} \right). \]

Functions that depend on \((\ln x)^k\) or on \(\exp((\ln x)^k)\) can have expansions that are much simpler in terms of \(u_2\) than in terms of \((x - x_0)\).

Example 16.2 – Logarithmic-Exponential Function

Consider:

\[ f(x) = \exp\!\bigl(A (\ln x)^2 \bigr). \]

Note that:

\[ (\ln x)^2 = \bigl(\ln x_0 + \ln(x/x_0)\bigr)^2, \]

or, using \(u_1 = \ln(x/x_0)\), we can rewrite \(f\) as an exponential of a quadratic polynomial in \(u_1\). Alternatively, we may define \(u_2\) so that \(\ln x = \ln x_0 e^{u_2}\), and express \(f\) in powers of \(u_2\). In both cases, the hierarchical coordinates reveal a more natural expansion variable than \((x-x_0)\).

16.6 Using Hierarchical Derivatives as Coefficients

In classical Taylor theory, the coefficients are given by derivatives:

\[ a_{0,k} = \frac{f^{(k)}(x_0)}{k!}. \]

In hierarchical series, we can define families of coefficients using the derivatives \(D_n\). For instance, a pure level–1 series might use:

\[ f(x) \approx \sum_{k=0}^{K} \frac{D_1^k f(x_0)}{k!} \bigl(\ln(x/x_0)\bigr)^k, \]

where \(D_1^k\) denotes repeated application of \(D_1\). Mixed series could use a combination of \(D_0, D_1, D_2,\dots\), with coefficients tailored to each scale.

16.7 Conceptual Picture

Hierarchical series can be viewed as:

  • Local expansions of \(f\) in several scale coordinates simultaneously.
  • A way to decompose \(f\) into contributions coming from absolute, relative, and logarithmic behavior.
  • A bridge between hierarchical derivatives and practical approximation methods.

Just as classical Taylor series support numerical analysis and differential equations, hierarchical series will be tools for analyzing hierarchical differential equations and multi–scale models.

16.8 Outlook

This introductory chapter sketches the structure of hierarchical series without entering into full convergence theory. Later chapters will:

  • Develop relative series expansions (Level–1 Taylor), where coefficients are based on \(D_1\).
  • Introduce logarithmic series and ultra–slow growth (Level–2 and beyond).
  • Compare hierarchical series with classical Taylor series and study when one outperforms the other.

16.9 Summary of Chapter 16

In this chapter we:

  • Motivated the need for hierarchical series as extensions of Taylor series.
  • Introduced hierarchical coordinates \(u_n(x)\) aligned with different levels.
  • Described the general form of a hierarchical series as a double sum over levels and powers.
  • Gave examples of relative and logarithmic expansions for simple functions.
  • Outlined how hierarchical derivatives \(D_n\) can be used to define series coefficients.

Hierarchical series provide the language for approximating and analyzing functions whose behavior is governed by absolute, relative and logarithmic scales all at once. The next chapters will specialize this framework to relative series, logarithmic series and ultra–slow growth.
