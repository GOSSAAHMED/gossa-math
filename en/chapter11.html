Chapter 11 – Hierarchical Integrals (Introduction)

In previous chapters, the hierarchical derivatives \(D_0, D_1, D_2, \dots\) were introduced as layered measures of change: absolute, relative, logarithmic, and so on. But every derivative has a natural inverse: an integral.

This chapter introduces the idea of hierarchical integrals: operators that invert the action of \(D_n\) at each hierarchical level. Classical integration corresponds only to the inverse of \(D_0\), but the hierarchical system requires an entire family of integrals:

\[ \mathcal{I}_0,\; \mathcal{I}_1,\; \mathcal{I}_2,\; \dots,\; \mathcal{I}_n,\dots \]

11.1 Motivation: Why Do We Need Hierarchical Integrals?

In many natural systems:

  • growth is relative, not absolute,
  • information accumulates on logarithmic scales,
  • evolutionary and cosmological changes occur on nested time scales.

These systems cannot be reconstructed from classical integrals because the inverse of \(D_0\) does not undo the effect of \(D_1\) or \(D_2\). For example:

\[ D_1 y = \frac{x}{y}D_0 y, \]

so its inverse must undo both:

  • the derivative itself,
  • and the normalization factor \(\frac{x}{y}\).

Thus, hierarchical integration is needed to “rebuild” a function from its hierarchical derivative.

11.2 What Should a Hierarchical Integral Do?

At each level \(n\), the integral operator \(\mathcal{I}_n\) must satisfy:

\[ \mathcal{I}_n(D_n y) = y + C_n, \]

where \(C_n\) is an integration constant appropriate for level \(n\).

For \(n = 0\), this reduces to the standard rule:

\[ \mathcal{I}_0(D_0 y) = y + C. \]

But for higher levels, the relation between \(D_n\) and \(\mathcal{I}_n\) becomes more intricate because of the scale factors:

\[ D_n y = \frac{L_n(x)}{L_n(y)} D_0 y. \]

Therefore, inverting \(D_n\) means handling both \(D_0\) and the ratio of scale functions simultaneously.

11.3 First Examples of Hierarchical Integrals

11.3.1 Inverse of the Relative Derivative \(D_1\)

For \(D_1 y\), we know:

\[ D_1 y = \frac{x}{y} D_0 y. \]

Rearranging:

\[ \frac{D_1 y}{x} = \frac{D_0 y}{y}. \]

Since

\[ \frac{D_0 y}{y} = D_0 (\ln y), \]

we get:

\[ D_0 (\ln y) = \frac{D_1 y}{x}. \]

Integrating both sides with respect to \(x\):

\[ \ln y = \int \frac{D_1 y}{x} \, dx + C. \]

Thus the inverse operator \(\mathcal{I}_1\) satisfies:

\[ y = \exp\!\left(\int \frac{D_1 y}{x} \, dx \right). \]

This illustrates how hierarchical integration involves both:

  • a classical integral,
  • and a structural function (here: exponentiation).

11.3.2 Inverse of the Logarithmic Derivative \(D_2\)

Recall:

\[ D_2 y = \frac{x \ln x}{y \ln y} D_0 y. \]

Rearranging:

\[ \frac{D_2 y}{x \ln x} = \frac{D_0 y}{y \ln y} = D_0(\ln(\ln y)). \]

Integrating:

\[ \ln(\ln y) = \int \frac{D_2 y}{x \ln x} \, dx + C. \]

Exponentiating twice:

\[ y = \exp\!\left( \exp\!\left( \int \frac{D_2 y}{x \ln x} \, dx \right)\right). \]

This structure becomes even deeper for higher levels.

11.4 Conceptual Meaning of Hierarchical Integrals

Each hierarchical level corresponds to a different kind of reconstruction:

  • Level \(0\): rebuild a function from its absolute rate of change.
  • Level \(1\): rebuild a function from its relative rate of change.
  • Level \(2\): rebuild a function from its logarithmic rate of change.
  • Level \(n\): rebuild a function from progressively more compressed scales.

In general:

\[ \mathcal{I}_n(D_n y) = y + C_n. \]

But the operator \(\mathcal{I}_n\) involves nested integrals and nested exponentials/logarithms depending on the level.

11.5 Summary of Chapter 11

In this chapter we:

  • Introduced the concept of hierarchical integrals.
  • Explained why classical integration is insufficient for inverting hierarchical derivatives.
  • Derived inverse formulas for \(D_1\) and \(D_2\).
  • Explained how each level requires both an integral and a structural inverse (exp/log layers).
  • Prepared the foundation for defining full operators \(\mathcal{I}_n\) in later chapters.

Hierarchical integrals form the “other half” of Hierarchical Calculus. They allow us not only to describe change, but to reconstruct functions across multiple layers of scale.
