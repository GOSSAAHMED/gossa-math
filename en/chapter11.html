Chapter 10 – Hierarchical Chain Rules

The chain rule is one of the most fundamental tools in classical calculus. It tells us how the derivative of a composite function \(y = f(g(x))\) is built from the derivatives of \(f\) and \(g\). In Hierarchical Calculus, we want to understand how this principle extends to all levels \(D_0, D_1, D_2, \dots, D_n\).

In this chapter we derive hierarchical chain rules for the first levels and then present the general pattern for \(D_n\).

10.1 Classical Chain Rule for D0

Let

\[ u = g(x), \qquad y = f(u). \]

Then \(y\) is a composite function of \(x\), written \(y = f(g(x))\). The classical chain rule states:

\[ D_0 y = \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{du} \cdot \frac{du}{dx}. \]

This identity remains valid in Hierarchical Calculus, because \(D_0\) is exactly the usual differential derivative.

10.2 Notation for Hierarchical Derivatives with Respect to Different Variables

To avoid confusion, we will sometimes write the variable explicitly in the notation. For example:

  • \(D_1^{(x)} y\): the relative derivative of \(y\) with respect to \(x\).
  • \(D_1^{(u)} y\): the relative derivative of \(y\) with respect to \(u\).

By definition:

\[ D_1^{(x)} y = \frac{dy \cdot x}{dx \cdot y}, \qquad D_1^{(u)} y = \frac{dy \cdot u}{du \cdot y}. \]

Similarly, for the logarithmic derivative:

\[ D_2^{(x)} y = \frac{dy \cdot x \ln x}{dx \cdot y \ln y}, \qquad D_2^{(u)} y = \frac{dy \cdot u \ln u}{du \cdot y \ln y}. \]

This notation will allow us to express clean multiplicative chain rules.

10.3 Chain Rule for the Relative Derivative D1

We start from the definitions:

\[ D_1^{(x)} y = \frac{x}{y} \frac{dy}{dx}, \qquad D_1^{(u)} y = \frac{u}{y} \frac{dy}{du}, \qquad D_1^{(x)} u = \frac{x}{u} \frac{du}{dx}. \]

Using the classical chain rule

\[ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{du}\,\frac{du}{dx}, \]

we compute:

\[ D_1^{(x)} y = \frac{x}{y} \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{x}{y} \frac{dy}{du} \frac{du}{dx}. \]

But

\[ D_1^{(u)} y = \frac{u}{y} \frac{dy}{du} \quad\Rightarrow\quad \frac{dy}{du} = \frac{y}{u} D_1^{(u)} y, \]

and

\[ D_1^{(x)} u = \frac{x}{u} \frac{du}{dx} \quad\Rightarrow\quad \frac{du}{dx} = \frac{u}{x} D_1^{(x)} u. \]

Substituting these into the expression for \(D_1^{(x)} y\), we obtain:

\[ D_1^{(x)} y = \frac{x}{y} \left( \frac{y}{u} D_1^{(u)} y \right) \left( \frac{u}{x} D_1^{(x)} u \right) = D_1^{(u)} y \cdot D_1^{(x)} u. \]

This gives a beautiful multiplicative chain rule:

\[ D_1^{(x)} y = D_1^{(u)} y \cdot D_1^{(x)} u. \]

In words:

The relative derivative of \(y\) with respect to \(x\) is the product of the relative derivative of \(y\) with respect to \(u\) and the relative derivative of \(u\) with respect to \(x\).

10.4 Chain Rule for the Logarithmic Derivative D2

We now repeat the same reasoning for the logarithmic derivative. Recall:

\[ D_2^{(x)} y = \frac{x \ln x}{y \ln y} \frac{dy}{dx}, \] \[ D_2^{(u)} y = \frac{u \ln u}{y \ln y} \frac{dy}{du}, \] \[ D_2^{(x)} u = \frac{x \ln x}{u \ln u} \frac{du}{dx}. \]

Again, starting with the classical chain rule:

\[ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{du} \frac{du}{dx}, \]

we compute:

\[ D_2^{(x)} y = \frac{x \ln x}{y \ln y} \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{x \ln x}{y \ln y} \frac{dy}{du} \frac{du}{dx}. \]

From the definitions:

\[ D_2^{(u)} y = \frac{u \ln u}{y \ln y} \frac{dy}{du} \quad\Rightarrow\quad \frac{dy}{du} = \frac{y \ln y}{u \ln u} D_2^{(u)} y, \]

and

\[ D_2^{(x)} u = \frac{x \ln x}{u \ln u} \frac{du}{dx} \quad\Rightarrow\quad \frac{du}{dx} = \frac{u \ln u}{x \ln x} D_2^{(x)} u. \]

Substituting:

\[ D_2^{(x)} y = \frac{x \ln x}{y \ln y} \left( \frac{y \ln y}{u \ln u} D_2^{(u)} y \right) \left( \frac{u \ln u}{x \ln x} D_2^{(x)} u \right) = D_2^{(u)} y \cdot D_2^{(x)} u. \]

Thus we obtain another multiplicative chain rule:

\[ D_2^{(x)} y = D_2^{(u)} y \cdot D_2^{(x)} u. \]

The pattern is clear: when we stay at the same level of the hierarchy, composition corresponds to multiplication of derivatives.

10.5 General Chain Rule for Dn

Let \(L_n\) be the \(n\)-th level scale function in the hierarchical ladder (built from \(x\) and \(\ln x\) in a multiplicative way). The general definition can be written symbolically as:

\[ D_n^{(x)} y = \frac{L_n(x)}{L_n(y)} \frac{dy}{dx}. \]

For the intermediate variable \(u\) we similarly have:

\[ D_n^{(u)} y = \frac{L_n(u)}{L_n(y)} \frac{dy}{du}, \qquad D_n^{(x)} u = \frac{L_n(x)}{L_n(u)} \frac{du}{dx}. \]

Using again the classical chain rule:

\[ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{du} \frac{du}{dx}, \]

we find:

\[ D_n^{(x)} y = \frac{L_n(x)}{L_n(y)} \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{L_n(x)}{L_n(y)} \frac{dy}{du} \frac{du}{dx}. \]

As before, we express \(\dfrac{dy}{du}\) and \(\dfrac{du}{dx}\) in terms of hierarchical derivatives:

\[ D_n^{(u)} y = \frac{L_n(u)}{L_n(y)} \frac{dy}{du} \quad\Rightarrow\quad \frac{dy}{du} = \frac{L_n(y)}{L_n(u)} D_n^{(u)} y, \] \[ D_n^{(x)} u = \frac{L_n(x)}{L_n(u)} \frac{du}{dx} \quad\Rightarrow\quad \frac{du}{dx} = \frac{L_n(u)}{L_n(x)} D_n^{(x)} u. \]

Substituting into the expression for \(D_n^{(x)} y\) and simplifying, all scale factors cancel out and we obtain:

\[ D_n^{(x)} y = D_n^{(u)} y \cdot D_n^{(x)} u. \]

This is the general hierarchical chain rule:

At each level \(n\), the hierarchical derivative of a composite function is the product of the hierarchical derivatives of each stage of the composition, evaluated at the same level \(n\).

10.6 Example: A Simple Composition

Consider

\[ u = x^k, \qquad y = e^{a u}, \]

so that \(y = e^{a x^k}\). We examine the relative derivative \(D_1\).

Step 1: Compute D1(x) u

For \(u = x^k\),

\[ D_0 u = k x^{k-1}, \qquad D_1^{(x)} u = \frac{x}{u} D_0 u = \frac{x}{x^k} \cdot k x^{k-1} = k. \]

Step 2: Compute D1(u) y

For \(y = e^{a u}\),

\[ D_0 y = a e^{a u} = a y, \qquad D_1^{(u)} y = \frac{u}{y} D_0 y = \frac{u}{y} \cdot a y = a u. \]

Step 3: Use the Hierarchical Chain Rule

The chain rule for \(D_1\) gives:

\[ D_1^{(x)} y = D_1^{(u)} y \cdot D_1^{(x)} u = (a u) \cdot k = a k x^k. \]

We can check this directly from the definition:

\[ y = e^{a x^k}, \quad D_0 y = a k x^{k-1} e^{a x^k} = a k x^{k-1} y, \] \[ D_1^{(x)} y = \frac{x}{y} D_0 y = \frac{x}{y} \cdot a k x^{k-1} y = a k x^k, \]

which agrees with the chain rule result.

10.7 Multiple Intermediate Variables (Sketch)

More generally, if

\[ u_1 = g_1(x),\quad u_2 = g_2(x),\quad \dots,\quad u_m = g_m(x), \] \[ y = f(u_1, \dots, u_m), \]

then the classical chain rule expresses \(D_0 y\) as a sum over the partial derivatives of \(f\). At the hierarchical levels \(D_n\), analogous formulas can be derived where:

  • Each term is weighted by a scale factor involving \(L_n\).
  • The structure is a sum of products of partial hierarchical derivatives \(D_n\) with respect to each intermediate variable.

The full multi–variable theory of hierarchical chain rules will be developed in later chapters, once partial derivatives and hierarchical gradients are introduced.

10.8 Summary of Chapter 10

In this chapter we:

  • Reviewed the classical chain rule for the base derivative \(D_0\).
  • Introduced explicit notation \(D_n^{(x)} y\), \(D_n^{(u)} y\), etc., to distinguish derivatives with respect to different variables.
  • Derived a multiplicative chain rule for the relative derivative: \(D_1^{(x)} y = D_1^{(u)} y \cdot D_1^{(x)} u\).
  • Derived a similar multiplicative chain rule for the logarithmic derivative: \(D_2^{(x)} y = D_2^{(u)} y \cdot D_2^{(x)} u\).
  • Generalized to all levels: \(D_n^{(x)} y = D_n^{(u)} y \cdot D_n^{(x)} u\).
  • Illustrated the rule with a concrete example \(y = e^{a x^k}\).
  • Outlined how multi–variable versions of the chain rule can be developed in the hierarchical setting.

The hierarchical chain rules confirm that the family of derivatives \(\{D_n\}\) behaves coherently under composition. At every level, change propagates through composite systems in a structured and predictable way.
