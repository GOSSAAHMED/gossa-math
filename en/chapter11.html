Chapter 11 – Hierarchical Integrals (Introduction)

In earlier chapters, Hierarchical Calculus introduced derivatives \(D_0, D_1, D_2, \dots, D_n\) as progressively deeper measures of change. The natural next step is to introduce their inverses: the hierarchical integrals.

11.1 Why Hierarchical Integrals?

Classical integration inverts only the absolute derivative \(D_0\). But hierarchical derivatives modify the rate of change through scale factors like \(x\), \(\ln x\), \(\ln(\ln x)\), etc.

Therefore, each derivative level needs its own inverse operator:

\[ \mathcal{I}_0,\; \mathcal{I}_1,\; \mathcal{I}_2,\; \dots \]

11.2 What Does a Hierarchical Integral Do?

At level \(n\), the integral operator must satisfy:

\[ \mathcal{I}_n(D_n y) = y + C_n. \]

For \(n=0\):

\[ \mathcal{I}_0(D_0 y) = y + C. \]

11.3 Inverting D₁

For the relative derivative:

\[ D_1 y = \frac{x}{y} D_0 y. \]

Rearranging:

\[ D_0(\ln y) = \frac{D_1 y}{x}. \]

Integrating:

\[ \ln y = \int \frac{D_1 y}{x} dx + C. \]

So:

\[ y = \exp\!\left(\int \frac{D_1 y}{x} dx \right). \]

11.4 Inverting D₂

For the logarithmic derivative:

\[ D_2 y = \frac{x\ln x}{y\ln y}D_0 y. \]

Rearranging:

\[ D_0(\ln(\ln y)) = \frac{D_2 y}{x\ln x}. \]

Integrating twice:

\[ y = \exp\!\left( \exp\!\left(\int \frac{D_2 y}{x\ln x} dx\right)\right). \]

11.5 Conceptual Meaning

Each integral level reconstructs a function from a deeper layer of change:

  • Level 0 → absolute reconstruction
  • Level 1 → relative reconstruction
  • Level 2 → logarithmic reconstruction
  • Level n → reconstruction across n nested scales

11.6 Summary

In this chapter we:

  • Introduced hierarchical integrals as inverses of \(D_n\).
  • Showed how to invert \(D_1\) using exponentials.
  • Showed how to invert \(D_2\) using double exponentials.
  • Clarified that \(\mathcal{I}_n\) reconstructs a function across n levels of scale.
