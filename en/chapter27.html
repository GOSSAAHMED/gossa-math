Chapter 27 – Hierarchical Models of Intelligence and Learning

In previous chapters we applied Hierarchical Calculus to physical, biological and economic systems. Here we turn inward and ask: Can intelligence itself be described hierarchically? We propose that human and artificial intelligence can be modelled using the same levels of change embodied in the derivatives \(D_0, D_1, D_2, \dots\).

Intelligence can be seen as the capacity to:

  • react quickly to stimuli (level 0),
  • adapt relatively to changing contexts (level 1),
  • restructure internal models slowly but deeply (level 2 and beyond).

27.1 Intelligence as a Function of Experience

Let \(I(t)\) be an abstract measure of effective intelligence or competence as a function of experience or training time \(t\). We can study how \(I\) changes at different hierarchical levels:

\[ D_0^{(t)} I = \frac{dI}{dt}, \qquad D_1^{(t)} I = \frac{t}{I}\frac{dI}{dt}, \qquad D_2^{(t)} I = \frac{t\ln t}{I\ln I}\frac{dI}{dt}. \]

Each level reveals a different “view” of learning:

  • \(D_0\): immediate gains per unit time,
  • \(D_1\): percentage gains per logarithmic time,
  • \(D_2\): ultra-slow improvement visible only on double-log scales.

27.2 Three Levels of Learning Systems

We can classify learning processes into three main levels:

  1. Reactive Level (D₀-dominated)
  2. Adaptive Level (D₁-dominated)
  3. Reflective Level (D₂-dominated)

27.2.1 Reactive Level – D₀

At this level, systems respond directly to inputs using fixed rules or shallow mappings. Learning is either absent or very local. Classical control systems and simple reflexes live here.

Mathematically, only \(D_0 I\) matters: small changes in performance follow short bursts of training but do not generalise deeply.

27.2.2 Adaptive Level – D₁

At this level, systems adjust relatively to experience. A change in context scales previous knowledge rather than replacing it. Examples include:

  • humans improving skills through practice across many tasks,
  • machine learning models trained on large datasets with transfer between tasks,
  • brains forming internal representations that scale with exposure.

Here \(D_1^{(t)} I\) is the central object: it measures learning in terms of relative progress per decade of experience.

Example 27.1 – Scaling Laws in Machine Learning

Empirically, many models show power-law improvement in error \(E\) as a function of data size \(N\):

\[ E(N) \approx E_0 N^{-\alpha}. \]

The corresponding performance \(I = 1/E\) then satisfies approximately:

\[ D_1^{(N)} I \approx \text{constant}. \]

This suggests that intelligence in such models has a strongly hierarchical, scale-invariant component.

27.2.3 Reflective Level – D₂

At the highest level, systems change not just their responses or parameters, but their internal learning strategies: how they search, represent, generalise and compress information. These changes occur extremely slowly.

This deep meta-learning can be described with \(D_2^{(t)} I\). When \(D_2^{(t)} I\) is non-zero but small, the system:

  • gradually discovers better inductive biases,
  • restructures its internal representations,
  • improves its own ability to learn efficiently.

Example 27.2 – Human Expertise as Level-2 Learning

A novice learns rules (D₀). An intermediate learner recognises patterns across situations (D₁). An expert subtly reshapes how they see problems: which features matter, which simplifications are valid.

This slow restructuring corresponds to a non-zero \(D_2^{(t)} I\): deep intelligence emerging on double-logarithmic scales of time.

27.3 Hierarchical Learning Equations

We can propose schematic hierarchical learning equations of the form:

\[ D_0^{(t)} I = F_0(I, t), \] \[ D_1^{(t)} I = F_1(I, t), \] \[ D_2^{(t)} I = F_2(I, t), \]

where each \(F_n\) describes contributions at the corresponding level. In simple models:

  • \(F_0\) encodes task-specific learning curves,
  • \(F_1\) captures improvements from scaling data or compute,
  • \(F_2\) encodes ultra-slow architectural or cognitive changes.

27.4 Intelligence Across Multiple Scales

Hierarchical Calculus suggests that intelligence should never be measured at a single resolution. Instead, one should examine:

  • Short term: How fast does performance improve (large \(D_0\))?
  • Medium term: How does performance scale with experience (structure of \(D_1\))?
  • Long term: Does the system become a better learner over time (behaviour of \(D_2\))?

Example 27.3 – Comparing Two Learning Systems

System A has rapid short-term gains (large \(D_0\)) but saturates quickly: \(D_1\) and \(D_2\) are small. System B learns slowly initially (small \(D_0\)) but its relative progress improves (larger \(D_1\)) and it continues to refine its own learning strategy (non-zero \(D_2\)).

In the long run, system B may surpass A, even if A appears “smarter” in short tasks. This matches many real-world observations of talent versus long-term dedication.

27.5 Hierarchical Representation of Knowledge

Beyond learning curves, knowledge itself can be structured hierarchically:

  • Level 0: raw facts and patterns.
  • Level 1: relations between facts (concepts, schemas).
  • Level 2: meta-concepts (thinking about how concepts connect, analogies across domains).
  • Higher levels: philosophical or abstract views that unify many fields.

Hierarchical Calculus provides a mathematical analogy: each level \(n\) corresponds to derivatives with respect to deeper logarithmic coordinates, describing slower and more global changes.

27.6 Artificial Systems and Hierarchical Design

For artificial intelligence, Hierarchical Calculus suggests that powerful systems should:

  • support fast adaptation at level 0 (fine-tuning, online updates),
  • scale smoothly at level 1 (performance predictable under more data, compute and parameters),
  • and slowly improve their own learning algorithms at level 2 (meta-learning, architecture search, self-refinement).

Designing algorithms that explicitly interact across these levels is a promising direction for multi-scale learning research.

27.7 Summary of Chapter 27

In this final application chapter we:

  • Modelled intelligence as a function of experience, analysed via \(D_0, D_1, D_2\).
  • Distinguished reactive, adaptive and reflective levels of learning.
  • Related scaling laws in machine learning to hierarchical derivatives.
  • Argued that deep expertise and meta-learning correspond to non-trivial \(D_2\)-level dynamics.
  • Outlined how knowledge and learning architectures can be viewed as hierarchical structures.

Hierarchical Calculus thus provides not only a mathematical framework for functions and series, but also a conceptual framework for understanding intelligence itself as a multi-level, multi-scale phenomenon.
