Chapter 8 – The General Hierarchical Derivative Dn

In the previous chapters we studied the three fundamental hierarchical derivatives: the differential derivative \(D_0\), the relative derivative \(D_1\), and the logarithmic derivative \(D_2\). In this chapter we generalize these ideas and define the full family

\[ D_0, D_1, D_2, \dots, D_n, \dots \]

Each level \(D_n\) corresponds to a deeper layer of sensitivity to change, constructed systematically from a logarithmic ladder \(L_n\).

8.1 The Logarithmic Ladder Ln

The general hierarchical derivative is built from a sequence of functions \(L_n(x)\), which we call the logarithmic ladder. The simplest form of this ladder is defined recursively by:

\[ L_0(x) = x,\quad L_1(x) = \ln x,\quad L_2(x) = \ln(\ln x),\quad L_3(x) = \ln(\ln(\ln x)),\quad \dots \]

In words, \(L_n(x)\) is obtained by applying the natural logarithm \(n\) times, starting from \(x\). In practice, the hierarchy is used in the region where all these expressions are well–defined and positive.

The ladder encodes how we progressively “compress” the scale of \(x\): from linear, to logarithmic, to log–of–log, and so on. The same construction is applied to \(y\).

8.2 General Definition of Dn

Let \(y = f(x)\) with \(x\) and \(y\) in a domain where all the relevant \(L_k(x)\) and \(L_k(y)\) are defined and nonzero. The general hierarchical derivative of order \(n\) is defined by:

\[ D_n y = \frac{L_n(x)}{L_n(y)}\, D_0 y, \]

where \(D_0 y = \dfrac{dy}{dx}\) is the usual differential derivative.

For small values of \(n\), this reproduces:

  • \(n = 0\): \(D_0 y = \dfrac{L_0(x)}{L_0(y)} D_0 y = \dfrac{x}{y} D_0 y\) does not give new information, so by convention we take \(D_0\) as the base derivative \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\).
  • \(n = 1\): \(D_1 y = \dfrac{L_0(x)}{L_0(y)} D_0 y = \dfrac{x}{y} D_0 y\), the relative derivative.
  • \(n = 2\): \(D_2 y = \dfrac{L_1(x)}{L_1(y)} D_1 y = \dfrac{\ln x}{\ln y} D_1 y\), the logarithmic derivative as studied in Chapter 7.

In general, each level \(D_n\) introduces a new layer of scaling through the functions \(L_n\).

8.3 Iterative Construction of Dn

The formula

\[ D_n y = \frac{L_n(x)}{L_n(y)}\, D_0 y \]

can be written in an iterative way. For \(n \ge 1\) we define

\[ D_n y = \frac{L_{n-1}(x)}{L_{n-1}(y)}\, D_{n-1} y. \]

This shows that:

  • Starting from \(D_0\), each step multiplies by a scaling factor depending on \(L_{n-1}(x)\) and \(L_{n-1}(y)\).
  • Each new level is obtained by “normalizing” the previous derivative with respect to a deeper logarithmic layer.

For example:

\[ D_1 y = \frac{L_0(x)}{L_0(y)} D_0 y = \frac{x}{y} D_0 y, \] \[ D_2 y = \frac{L_1(x)}{L_1(y)} D_1 y = \frac{\ln x}{\ln y} D_1 y, \] \[ D_3 y = \frac{L_2(x)}{L_2(y)} D_2 y = \frac{\ln(\ln x)}{\ln(\ln y)} D_2 y, \]

and so on. Each level refines our description of change by moving one step higher on the logarithmic ladder.

8.4 Hierarchical Golden–Type Relations

For the first levels, we have seen:

  • \(x D_0(y) = y D_1(y)\),
  • \(\ln x \, D_1(y) = \ln y \, D_2(y)\).

These are examples of a more general pattern. If we define

\[ G_n(x, y) = L_n(x) D_n(y), \]

then the relation

\[ L_n(x)\, D_n(y) = L_n(y)\, D_{n+1}(y) \]

expresses a hierarchical Golden Relation: each level \(D_{n+1}\) is linked to \(D_n\) via the same type of identity, but now on the scale of \(L_n\). The absolute, relative and logarithmic cases are simply the first instances of this infinite chain.

8.5 Examples: Power and Exponential Functions

8.5.1 Power Functions

Let \(y = x^k\) with \(k > 0\). We already know:

\[ D_0 y = k x^{k-1},\quad D_1 y = k,\quad D_2 y = 1. \]

For higher levels, we use

\[ We define the iterated logarithms by $$ \ln^{(0)}(z)=z,\quad \ln^{(1)}(z)=\ln z,\quad \ln^{(2)}(z)=\ln(\ln z),\dots $$ and the hierarchical ladder by $$ L_n(z)=\prod_{k=0}^{n-1}\ln^{(k)}(z). $$ Then the $n$-th hierarchical derivative of $y$ with respect to $x$ is $$ D_n(y)=\frac{dy}{dx}\,\frac{L_n(x)}{L_n(y)}. $$ \]

Because \(y = x^k\), we have \(L_n(y) = L_n(x^k)\). Due to the slow variation of the logarithmic ladder, many of these ratios tend to constants or very slowly varying functions. The important conceptual point is that for power laws, \(D_n y\) quickly becomes simple: \(D_1\) gives the exponent, \(D_2\) gives 1, and higher levels detect finer structure if present.

8.5.2 Exponential Functions

Let \(y = e^{a x}\). We already found:

\[ D_0 y = a y,\quad D_1 y = a x,\quad D_2 y = \ln x. \]

For \(D_3\), we use the iterative definition:

\[ D_3 y = \frac{L_2(x)}{L_2(y)} D_2 y = \frac{\ln(\ln x)}{\ln(\ln y)} \ln x. \]

Here \(\ln y = a x\), so \(\ln(\ln y) = \ln(a x)\). Thus,

\[ D_3 y = \frac{\ln(\ln x)}{\ln(a x)} \ln x, \]

which varies extremely slowly and captures subtle changes in how different logarithmic scales interact.

8.6 Hierarchical View of Sensitivity

Each derivative \(D_n\) provides a different measure of sensitivity of \(y\) with respect to \(x\):

Level Derivative Type of Sensitivity
0 \(D_0\) Absolute change: \(dy/dx\).
1 \(D_1\) Relative change: how \(y\) changes compared to its own size.
2 \(D_2\) Logarithmic change: behavior on compressed scales.
\(n\) \(D_n\) Change after \(n\) levels of logarithmic compression.

In complex systems, different phenomena may be most naturally described at different hierarchical levels. The power of Hierarchical Calculus lies in its ability to move up and down this ladder systematically.

8.7 Conceptual Summary

The general hierarchical derivative \(D_n\) is not just a formal extension. It encodes the idea that:

  • Change has many layers: absolute, relative, logarithmic, and beyond.
  • Each layer corresponds to a particular way of compressing and comparing scales.
  • The logarithmic ladder \(L_n\) provides the structure to build these layers.
  • Golden–type relations connect all levels into a coherent algebraic system.

8.8 Summary of Chapter 8

In this chapter we:

  • Introduced the logarithmic ladder \(L_n(x)\) as a central tool.
  • Defined the general hierarchical derivative \(D_n y = \dfrac{L_n(x)}{L_n(y)} D_0 y\).
  • Showed the iterative construction: \(D_n y = \dfrac{L_{n-1}(x)}{L_{n-1}(y)} D_{n-1} y\).
  • Extended Golden–type relations to all levels.
  • Illustrated the behavior of \(D_n\) on power and exponential functions.
  • Interpreted each \(D_n\) as a distinct layer of sensitivity to change, associated with \(n\)-fold logarithmic compression.

The family \(\{D_n\}_{n\ge 0}\) is the backbone of Hierarchical Calculus. It provides a unified language for describing how systems evolve across many scales of magnitude and complexity.
