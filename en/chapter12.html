Chapter 12 – The Hierarchical Integral Operator 𝓘n

In Chapter 11 we introduced hierarchical integrals informally as inverses of the derivatives \(D_0, D_1, D_2\). In this chapter we give a more systematic description of the hierarchical integral operator \(\mathcal{I}_n\) and its basic properties.

12.1 Inverse Perspective

Recall that the general hierarchical derivative is defined by

\[ D_n y = \frac{L_n(x)}{L_n(y)}\, D_0 y, \]

where \(D_0 y = \dfrac{dy}{dx}\) and \(L_n\) is the \(n\)-th hierarchical scale function. The integral operator \(\mathcal{I}_n\) should invert this action, at least formally:

\[ \mathcal{I}_n(D_n y) = y + C_n, \]

where \(C_n\) is a constant (or more generally, a constant of integration adapted to level \(n\)).

12.2 Solving for D₀y in Terms of Dₙy

From the definition of \(D_n\) we can solve for the base derivative:

\[ D_0 y = \frac{L_n(y)}{L_n(x)}\, D_n y. \]

This means that to reconstruct \(y\) from \(D_n y\), we must:

  1. Multiply \(D_n y\) by the factor \(\dfrac{L_n(y)}{L_n(x)}\).
  2. Integrate the result with respect to \(x\) at the base level.

Formally:

\[ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{L_n(y)}{L_n(x)}\, D_n y. \]

This is a first–order differential equation for \(y\) whose right-hand side involves both \(x\) and \(y\).

12.3 Operator Form: 𝓘ₙ Acting on a Given Function

Suppose we are given a function \(F_n(x)\) which we want to interpret as a hierarchical derivative:

\[ F_n(x) = D_n y(x). \]

Then \(y\) must satisfy:

\[ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{L_n(y)}{L_n(x)}\, F_n(x). \]

The operator \(\mathcal{I}_n\) maps the “data” \(F_n\) to a solution \(y\). In formal notation:

\[ y(x) = \mathcal{I}_n[F_n](x). \]

At low levels, this reproduces the constructions from Chapter 11:

  • For \(n=0\): \(\dfrac{dy}{dx} = F_0(x)\) and \(\mathcal{I}_0[F_0](x) = \displaystyle \int F_0(x)\,dx\).
  • For \(n=1\): \(\dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{y}{x}F_1(x)\) and \(\ln y = \displaystyle \int \dfrac{F_1(x)}{x}\,dx + C\).
  • For \(n=2\): \(\dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{y\ln y}{x\ln x}F_2(x)\) and \(\ln(\ln y) = \displaystyle \int \dfrac{F_2(x)}{x\ln x}\,dx + C\).

12.4 Hierarchical Change of Variables

The structure of \(\mathcal{I}_n\) becomes clearer if we introduce a new dependent variable adapted to the level \(n\). Define:

\[ z_n = \Phi_n(y), \]

where \(\Phi_n\) is chosen so that \(D_n y\) becomes the ordinary derivative of \(z_n\) up to a simple factor of \(x\). For example:

  • \(n=0\): we can take \(\Phi_0(y) = y\).
  • \(n=1\): we can take \(\Phi_1(y) = \ln y\).
  • \(n=2\): we can take \(\Phi_2(y) = \ln(\ln y)\).

Then, as shown previously:

  • \(D_1 y\) relates to \(D_0(\ln y)\), so integrating \(D_1 y\) is equivalent to integrating the derivative of \(\ln y\) after rescaling by \(x\).
  • \(D_2 y\) relates to \(D_0(\ln(\ln y))\), so integrating \(D_2 y\) is equivalent to integrating the derivative of \(\ln(\ln y)\) after rescaling by \(x\ln x\).

At level \(n\), \(\Phi_n\) is a multi–logarithmic transformation of \(y\) aligned with the structure of \(L_n\).

12.5 𝓘ₙ as “Integrate Then Invert”

The examples for \(D_1\) and \(D_2\) show a common pattern:

  1. Convert \(D_n y\) into a base–level derivative of \(\Phi_n(y)\) using the definition of \(D_n\).
  2. Integrate the resulting expression with respect to \(x\).
  3. Apply the inverse transformation \(\Phi_n^{-1}\) to recover \(y\).

Symbolically, for suitable \(\Phi_n\), we have:

\[ D_0 \Phi_n(y) = \Psi_n(x)\, D_n y, \]

for some scale factor \(\Psi_n(x)\) depending only on \(x\). Then:

\[ \Phi_n(y(x)) = \int \Psi_n(x)\, D_n y(x)\, dx + C, \]

and

\[ y(x) = \Phi_n^{-1}\!\left( \int \Psi_n(x)\, D_n y(x)\, dx + C \right). \]

This is the conceptual definition of \(\mathcal{I}_n\):

\[ \mathcal{I}_n[F_n](x) = \Phi_n^{-1}\!\left( \int \Psi_n(x)\, F_n(x)\, dx + C \right), \]

whenever \(F_n(x) = D_n y(x)\).

12.6 Low–Level Operators as Special Cases

12.6.1 𝓘₀ – Classical Integral

For \(n=0\), we have \(D_0 y = F_0(x)\). We can take \(\Phi_0(y) = y\), \(\Psi_0(x) = 1\). Then:

\[ \mathcal{I}_0[F_0](x) = \int F_0(x)\, dx. \]

12.6.2 𝓘₁ – Relative Integral

For \(n=1\), with \(F_1(x) = D_1 y(x)\), we found:

\[ D_0(\ln y) = \frac{F_1(x)}{x}. \]

So we can take:

\[ \Phi_1(y) = \ln y,\quad \Psi_1(x) = \frac{1}{x}, \]

giving:

\[ \ln y(x) = \int \frac{F_1(x)}{x}\, dx + C, \] \[ \mathcal{I}_1[F_1](x) = \exp\!\left( \int \frac{F_1(x)}{x}\, dx + C \right). \]

12.6.3 𝓘₂ – Logarithmic Integral

For \(n=2\), with \(F_2(x) = D_2 y(x)\), we found:

\[ D_0(\ln(\ln y)) = \frac{F_2(x)}{x\ln x}. \]

Thus:

\[ \Phi_2(y) = \ln(\ln y),\quad \Psi_2(x) = \frac{1}{x\ln x}, \]

and:

\[ \ln(\ln y(x)) = \int \frac{F_2(x)}{x\ln x}\, dx + C, \] \[ \mathcal{I}_2[F_2](x) = \exp\!\left( \exp\!\left( \int \frac{F_2(x)}{x\ln x}\, dx + C \right) \right). \]

12.7 Conceptual Interpretation of 𝓘ₙ

We can summarize the role of \(\mathcal{I}_n\) as:

  • At level \(n\), we no longer integrate “raw” changes, but changes that have been filtered through \(n\) hierarchical scales.
  • The operator \(\mathcal{I}_n\) first unpacks those scales (via \(\Psi_n(x)\) and the integral), then inverts the structural transformation (via \(\Phi_n^{-1}\)).
  • The higher \(n\) is, the more compressed the information is, and the more nested exponentials/logarithms appear in the reconstruction.

12.8 Summary of Chapter 12

In this chapter we:

  • Defined the hierarchical integral operator \(\mathcal{I}_n\) as the formal inverse of \(D_n\).
  • Derived the differential equation \(\dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{L_n(y)}{L_n(x)} D_n y\) for reconstruction.
  • Introduced the idea of a transformed variable \(\Phi_n(y)\) that linearizes the effect of \(D_n\) at level \(n\).
  • Expressed \(\mathcal{I}_n\) schematically as \(\mathcal{I}_n[F_n](x) = \Phi_n^{-1}\!\left(\int \Psi_n(x) F_n(x)\, dx + C\right)\).
  • Showed that classical, relative and logarithmic integrals (\(\mathcal{I}_0, \mathcal{I}_1, \mathcal{I}_2\)) fit this scheme as special cases.

In later chapters, hierarchical integrals will be used to construct series, solve differential equations and model systems whose dynamics unfold across multiple levels of scale.
