Chapter 5 – The Differential Derivative D0

The differential derivative D0 is the base of the entire hierarchical structure of derivatives. In this chapter we revisit the classical derivative from the point of view of Hierarchical Calculus and clarify its role as the absolute measure of change, from which all higher hierarchical derivatives are built.

5.1 Definition of D0

In Hierarchical Calculus we adopt the standard definition of the differential derivative:

\[ D_0 y = \frac{dy}{dx}. \]

More precisely, if \(y = f(x)\), then \(D_0 y\) is the limit

\[ D_0 y = \lim_{\Delta x \to 0} \frac{f(x + \Delta x) - f(x)}{\Delta x}, \]

whenever this limit exists. This is exactly the classical derivative and measures the instantaneous rate of change of \(y\) with respect to \(x\).

5.2 Geometric Meaning

Geometrically, \(D_0 y\) is the slope of the tangent line to the graph of \(y = f(x)\) at a point:

  • If \(D_0 y > 0\), the graph is increasing at that point.
  • If \(D_0 y < 0\), the graph is decreasing.
  • If \(D_0 y = 0\), the graph is locally flat (critical point).

In Hierarchical Calculus, this slope is regarded as the first layer of change in a hierarchy of deeper and more refined measures of variation.

5.3 Algebraic Rules for D0

The differential derivative satisfies the usual algebraic rules:

5.3.1 Linearity

For functions \(u(x)\) and \(v(x)\), and constants \(a, b\),

\[ D_0 (a u + b v) = a\,D_0 u + b\,D_0 v. \]

5.3.2 Product Rule

\[ D_0 (u v) = u\,D_0 v + v\,D_0 u. \]

5.3.3 Quotient Rule

For \(v(x) \neq 0\),

\[ D_0 \!\left(\frac{u}{v}\right) = \frac{v\,D_0 u - u\,D_0 v}{v^2}. \]

5.3.4 Chain Rule

If \(y = f(u)\) and \(u = g(x)\), then

\[ D_0 y = \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{du}\,\frac{du}{dx}. \]

These rules remain valid within Hierarchical Calculus, since \(D_0\) is the classical derivative.

5.4 D0 and the Golden Relation

The central identity of Hierarchical Calculus is the Golden Relation:

\[ x \, D_0(y) = y \, D_1(y). \]

This equation links the absolute derivative \(D_0\) with the relative derivative \(D_1\). Using the definition of \(D_1\),

\[ D_1 y = \frac{dy \cdot x}{dx \cdot y}, \]

we see that \(D_1 y\) is essentially a rescaled version of \(D_0 y\):

\[ D_1 y = \frac{x}{y} \, D_0 y. \]

Thus, \(D_0\) serves as a reference derivative, and higher hierarchical derivatives are obtained by multiplying \(D_0\) by factors that encode scale and structure.

5.5 D0 as the Base of the Hierarchy

In general, the \(n\)-th hierarchical derivative has the form

\[ D_n y = \frac{L_n(x)}{L_n(y)} \, D_0 y, \]

where \(L_n\) is the logarithmic ladder:

\[ L_0(x) = x,\quad L_1(x) = \ln x,\quad L_2(x) = \ln(\ln x),\quad \dots \]

The key point is that every hierarchical derivative \(D_n\) is constructed from \(D_0\) by a multiplicative factor that depends on \(x\) and \(y\). This confirms the foundational role of the differential derivative in the entire theory.

5.6 Examples of D0 in the Hierarchical Context

5.6.1 Polynomials

Let \(y = x^k\), where \(k\) is a constant. Then

\[ D_0 y = k x^{k-1}. \]

The relative derivative is

\[ D_1 y = \frac{x}{y} D_0 y = \frac{x}{x^k} \cdot k x^{k-1} = k, \]

a constant. Thus, for power laws, \(D_1\) measures a fixed proportional rate of change, while \(D_0\) still varies with \(x\).

5.6.2 Exponential Functions

Let \(y = e^{ax}\). Then

\[ D_0 y = a e^{ax} = a y. \]

The derivative is directly proportional to the function itself. In hierarchical terms, \(D_0\) already behaves “relatively” for exponentials, and higher derivatives like \(D_1\) and \(D_2\) refine this behavior further.

5.6.3 Logarithmic Functions

Let \(y = \ln x\). Then

\[ D_0 y = \frac{1}{x}. \]

This is a classical example of a function whose change decreases with scale. In a hierarchical framework, logarithmic functions are naturally associated with higher levels \(D_1, D_2,\dots\), but their base behavior is still encoded by \(D_0\).

5.7 Summary of Chapter 5

In this chapter we:

  • Reintroduced the classical derivative as the differential derivative \(D_0\).
  • Reviewed its geometric and algebraic properties.
  • Explained how the Golden Relation links \(D_0\) to the relative derivative \(D_1\).
  • Showed that every hierarchical derivative \(D_n\) is built from \(D_0\) via the logarithmic ladder \(L_n(x)\).
  • Illustrated \(D_0\) with polynomial, exponential and logarithmic examples in the hierarchical context.

The differential derivative \(D_0\) is therefore the foundation of Hierarchical Calculus: it is the first step in measuring change, upon which all higher layers of the hierarchy are constructed.
