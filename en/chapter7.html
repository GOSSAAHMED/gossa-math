Chapter 7 – The Logarithmic Derivative D2

The logarithmic derivative D2 is the next step in the hierarchy of derivatives in Hierarchical Calculus. While \(D_0\) measures absolute change and \(D_1\) measures relative change, \(D_2\) measures change at the level of logarithmic scales. It is especially suited for systems that evolve extremely slowly or through nested layers of scaling.

7.1 Definition of D2

Let \(y = f(x)\) with \(x > 0\) and \(y > 0\). The logarithmic derivative \(D_2 y\) is defined by:

\[ D_2 y = \frac{dy \cdot x \ln x}{dx \cdot y \ln y}. \]

In terms of the classical derivative \(D_0 y = \dfrac{dy}{dx}\), we can write:

\[ D_2 y = \frac{x \ln x}{y \ln y}\, D_0 y. \]

This fits the general formula of hierarchical derivatives

\[ D_n y = \frac{L_n(x)}{L_n(y)} D_0 y, \]

where the logarithmic ladder is given by

\[ L_0(x) = x,\quad L_1(x) = \ln x,\quad L_2(x) = \ln(\ln x),\quad \dots \]

For \(n = 2\), the factor \(\dfrac{x \ln x}{y \ln y}\) indicates that we are comparing the scales of \(x\) and \(y\) after passing them through a logarithmic transformation.

7.2 Interpretation: Change on Logarithmic Scales

Consider the following transformations:

  • Absolute: \(y\)
  • Relative: \(\ln y\) captures multiplicative changes in \(y\)
  • Logarithmic of logarithmic: \(\ln(\ln y)\) captures changes on ultra–slow or compressed scales

The derivative \(D_2 y\) can be informally understood as measuring how the logarithmic structure of \(y\) responds to changes in the logarithmic structure of \(x\). It is especially relevant when both variables are controlled by slow, cumulative processes.

In many systems:

  • Entropy grows logarithmically with some parameter.
  • Information accumulates in a sub–linear, ultra–slow regime.
  • Complexity increases through repeated layers of transformation.

In such regimes, \(D_0\) may become almost zero, and \(D_1\) may still be too coarse. The derivative \(D_2\) becomes the right “lens” to see change.

7.3 Relation to D0 and D1

Recall:

\[ D_0 y = \frac{dy}{dx}, \qquad D_1 y = \frac{x}{y} D_0 y. \]

We can express \(D_2\) in terms of \(D_1\) as well. Starting from:

\[ D_2 y = \frac{x \ln x}{y \ln y} D_0 y, \]

and noting that

\[ D_1 y = \frac{x}{y} D_0 y \quad\Rightarrow\quad D_0 y = \frac{y}{x} D_1 y, \]

we obtain:

\[ D_2 y = \frac{x \ln x}{y \ln y} \cdot \frac{y}{x} D_1 y = \frac{\ln x}{\ln y} D_1 y. \]

This shows that \(D_2\) is a logarithmically–scaled version of \(D_1\). While \(D_1\) compares absolute levels \(x\) and \(y\), \(D_2\) compares their logarithmic levels \(\ln x\) and \(\ln y\).

7.4 Logarithmic Golden–Type Relations

The Golden Relation for \(D_0\) and \(D_1\) is:

\[ x D_0(y) = y D_1(y). \]

For \(D_2\), using

\[ D_2 y = \frac{\ln x}{\ln y} D_1 y, \]

we can write a similar identity:

\[ \ln x \cdot D_1(y) = \ln y \cdot D_2(y). \]

This is a logarithmic Golden Relation. It states that the relative derivative \(D_1\) and the logarithmic derivative \(D_2\) are related in the same way that the absolute derivative \(D_0\) and relative derivative \(D_1\) are linked, but now at the level of logs:

\[ x D_0(y) = y D_1(y), \qquad \ln x \, D_1(y) = \ln y \, D_2(y). \]

These identities form a ladder of relations connecting the hierarchy of derivatives through algebraic and logarithmic scaling.

7.5 Algebraic Behavior of D2

Since \(D_2\) is derived from \(D_0\), it inherits certain structural properties but in a transformed way.

7.5.1 Product Rule in Logarithmic Form

For \(y = u v\), we already know that:

\[ D_1(u v) = D_1 u + D_1 v. \]

Using

\[ D_2 y = \frac{\ln x}{\ln y} D_1 y, \]

we get:

\[ D_2(u v) = \frac{\ln x}{\ln (u v)} (D_1 u + D_1 v). \]

In general this will not split into a simple sum, but the formula highlights how \(D_2\) encodes a logarithmic normalization of the relative derivatives of the factors.

7.6 Examples of the Logarithmic Derivative

7.6.1 Power Functions

Let \(y = x^k\) with \(k > 0\). Then:

\[ D_0 y = k x^{k-1}, \quad D_1 y = \frac{x}{x^k} \cdot k x^{k-1} = k. \]

For \(D_2\), we have:

\[ D_2 y = \frac{\ln x}{\ln y} D_1 y. \]

Since \(y = x^k\), we have \(\ln y = k \ln x\), and therefore

\[ \frac{\ln x}{\ln y} = \frac{\ln x}{k \ln x} = \frac{1}{k}. \]

Thus:

\[ D_2 y = \frac{1}{k} \cdot D_1 y = \frac{1}{k} \cdot k = 1. \]

So for power functions, \(D_2 y = 1\) is constant. This is a beautiful result: \(D_0\) gives a power of \(x\), \(D_1\) gives the exponent \(k\), and \(D_2\) gives the constant value 1.

7.6.2 Exponential Functions

Let \(y = e^{a x}\). Then:

\[ D_0 y = a e^{ax} = a y,\quad D_1 y = \frac{x}{y} D_0 y = \frac{x}{y} \cdot a y = a x. \]

For \(D_2\):

\[ D_2 y = \frac{\ln x}{\ln y} D_1 y. \]

Here \(\ln y = a x\). Therefore:

\[ D_2 y = \frac{\ln x}{a x} \cdot a x = \ln x. \]

So for exponential functions, the logarithmic derivative \(D_2 y\) equals \(\ln x\). This is another layered effect: \(D_0\) is proportional to \(y\), \(D_1\) grows linearly with \(x\), and \(D_2\) grows logarithmically with \(x\).

7.6.3 Logarithmic Functions

Let \(y = \ln x\). Then:

\[ D_0 y = \frac{1}{x}, \quad D_1 y = \frac{x}{\ln x} \cdot \frac{1}{x} = \frac{1}{\ln x}. \]

To find \(D_2\), we use:

\[ D_2 y = \frac{\ln x}{\ln y} D_1 y. \]

Now \(y = \ln x\), so \(\ln y = \ln(\ln x)\). Hence:

\[ D_2 y = \frac{\ln x}{\ln(\ln x)} \cdot \frac{1}{\ln x} = \frac{1}{\ln(\ln x)}. \]

This derivative decays even more slowly than \(D_1 y\). It reflects an ultra–slow level of change, appropriate for processes whose growth is slower than any simple logarithm.

7.7 Conceptual Role of D2

The logarithmic derivative \(D_2\) plays a crucial conceptual role in Hierarchical Calculus:

  • It captures dynamics on highly compressed, logarithmic scales.
  • It provides a bridge between relative and ultra–slow growth.
  • It is naturally linked to phenomena in information theory, entropy and deep learning processes.
  • It demonstrates that higher hierarchical derivatives are not artificial, but correspond to genuine levels of behavior.

7.8 Summary of Chapter 7

In this chapter we:

  • Defined the logarithmic derivative \(D_2 y = \dfrac{dy \cdot x \ln x}{dx \cdot y \ln y}\).
  • Expressed it in terms of \(D_0\) and \(D_1\): \(D_2 y = \dfrac{x \ln x}{y \ln y} D_0 y = \dfrac{\ln x}{\ln y} D_1 y\).
  • Introduced a logarithmic Golden Relation: \(\ln x \, D_1(y) = \ln y \, D_2(y)\).
  • Showed how \(D_2\) operates on logarithmic scales of change.
  • Computed \(D_2\) for power, exponential and logarithmic functions, revealing simple and elegant patterns.
  • Clarified the conceptual role of \(D_2\) in describing ultra–slow, hierarchical and information–related processes.

Together, \(D_0\), \(D_1\) and \(D_2\) form the core of the hierarchical derivative ladder, providing three complementary views of how quantities evolve: absolute, relative and logarithmic.
