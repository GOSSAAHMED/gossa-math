Chapter 9 – Identities and Properties of Dn

In the previous chapters we introduced the basic hierarchy of derivatives: the differential derivative \(D_0\), the relative derivative \(D_1\), the logarithmic derivative \(D_2\), and the general derivative \(D_n\). In this chapter we collect and organize the main identities and structural properties that these derivatives satisfy.

Our goal is to show that the family \(\{D_n\}\) forms a coherent algebraic system, connected by simple formulas that reflect the layered nature of change.

9.1 Recap: Definition of Dn

Let \(y = f(x)\), with \(x\) and \(y\) in a domain where the hierarchical scales are defined. We use a hierarchical scale \(H_n(z)\), built from \(z\) by multiplying successive logarithmic factors. For example, at low levels: \[ H_1(z) = z,\quad H_2(z) = z \ln z,\quad H_3(z) = z \ln z \ln(\ln z),\ \dots \] whenever these expressions are defined.

The general hierarchical derivative is defined by:

\[ D_n y = \frac{H_n(x)}{H_n(y)}\, D_0 y, \]

where \(D_0 y = \dfrac{dy}{dx}\) is the usual differential derivative.

For the first levels we recover:

  • \(D_0 y = \dfrac{dy}{dx}\)    (differential derivative),
  • \(D_1 y = \dfrac{dy \cdot x}{dx \cdot y} = \dfrac{x}{y} D_0 y\)    (relative derivative),
  • \(D_2 y = \dfrac{dy \cdot x \ln x}{dx \cdot y \ln y} = \dfrac{x \ln x}{y \ln y} D_0 y\)    (logarithmic derivative).

9.2 Iterative Identity Between Consecutive Levels

The general formula may be written in iterative form. For \(n \ge 1\), we have:

\[ \[ D_n y = \frac{H_n(x)}{H_n(y)}\, D_0 y = \frac{H_n(x)}{H_n(y)} \cdot \frac{H_{n-1}(y)}{H_{n-1}(x)} \, D_{n-1} y. \]

This can be rewritten as:

\[ \[ D_n y = \frac{H_n(x) H_{n-1}(y)}{H_n(y) H_{n-1}(x)} \, D_{n-1} y. \] \]

At low levels, this gives:

    * \(D_1 y = \dfrac{H_1(x)}{H_1(y)} D_0 y\), * \(D_2 y = \dfrac{H_2(x) H_1(y)}{H_2(y) H_1(x)} D_1 y\), * \(D_3 y = \dfrac{H_3(x) H_2(y)}{H_3(y) H_2(x)} D_2 y\),

Each derivative level \(D_n\) is thus obtained from \(D_{n-1}\) by re–normalizing with respect to a deeper hierarchical scale.

9.3 Chains of Golden–Type Relations

For the first levels we already know the Golden Relations:

  • \(x D_0(y) = y D_1(y)\),
  • \(x \ln x \, D_0(y) = y \ln y \, D_2(y)\),

more generally:

\[ H_n(x)\, D_0(y) = H_n(y)\, D_n(y). \]

This is just another way of writing the definition of \(D_n\), but it highlights an important symmetry:

The product of a scale factor in \(x\) with \(D_0(y)\) equals the product of the corresponding scale factor in \(y\) with \(D_n(y)\).

For example, for \(n = 1\),

\[ x D_0(y) = y D_1(y), \]

and for \(n = 2\),

\[ x \ln x \, D_0(y) = y \ln y \, D_2(y). \]

Such relations form a chain of identities connecting the base derivative \(D_0\) to all higher levels.

9.4 Ratios Between Different Levels Dn and Dm

An important property of the hierarchy is the ability to compare different levels directly. Using the general definition:

\[ D_n y = \frac{H_n(x)}{H_n(y)} D_0 y, \qquad D_m y = \frac{H_m(x)}{H_m(y)} D_0 y, \]

we obtain:

\[ \frac{D_n y}{D_m y} = \frac{H_n(x) H_m(y)}{H_m(x) H_n(y)}. \]

This ratio does not involve \(D_0\) at all. It expresses a pure hierarchical comparison between levels \(n\) and \(m\) for a given pair \((x, y)\).

For example, for \(n = 2\) and \(m = 1\),

\[ \frac{D_2 y}{D_1 y} = \frac{H_2(x) H_1(y)}{H_1(x) H_2(y)}. \]

This type of expression describes how the logarithmic layer of change compares to the relative layer for the same function.

9.5 Behavior Under Scaling and Power Laws

9.5.1 Power Functions: \(y = x^k\)

Consider again the power function \(y = x^k\), with \(k > 0\). We recall:

\[ D_0 y = k x^{k-1},\quad D_1 y = k,\quad D_2 y = 1. \]

For higher levels, each \(D_n\) for \(n \ge 2\) tends to extremely simple behavior, because the ratios involving \(L_n(x)\) and \(L_n(y)\) often simplify drastically when \(y\) is a pure power of \(x\).

This illustrates a general principle:

Power laws are “natural eigenfunctions” for the hierarchical derivatives.

At low levels, \(D_1\) extracts the exponent \(k\), and \(D_2\) collapses to the constant 1. Higher levels reveal whether there is any additional hierarchical structure beyond a simple power law.

9.5.2 Exponential Functions: \(y = e^{a x}\)

For exponential functions we previously found:

\[ D_0 y = a y,\quad D_1 y = a x,\quad D_2 y = \text{(logarithmic in } x \text{)}. \]

Here the hierarchy unfolds differently:

  • \(D_0\) is proportional to \(y\),
  • \(D_1\) grows like a power of \(x\),
  • \(D_2\) grows like a logarithm of \(x\),
  • \(D_n\) for larger \(n\) captures increasingly slow variations.

This illustrates how the same function can display very different behaviors depending on which derivative level we use.

9.6 Product Rules at Different Levels

9.6.1 Product Rule for D1

We have already shown that for \(y = u v\),

\[ D_1 (u v) = D_1 u + D_1 v. \]

This is a very clean rule: the relative derivative of a product is the sum of the relative derivatives, mirroring the logarithmic identity

\[ \ln(u v) = \ln u + \ln v. \]

9.6.2 Product Behavior for Higher Levels

For higher levels \(D_n\) with \(n \ge 2\), the product rules become more involved, because the normalization factors \(L_n(x)/L_n(y)\) interact with the structure of \(u\) and \(v\). Nevertheless, a general pattern remains:

  • Products at level \(D_n\) often correspond to sums at the level of transformed variables \(L_{n-1}\) or deeper.
  • In many cases, taking logarithms of \(u\) and \(v\) simplifies the analysis of \(D_n(u v)\).

The exact formulas depend on the explicit choice of the ladder \(L_n\), but the qualitative behavior is robust: higher levels treat multiplication as an additive effect in some transformed scale.

9.7 Composition and Change of Variables

Let \(y = f(x)\) and suppose we apply a change of variables \(x = g(t)\). Then \(y\) becomes a function of \(t\), and all derivatives \(D_n\) can be re–expressed in terms of \(t\).

At level \(D_0\) we have the usual chain rule:

\[ \frac{dy}{dt} = \frac{dy}{dx} \cdot \frac{dx}{dt}. \]

For \(D_n\), the transformation introduces the scale factors \(L_n(x)\) and \(L_n(y)\) evaluated now as functions of \(t\). Schematically:

\[ \[ D_n^{(t)} y = \frac{H_n(x(t))}{H_n(y(t))} \frac{dy}{dt}. \]

This means that a change of variables may shift which level in the hierarchy is most natural. Sometimes a complicated behavior in \(x\) becomes simpler in another variable \(t\), or vice versa.

  • Derived iterative identities relating \(D_n\) to \(D_{n-1}\).
  • Presented a chain of Golden–type relations connecting scale factors and derivatives at all levels.
  • Computed ratios \(D_n y / D_m y\) purely in terms of the ladder functions \(L_n\) and \(L_m\).
  • Analyzed the behavior of \(D_n\) for power and exponential functions.
  • Reviewed the clean product rule for \(D_1\) and discussed the more subtle product behavior for higher levels.
  • Outlined how changes of variables interact with the hierarchy.

These identities show that the family \(\{D_n\}\) is not a loose collection of operators, but a tightly connected hierarchy. Each level refines our description of change, and the algebraic structure of the system reflects the layered structure of nature itself.
