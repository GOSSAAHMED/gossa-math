Differential derivative \(D_0\)
$$ D_0 y = \frac{dy}{dx} $$

$$ D_1 y = \frac{dy \cdot x}{dx \cdot y} $$

$$ D_2 y = \frac{dy \cdot x \ln x}{dx \cdot y \ln y} $$

$$ D_n y = \frac{dy \displaystyle\prod_{k=0}^{n-1} L_k(x)} {dx \displaystyle\prod_{k=0}^{n-1} L_k(y)}, \quad L_0(x)=x,\ L_1(x)=\ln x,\ L_2(x)=\ln(\ln x),\dots $$

$$ x \cdot D_0(y) = y \cdot D_1(y) $$

What is Hierarchical Calculus?

Hierarchical Calculus is a mathematical framework that describes change not only through a single linear derivative, but through a hierarchy of derivative layers. It starts from the classical differential derivative \(D_0\), then the relative derivative \(D_1\), the logarithmic derivative \(D_2\), the super-logarithmic derivative \(D_3\), and so on up to \(D_n\).

The key idea is that natural phenomena do not always evolve linearly: many of them are governed by ratios, logarithms and higher-order transformations. Each layer has its own derivative, series, integrals and equations.

Hierarchical derivatives

Hierarchical derivatives are the core of the theory. For each order \(n\) there is a derivative \(D_n\) linking the way the variable \(x\) changes to the way the function \(y\) changes on the same layer.

Layer 0

Differential derivative \(D_0\)

Describes classical linear change:

$$ D_0 y = \frac{dy}{dx} $$

This is the base of classical calculus and is now viewed here as the zero layer of the hierarchy.

Layer 1

Relative derivative \(D_1\)

Links relative rates of change of \(x\) and \(y\):

$$ D_1 y = \frac{dy \cdot x}{dx \cdot y} $$

It naturally appears in systems with relative or exponential growth, such as population dynamics and compound interest models.

Layer 2 and beyond

Logarithmic and higher derivatives

The logarithmic derivative introduces the logarithm in the structure:

$$ D_2 y = \frac{dy \cdot x \ln x}{dx \cdot y \ln y} $$

Higher layers \(D_3, D_4, \dots, D_n\) capture even more complex forms of behaviour and scaling in nature.

Hierarchical series

Just as Taylor series plays a central role in classical calculus, Hierarchical Calculus introduces hierarchical series that rely on ratios and logarithms instead of only linear differences in \(x\).

Classical reference

Taylor series

$$ f(x) = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{D_0^k f(a)}{k!}(x-a)^k $$

This expansion is built on linear increments in \(x\) and works well for functions with simple local behaviour.

Hierarchical example

Relative hierarchical series

A typical example of a series based on ratios:

$$ \ln f(x) = \ln f(a) + D_1 f(a)\, \ln\left(\frac{x}{a}\right) + \cdots $$

Such series fit naturally functions with multiplicative or exponential behaviour and may converge faster than Taylor in these regimes.

Hierarchical integrals

For each derivative layer \(D_n\) there is a corresponding hierarchical integral operator describing the accumulation of phenomena along that layer, not only by simple summation.

Hierarchical integrals lead to new types of equations in physics, biology, medicine and economics where the notion of accumulation itself has a hierarchical structure.

Applications in science

Hierarchical Calculus is designed as a candidate language to describe complex systems and multi-scale phenomena in both natural and human sciences.

Physics & Cosmology

Physics and astrophysics

Hierarchical equations of motion, new views on gravity, and models of cosmic structures across multiple layers \(D_n\), from orbital mechanics to galactic scales.

Biology & Medicine

Biology and medicine

Hierarchical growth models for cells, tissues, organs and whole organisms; relative and logarithmic descriptions of disease dynamics and therapies.

Economy & Complex Systems

Economics and complex systems

Describing markets, networks and societies through layers of relative and logarithmic derivatives instead of a single differential derivative.
