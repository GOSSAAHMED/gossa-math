Hierarchical Calculus
A unified multilayer framework for change

Hierarchical Calculus is a mathematical framework proposed by AHMED GOSSA that extends classical differential calculus from a single level of change to a hierarchy of derivative layers. It provides a unified language to describe physical, biological and economic processes across multiple scales.

What is Hierarchical Calculus?

Classical calculus describes change through a single derivative, usually interpreted as a linear rate of variation in the variable \(x\). Hierarchical Calculus generalizes this view by introducing a sequence of derivative layers \(\{D_0, D_1, D_2, \dots, D_n\}\), each adapted to a particular structural transformation of the variables: linear, relative, logarithmic, and beyond.

The key idea is that many natural and human-made systems do not evolve purely linearly. They are governed by ratios, logarithms and higher-order transformations. Each of these regimes requires its own notion of derivative, series, integral and differential equation. Hierarchical Calculus organises these notions into a coherent multilayer framework.

Hierarchical derivatives

Hierarchical derivatives form the core of the theory. For each order \(n\) there is a derivative \(D_n\) that links the way the independent variable \(x\) changes to the way the dependent variable \(y\) changes on the same structural layer.

Layer 0

Differential derivative \(D_0\)

Measures classical linear change:

$$ D_0 y = \frac{dy}{dx} $$

This is the starting point of standard calculus and is reinterpreted here as the zero level of the hierarchy.

Layer 1

Relative derivative \(D_1\)

Measures change in relative, rather than absolute, terms:

$$ D_1 y = \frac{dy \cdot x}{dx \cdot y} $$

It naturally appears in systems with exponential or proportional growth, such as population dynamics, finance with compound interest, and many feedback-driven processes.

Layers 2 and above

Logarithmic and higher derivatives

The logarithmic derivative incorporates the logarithm into the structure:

$$ D_2 y = \frac{dy \cdot x \ln x}{dx \cdot y \ln y} $$

Higher layers \(D_3, D_4, \dots, D_n\) apply successive transformations such as \(\ln(\ln x)\) and beyond, capturing more intricate scaling behaviours in complex systems.

Hierarchical series

Just as Taylor series play a central role in classical calculus, Hierarchical Calculus introduces series expansions adapted to each structural layer. Instead of relying only on linear increments in \(x\), these series make use of ratios and logarithmic transformations that match the underlying behaviour of the system.

Classical reference

Taylor series

$$ f(x) = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{D_0^k f(a)}{k!}(x-a)^k $$

This expansion is built on linear increments in \(x\) and works well for functions with locally smooth, approximately linear behaviour.

Hierarchical example

Relative hierarchical series

A typical hierarchical expansion based on ratios:

$$ \ln f(x) = \ln f(a) + D_1 f(a)\, \ln\left(\frac{x}{a}\right) + \cdots $$

Such series are more natural for multiplicative and exponential phenomena and may converge more efficiently than Taylor series in these regimes.

Hierarchical integrals

For each derivative layer \(D_n\) there exists a corresponding hierarchical integral operator. These integrals describe accumulation in a way that is consistent with the transformation structure of their layer, rather than through simple summation alone.

Hierarchical integrals lead to new families of differential–integral equations suitable for modelling processes in which growth, dissipation, interaction or transport occur simultaneously across several scales.

Applications in science

Hierarchical Calculus is designed as a candidate language for complex and multiscale systems in both natural and human sciences. By combining linear, relative and logarithmic descriptions of change, it offers tools to model phenomena that are difficult to capture with a single derivative.

Physics & Cosmology

Physics and astrophysics

Hierarchical equations of motion, multi-scale gravitational models and structural descriptions of cosmic systems using higher-order layers \(D_n\), from orbital mechanics to galactic and cosmological scales.

Biology & Medicine

Biology and medicine

Hierarchical growth models for cells, tissues, organs and whole organisms; relative and logarithmic representations of disease progression and therapeutic response across several biological levels.

Economy & Complex Systems

Economics and complex systems

Describing markets, networks and societies through layered derivatives that capture multiplicative effects, feedback loops and nonlinear scaling, instead of relying solely on linear differential models.

Book project: “Hierarchical Calculus”

AHMED GOSSA is preparing a comprehensive monograph of about 250 pages and 30 chapters, provisionally titled “Hierarchical Calculus”. The book will present:

  • All hierarchical derivatives \(D_0, D_1, \dots, D_n\).
  • Hierarchical series, integrals and associated equations.
  • Numerous worked examples in physics, medicine, economics and complex systems.

Once completed, a PDF version of the book will be made available on this website, together with the LaTeX source files for researchers who wish to build upon the theory.
