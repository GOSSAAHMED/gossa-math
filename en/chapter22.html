Chapter 22 – Applications in Biology

Biological systems operate across many scales: molecules, cells, tissues, organisms, populations and ecosystems. Their dynamics often combine fast processes with slow adaptation and ultra–slow evolutionary change. Hierarchical Calculus provides a natural language for describing such multi–scale behaviour, using the derivatives \(D_0\) (absolute), \(D_1\) (relative) and \(D_2\) (logarithmic).

In this chapter we illustrate three classes of applications:

  • population and epidemic dynamics,
  • allometric scaling and growth of organisms,
  • adaptation and ultra–slow changes in biological parameters.

22.1 Population Growth: From Exponential to Relative Dynamics

Let \(N(t)\) denote the size of a biological population at time \(t\). The classical exponential growth model is

\[ D_0 N = r N, \]

with intrinsic growth rate \(r\). The solution is

\[ N(t) = N_0 e^{r(t-t_0)}. \]

This model is appropriate when growth is roughly constant per unit time. However, many populations follow power–law or scale–invariant dynamics, especially when environmental changes also depend on time. For such cases, the level–1 derivative is more natural:

\[ D_1^{(t)} N = \frac{t}{N}\,\frac{dN}{dt}. \]

Example 22.1 – Power-Law Population Growth

Assume the relative derivative is constant:

\[ D_1^{(t)} N(t) = k. \]

Then

\[ \frac{t}{N}\frac{dN}{dt} = k \quad\Rightarrow\quad N(t) = N_0 \left(\frac{t}{t_0}\right)^k. \]

The population follows a power law in time rather than an exponential. This behaviour is often seen in systems with growth limited by scale–dependent resources or slowly expanding habitats.

22.2 Epidemic Growth and Relative Series

In epidemiology, the number of infected individuals \(I(t)\) often grows super–exponentially at early times, then slows down as immunity or interventions appear. Relative derivatives can help disentangle these phases.

Define the relative time derivative of \(I(t)\):

\[ D_1^{(t)} I = \frac{t}{I}\frac{dI}{dt}. \]

A constant value of \(D_1^{(t)} I\) corresponds to a pure power law in time, whereas a decaying \(D_1^{(t)} I\) indicates a slowdown in the epidemic after accounting for scale in time and cases.

Example 22.2 – Analysing an Epidemic Curve

Suppose data for \(I(t)\) over several decades of time shows that \(\ln I\) is approximately linear in \(\ln t\) during an intermediate phase. This implies

\[ I(t) \approx C t^{\alpha}, \]

and therefore

\[ D_1^{(t)} I(t) \approx \alpha. \]

Deviations of \(D_1^{(t)} I\) from a constant value can be interpreted as interventions, behavioural changes or depletion of susceptible individuals. Hierarchical derivatives thus provide a compact view of epidemic phases.

22.3 Allometric Scaling and D₁ with Respect to Size

Allometric laws relate biological quantities to body size. A typical relation has the form

\[ Y(M) = Y_0 M^{\beta}, \]

where \(M\) is body mass and \(Y\) could be metabolic rate, heart rate, lifespan, etc. The level–1 derivative with respect to \(M\) is

\[ D_1^{(M)} Y = \frac{M}{Y}\frac{dY}{dM}. \]

For a pure allometric law, we obtain:

\[ Y(M) = Y_0 M^{\beta} \quad\Rightarrow\quad D_1^{(M)} Y = \beta. \]

Thus \(D_1^{(M)} Y\) directly measures the allometric exponent. This provides a differential definition of allometry which generalizes to cases where \(\beta\) varies slowly with size.

Example 22.3 – Size-Dependent Metabolic Exponent

Suppose empirical data suggest that the metabolic exponent is not perfectly constant, but slowly increases with body mass:

\[ D_1^{(M)} Y(M) = \beta(M). \]

Hierarchical series in \(\ln(M/M_0)\) (Chapter 17) can be used to approximate \(\beta(M)\), and reconstruct \(Y(M)\) using hierarchical integration. This goes beyond fixed–exponent power laws.

22.4 Ultra-Slow Adaptation and D₂

Many biological parameters adapt very slowly: mutation rates, gene expression thresholds, ecosystem carrying capacities, etc. Their dynamics may be visible only over many generations or environmental cycles.

Let \(P(t)\) be such a parameter. The level–2 derivative with respect to time is:

\[ D_2^{(t)} P = \frac{t\ln t}{P\,\ln P}\,\frac{dP}{dt}. \]

A model of the form

\[ D_2^{(t)} P(t) = \varepsilon, \]

with small \(\varepsilon\) describes ultra–slow adaptation where \(\ln(\ln P)\) is nearly linear in \(\ln(\ln t)\).

Example 22.4 – Slow Environmental Adaptation

Consider a population adapting to a gradually changing climate. Let \(P(t)\) represent the mean tolerance to temperature extremes. If experiments or simulations indicate that

\[ \ln(\ln P(t)) \]

is nearly linear in \(\ln(\ln t)\), then a constant \(D_2^{(t)} P\) provides a compact model for long-term adaptation. Classical derivatives would show almost no change, but \(D_2\) reveals structure on logarithmic time scales.

22.5 Hierarchical View of Biological Time Scales

Biological systems exhibit a hierarchy of time scales:

  • fast molecular reactions (microseconds to seconds),
  • cell cycles and organism lifetimes (hours to years),
  • population dynamics (years to centuries),
  • evolutionary change (thousands to millions of years).

Hierarchical Calculus aligns naturally with this structure:

  • \(D_0\) for fast processes at fixed scales,
  • \(D_1\) for scale–invariant growth and decay across generations,
  • \(D_2\) for ultra–slow evolutionary or ecological drift.

Models that combine these derivatives can describe how short-term dynamics and long-term adaptation interact.

22.6 Conceptual Advantages

Using hierarchical derivatives in biology offers several advantages:

  • A clear separation between absolute change, relative change and logarithmic drift.
  • Natural descriptions of power laws and allometric relations.
  • Tools for ultra–slow processes that are almost invisible to classical derivatives.
  • A unified language linking molecular, organism and population scales.

22.7 Summary of Chapter 22

In this chapter we:

  • Applied \(D_1\) to population and epidemic dynamics, showing how power–law behaviour arises naturally.
  • Used relative derivatives with respect to body mass to describe allometric scaling laws.
  • Introduced \(D_2\) as a tool for modelling ultra–slow biological adaptation.
  • Discussed how hierarchical time scales match the hierarchy of biological processes.

These examples demonstrate that Hierarchical Calculus is not limited to abstract mathematics, but provides a flexible framework for modelling complex living systems across scales. In the next chapter we will explore similar ideas in economics, where growth, inequality and market dynamics also span many orders of magnitude.
