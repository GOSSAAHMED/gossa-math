Chapter 23 – Applications in Economics

Economic systems evolve on many time scales: seconds in financial markets, years for business cycles, decades for structural changes and inequality trends. Classical models use ordinary derivatives of output, prices or wealth. Hierarchical Calculus enriches this picture by adding relative and logarithmic derivatives, capturing growth in percentages and ultra–slow structural shifts.

In this chapter we illustrate how the operators \(D_0, D_1, D_2\) can be used in economic modelling.

  • macroeconomic growth and GDP,
  • inflation and price indices,
  • wealth distributions and inequality,
  • ultra–slow structural change.

23.1 Macroeconomic Growth and D₁

Let \(Y(t)\) denote real output (GDP) at time \(t\). The classical continuous growth model is:

\[ D_0 Y = g Y, \]

where \(g\) is the instantaneous growth rate. The solution is:

\[ Y(t) = Y_0 e^{g(t-t_0)}. \]

This treats time linearly. To study growth over many decades or centuries, it is often more natural to consider growth per logarithmic time step, using the level–1 derivative:

\[ D_1^{(t)} Y = \frac{t}{Y}\frac{dY}{dt}. \]

Constant \(D_1^{(t)} Y\) corresponds to a power–law relation between GDP and time.

Example 23.1 – Long-Term GDP as a Power Law

Suppose long historical data suggest that GDP grows approximately like:

\[ Y(t) \approx C t^{\alpha}, \]

for large \(t\). Then:

\[ D_1^{(t)} Y(t) = \alpha, \]

a constant. Modelling long-term growth via

\[ D_1^{(t)} Y = \alpha, \]

emphasises scaling behaviour rather than fixed time scales. Short-term fluctuations can then be treated as deviations from this hierarchical backbone.

23.2 Growth Rates, Percentages and Relative Derivatives

Economists often work with percentage growth rates. For small changes over a period, the percentage change in \(Y\) is approximated by \(\Delta Y / Y\). In continuous time:

\[ \frac{1}{Y}\frac{dY}{dt} \]

is the instantaneous percentage growth rate. Hierarchical Calculus generalizes this by relating percentage changes in different variables via derivatives like \(D_1\) and \(D_2\).

For example, with respect to time:

\[ D_1^{(t)} Y = t \cdot \frac{1}{Y}\frac{dY}{dt} = t \cdot (\text{percentage growth per unit time}). \]

Thus \(D_1^{(t)} Y\) measures how many percent \(Y\) grows per logarithmic step in time.

23.3 Inflation and Price Indices

Let \(P(t)\) denote a price index (e.g. CPI). The instantaneous inflation rate is:

\[ \pi(t) = \frac{1}{P}\frac{dP}{dt}. \]

The level–1 derivative of prices with respect to time is:

\[ D_1^{(t)} P = \frac{t}{P}\frac{dP}{dt} = t\,\pi(t). \]

Over long horizons, one may be interested not only in the value of \(\pi(t)\) but in its slow drift. This can be captured by hierarchical derivatives acting on \(\pi\) itself or even on \(\ln P\).

Example 23.2 – Slow Disinflation

Suppose a country undergoes a long period where inflation gradually declines. Let \(\pi(t)\) be modelled as:

\[ D_1^{(t)} \pi(t) = -\beta, \]

with small \(\beta>0\). This means the inflation rate itself decays roughly like a power law in time. Solving and integrating back to \(P\) yields a hierarchical description of disinflation over many years, rather than a single exponential regime.

23.4 Inequality, Distributions and Scale

Wealth and income distributions often exhibit heavy tails and approximate power laws. Let \(F(w)\) denote the fraction of individuals with wealth greater than \(w\). In many models:

\[ F(w) \propto w^{-\gamma}, \]

at large \(w\). Taking logarithms:

\[ \ln F(w) = -\gamma \ln w + \text{const}. \]

The level–1 derivative with respect to \(w\) is:

\[ D_1^{(w)} F = \frac{w}{F}\frac{dF}{dw}. \]

For a pure power law, one can show:

\[ D_1^{(w)} F(w) = -\gamma. \]

Thus the heavy–tail exponent \(\gamma\) can be interpreted as a hierarchical derivative of the distribution function with respect to wealth scale.

Example 23.3 – Evolving Inequality Exponent

If the tail exponent changes slowly over time, we may write:

\[ D_1^{(w)} F(w,t) = -\gamma(t), \]

where \(\gamma(t)\) is a slowly varying function. A hierarchical series in \(\ln(t/t_0)\) can then approximate \(\gamma(t)\), leading to a dynamical model of inequality where the distribution's tail thickens or thins ultra–slowly.

23.5 Ultra-Slow Structural Change and D₂

Many structural properties of economies (institutions, norms, technology platforms) change so slowly that their evolution is visible only on generational or secular time scales. Hierarchical Calculus can model such processes via \(D_2\).

Let \(S(t)\) be an indicator of structural characteristics (e.g. a measure of financial depth, digitalisation or institutional quality). The level–2 derivative is:

\[ D_2^{(t)} S = \frac{t\ln t}{S\,\ln S}\,\frac{dS}{dt}. \]

A model of the form:

\[ D_2^{(t)} S(t) = \varepsilon, \]

with small \(\varepsilon\), describes ultra–slow, double–logarithmic change in \(S\) over time.

Example 23.4 – Very Long-Run Financial Development

Suppose \(S(t)\) measures the ratio of financial assets to GDP, and empirical data suggest that its growth is extremely slow but persistent over centuries. Then a hierarchical model with small constant

\[ D_2^{(t)} S = \varepsilon \]

can encode this ultra–slow trend in a compact way, separating it from short-term cycles (handled by \(D_0\)) and medium-term growth (captured by \(D_1\)).

23.6 Hierarchical Economic Time Scales

Economic dynamics can be organised hierarchically:

  • Level 0 (D₀): high-frequency markets, quarterly growth, business cycles.
  • Level 1 (D₁): long-run growth in GDP, capital, population; power–law scaling in time.
  • Level 2 (D₂): secular changes in structure, inequality shape, institutions.

A full model can combine these levels into hierarchical differential equations in which different terms dominate at different horizons.

23.7 Conceptual Advantages in Economics

Using hierarchical derivatives in economics offers:

  • A clear separation between cycles, trends and ultra–slow shifts.
  • A natural language for percentage growth and scaling (via \(D_1\)).
  • A framework for modelling structural change that is almost invisible to ordinary derivatives (via \(D_2\)).
  • A unified approach to both aggregate quantities and distributions with power–law tails.

23.8 Summary of Chapter 23

In this chapter we:

  • Applied \(D_1\) to long-term GDP growth and percentage rates.
  • Discussed inflation and disinflation using relative derivatives.
  • Viewed heavy–tailed wealth distributions through the lens of hierarchical derivatives with respect to wealth.
  • Introduced \(D_2\) as a tool for modelling ultra–slow structural trends in economic systems.
  • Organised economic time scales into levels corresponding to \(D_0, D_1, D_2\).

Hierarchical Calculus thus provides a bridge between short-run macroeconomic dynamics and very long-run structural evolution. In the next chapters we will explore related ideas in medicine, probability and information, and finally in models of human understanding and intelligence.
